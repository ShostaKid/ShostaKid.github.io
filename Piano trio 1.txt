<p align="center"><em>Andante amoroso, in un solo movimento; alla fine, in maggiore</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>On the morning of the nineteenth of July, Anna Shcherbakova sat at the kitchen table with her phone propped against the sugar bowl and a decision to make eleven times.</p>

<p>The folder held two hundred photographs. The post would hold eleven. She went through them the way she had once built programs, deliberately, unsentimentally, each element earning its place or losing it, and the summer light came in over her shoulder and lay across the screen, and she did not hurry. Some things she had learned from a decade of judged mornings: you do not rush the run-through. You especially do not rush it on the day of the performance.</p>

<p>He came in with coffee, still sleep-mussed, tall enough that the kiss he set on the top of her head required no bending to speak of, and watched the sorting over her shoulder for a while without comment, because he had learned, early and wisely, which of her silences had their doors ajar.</p>

<p>"Seven million strangers," he said at last, setting the cup by her wrist. "Did you warn anyone first? Before the internet finds out you own a ring."</p>

<p>"Two people." Anna moved a photograph from the maybe pile to the yes pile: the hockey game, his mouth in her hair, the crowd going gold behind them. "The two that matter. Days ago."</p>

<p>"The loud one and the quiet one."</p>

<p>"The loud one," Anna said, "and the quiet one."</p>

<p>She reached for the coffee, and her thumb paused over a photograph of a river.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Petersburg had been holding its long July evening the way only Petersburg holds it, the light refusing to commit to ending, the Neva running wide and gold-skinned past the parapets, and Sasha had spotted her from forty meters despite the sunglasses, because disguises had never once worked on Sasha, and had announced her discovery to most of the embankment.</p>

<p>"Shcherbakova! In my city! Unsupervised!" Two ice-show colleagues turned to look; a tour boat may have altered course. "Who let you out? Does Moscow know? Take those off, you look like a witness in protection."</p>

<p>"I'm undercover."</p>

<p>"You're famous and terrible at this." The hug arrived mid-sentence at its usual velocity, smelling of arena and peach gum, and then Anna was seized by the arm and steered down the embankment, already under interrogation. "How long are you here? Why are you here? You didn't text. You always text. Three words, no punctuation, like a ransom note, but you text."</p>

<p>"I wanted to see your face."</p>

<p>"Suspicious. Deeply suspicious. But acceptable." Sasha squeezed the arm she was holding. "God, it's good to see you. Nobody in this show understands my genius."</p>

<p>"How's the genius's family?" Anna asked. "How's Makar?"</p>

<p>"Enormous. Happy. He's built the little one a slide in the living room, an actual slide, I came home from tour and there was construction, Anya, sawdust, blueprints, my husband on the floor with a level, and the two of them looked up at me like I was the unreasonable one." Sasha's whole face had changed key, the way it always did on this subject, brightness going soft at the edges. "And the boy. He runs now. Everywhere, at everything, full speed, no brakes, doors, furniture, the dog. The doctor says it's normal. I said, doctor, look at his mother, and he stopped charging us for those appointments."</p>

<p>"He has your engine."</p>

<p>"He has my engine and Makar's steering, thank God, or we'd be raising a wrecking ball." She glanced sideways, warm and sly. "He asked about you last month. Anya-Anya. Both syllables twice, very serious. You've been promoted to a double name, it's a great honor, the dog is still just Dog."</p>

<p>"Tell him Anya-Anya is bringing him something from Moscow."</p>

<p>"He will hold you to it, he forgets nothing, he's a little federation official." They walked; the river kept alongside them, patient, enormous, folding the evening light over and over into its current. "And you? You look, " Sasha narrowed her eyes, professional, scanning, "rested. Suspiciously rested. You have a face today, Anya. You've had a face since I spotted you. What's in Petersburg that couldn't take a train to me in October like a normal person?"</p>

<p>At the stretch of embankment where the hotels begin, Anna stopped walking. She put her hands on the parapet. The stone still held the day's warmth.</p>

<p>"Sasha. I'm getting married."</p>

<p>And for the first time in half a life of knowing her, there was no immediate sound.</p>

<p>Anna had braced for the eruption, the shriek, the embankment-wide announcement, the demands for dates and dress cuts. What came instead was quiet, and it lasted, and when she turned her head, Sasha was looking up at the row of hotel windows catching the low sun, lips pressed together, chin very slightly raised, the posture of a girl at the boards waiting for a score she cares too much about.</p>

<p>"Up there," Sasha said finally, and her voice came out at a speed Anna had not known she owned. "The winter of the camps. Which floor were we?"</p>

<p>"Fifth."</p>

<p>"You remember the floor."</p>

<p>"I remember the lamp."</p>

<p>"The trams made it sway." Sasha's eyes stayed on the windows. "You put on that nocturne. The boring one. I told you it was too boring to skate to."</p>

<p>"You danced to it anyway."</p>

<p>"I was humoring you."</p>

<p>"For several minutes," Anna said. "With your eyes closed."</p>

<p>The river folded and unfolded against the granite below them. Somewhere behind, the city went on being loud about nothing.</p>

<p>"We never talked about it," Sasha said. "Not once. Half our lives side by side, Anya, and we never once."</p>

<p>"No."</p>

<p>"Why didn't we?"</p>

<p>Anna considered the question the way she considered everything, fully, without hurry, her eyes on the water. "Because some things keep better unopened," she said. "We both knew where it was. That was enough."</p>

<p>"That was enough," Sasha repeated, and then her breath did something ragged in the middle of it, and Anna turned, and the tears were already coming, streaming, absurd, catastrophic, over a smile that refused to yield a centimeter of ground, the two of them fighting for the same face and both winning. "Don't look at me like that. I'm happy. This is happy. I do this now, I cry at everything, ask my husband."</p>

<p>"Sasha."</p>

<p>"I was afraid for you." It came out fast now, the dam gone. "For years, Anya. Years. You want to know what I did with all that loudness everyone teases me for? I spent half of it being afraid for you. You live with the volume down, you always have, and I used to lie awake thinking, the world is loud and stupid, it only hears people who knock hard, and my Anya will never knock hard, it will walk right past her door and she'll let it, she'll stand behind that door being perfect and quiet and it will just, walk, past."</p>

<p>"I know," Anna said.</p>

<p>"You know? You can't know, I never once said..."</p>

<p>"You called every Sunday for three years. Even from Japan. Even the week your son was born, Sasha, you called me from the hospital corridor." Anna's voice stayed level, but her hands had left the parapet. "Nobody calls that reliably out of friendship alone. That was fear with a phone plan."</p>

<p>Sasha made a sound that was half laugh and half something breaking open, and wiped one cheek with the heel of her hand, uselessly, and gave up, and let the rest come. "And now look. It didn't walk past. It found you anyway. The quiet worked, the whole time it worked, and I spent all those years knocking on the world's door on your behalf for nothing, absolutely nothing, and I have never in my life been so happy to be useless."</p>

<p>"Not for nothing."</p>

<p>"Anya..."</p>

<p>"You were the knock." Anna said it plainly, the way she said scores and verdicts and true things, and watched it land. "All those years. The world walked past my door exactly as often as you let it, which was never. I heard every Sunday, Sasha. I counted on them. I built on them." A breath. "The quiet worked because someone loud stood guard over it."</p>

<p>Sasha stared at her, wet-faced, wrecked, radiant. Then she reached out and took Anna's face in one hand, palm warm against her cheek, thumb just below the cheekbone, the way you steady something precious you have no intention of letting go of.</p>

<p>"Be happy, Anneska," she said. "Truly. Completely. That's the only thing you've ever owed me."</p>

<p>And Anna, who had spent a career keeping her hands folded while the world had feelings at her, lifted both of hers and pressed them over Sasha's hand, and held it there against her cheek, and turned her face into the warm palm with her eyes closed, one slow breath, lips just brushing the base of the thumb, a payment half a lifetime overdue and delivered in her own currency, small, exact, unwitnessed.</p>

<p>"Paid in full," she said quietly. "Both of us."</p>

<p>They stayed like that a moment longer than either of them would ever report. Then Sasha sniffed hugely, laughed at her own wreckage, and held out an imperious wet hand. "Now show me the ring before I die."</p>

<p>The ring was shown. The verdict was loud, and favorable, and audible from the water.</p>

<p>For a moment, the city fell away behind them. For a moment, the long gold evening held nothing but the parapet, and the laughter, and the drying tears. For a moment, their world held only each other, the way it had once before, five floors up, to a quiet song neither of them ever explained.</p>

<p>And below, unhurried, the Neva gathered it all from its banks the way she has gathered things for three hundred years, the swaying lamps, the unfinished dances, two women laughing through tears in the last of the light, and folded it into her long gold current, murmuring, and carried it gently toward the sea.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The rink, three days later, at six in the morning, belonged to no one, which was the building's polite way of saying it belonged to whoever wanted it badly enough to arrive before the lights did.</p>

<p>Anna came up the back stairs unannounced, the way she had come up ten thousand mornings of her old life, and heard the ice before she saw it: strokes, patient and even, laid down in the half-dark where only one bank of lights had been switched on. She stopped in the tunnel mouth, in the blue shadow, at the boards where a certain junior had once stood morning after morning believing herself unobserved, and she watched.</p>

<p>Kamila was warming up. Not with her own pattern, the sensible assigned one; with another line entirely, older, patient, mannerly in its curves, a phrase from a junior program filmed sideways half her life ago, and she skated it the way she had always skated it, every left made right, every curve opened where the original closed, half a beat behind a partner who was not there, on the wrong foot, faithful as tide.</p>

<p>Anna let her finish the phrase. Then she said, into the cold bright air:</p>

<p>"Still on the wrong foot."</p>

<p>The edge wobbled, one bad centimeter, years of composure hiccupping at once. Kamila caught herself, spun, stared across the ice at the dark half, at the figure standing exactly where she herself used to stand, and then she was moving, no decision visible anywhere in it, crossing the rink at competition speed, and she did not slow at the boards so much as arrive against them, and the hug happened over the barrier before a single word did, gloves and all, cold cheek pressed hard against warm one, ice-air and morning and the whole of her.</p>

<p>"You're here," she said into Anna's shoulder. "You're actually here. Do you know how long it's been? Don't answer. I know exactly how long it's been. Two hundred and six days. Don't ask me why I know that."</p>

<p>"I brought coffee," Anna said, holding up the paper bag in her free hand, "for two hundred and six days."</p>

<p>"You brought coffee to my ice at six in the morning." Kamila pulled back to look at her, hands still fisted in Anna's coat, inspecting her with the thoroughness of a border official, face, shoulders, the whole of her. "You look rested. You look, " she frowned, recalibrating, "you look happy, actually. Moscow's been feeding you. Good. Someone has to."</p>

<p>"And you look fast. I watched the last phrase. The new free program?"</p>

<p>"The new free program," Kamila groaned, releasing her at last to lean on the boards. "Which I love, and which is trying to kill me. The second half is all transitions, uphill, in traffic. My coach says it builds character. I said I have character. She said, build more."</p>

<p>"And the knee?"</p>

<p>"Behaving. Mostly. It files complaints on cold mornings and I ignore them, we have a system." She tipped her head at the ice, at the dark, at the hour. "Why are you here, Anna? Not that you need a reason, you never need a reason, but it's six in the morning and you're standing in my tunnel with coffee, and the last time you surprised me at a rink I was fourteen and you'd brought my forgotten skate guards from the dorm, so my heart is currently making up stories and I'd like the real one."</p>

<p>Anna looked at her for a moment. Then she set the coffee on the boards between them, carefully, the way you clear a table before laying something important on it.</p>

<p>"I'm getting married."</p>

<p>Kamila went very still, one heartbeat of it, the old trained stillness arriving out of habit, and then it simply failed, outvoted, and she seized both of Anna's hands over the boards, gloves around bare fingers, gripping hard.</p>

<p>"Say it again."</p>

<p>"I'm getting married."</p>

<p>"Slower. No. Exactly the same."</p>

<p>"You'll wear it out."</p>

<p>"It's mine now too. I'll do what I like with it." And the second hug came then, fiercer than the first, longer, Kamila's arms wrapped whole around her over the barrier with the boards digging into both of them and neither of them adjusting, and when she finally spoke again it was muffled and it was laughing and it might have been slightly wet at the edges: "Finally. Finally, finally. When?"</p>

<p>"June. Small. You're coming."</p>

<p>"Is that an invitation?"</p>

<p>"It's a seating chart. You're already on it."</p>

<p>They sat on the bench by the gate after that, in the blue half, the way the morning shift of two had always sat, the coffee steaming between them, and the rink ticked with cold around them, and Kamila unlaced nothing and went nowhere, practice forgotten on the ice behind her.</p>

<p>"Is he good to you?" she asked, in the tone of a border official resuming a serious inspection.</p>

<p>"Yes."</p>

<p>"Is he kind when no one's watching? That's the only kind that counts."</p>

<p>"He is."</p>

<p>"Then I have very few notes." A pause; the inspection reached its most important checkpoint. "Does he know how you take your milk?"</p>

<p>"He makes tea."</p>

<p>A silence of genuine horror. "Tea."</p>

<p>"He's learning."</p>

<p>"Warm milk. Two spoonfuls."</p>

<p>"Never three," Anna said. "I remember. You counted for me for two years, Kamila. I can count."</p>

<p>The words settled between them on the cold bench, and Kamila looked at her hands, and the question that had waited years finally found a morning quiet enough to be asked in.</p>

<p>"You knew it was me. The milk."</p>

<p>"Since the third night."</p>

<p>"The third." Her voice went somewhere thin. "You never said. Two years, Anna. You never said one word."</p>

<p>"You weren't ready." Anna said it gently, without apology, a fact set down like a cup. "And I didn't want you to stop."</p>

<p>Kamila absorbed that with her eyes on the ice, on the mirrored line still faintly written out there in the fresh surface, left-handed, wrong-footed, hers. Anna watched her absorb it, and then asked, quietly, the question she had carried up the back stairs along with the coffee:</p>

<p>"And you? Who makes your milk now?"</p>

<p>Kamila was silent a moment. "The kettle and I manage," she said. "Two spoonfuls. I make it properly. Someone taught me the margin." She looked up with half a smile that did not quite cover what it was assigned to cover. "It keeps, Anna. That kind of thing keeps."</p>

<p>Then she stood, went to her bag on the bench, and came back with something small held in both hands.</p>

<p>A glass bottle. Little, round-shouldered, the kind sold at farm stalls, honey holding the rink's thin light inside it like a lamp turned low. The label had been gone for years. The bottle had not.</p>

<p>"I still carry it," she said, and her voice had gone down into its lowest register, even and quiet, the register that had always held more than its words. "After you left the ice, I kept buying the same kind. Carrying it. There was never anyone to make the milk for. I did it anyway."</p>

<p>"Kamila."</p>

<p>"Take it. For the mornings he doesn't know how to fix yet. Two spoonfuls, tell him, never more. He'll learn the difference between not enough and too sweet." She pressed the bottle into Anna's palm and folded Anna's fingers over it with both of her hands, the old both-hands hold, the one that had once warmed cold fingers and steadied ceramic cups, and kept them folded there one beat longer than the errand required, and one more after that.</p>

<p>Anna looked at their stacked hands. She did not pull away, and she did not stand to go, and the morning held very still around the bench.</p>

<p>"Anna." Kamila's thumb moved once, barely, across her knuckles. "There was a winter when I..."</p>

<p>She stopped. The sentence stood there with its coat half buttoned, years old, and looked at both of them.</p>

<p>"I know," Anna said.</p>

<p>"You always knew."</p>

<p>"Everything you ever left at my door," Anna said, "I kept. The milk. The mornings. The winter you couldn't finish just now. All of it, Kamila. Kept, and counted, and none of it wasted, and none of it ever once unwelcome." She turned her hand inside the fold until their palms met around the small warm weight of the bottle. "A person can be loved carefully for years and never find the day to say thank you. This is the day. Thank you."</p>

<p>And she leaned across the narrow cold space of the bench and kissed Kamila's cheek, once, unhurried, the first she had ever given her, on skin still cold from the ice, and drew back just far enough to see it land.</p>

<p>Kamila sat extraordinarily still, in the stillness of a girl working very hard at it, the pink arriving from somewhere and climbing to the tips of her ears with no regard at all for years of discipline.</p>

<p>"Go," she managed, finally, with great dignity. "Some of us have practice."</p>

<p>Anna went. At the tunnel mouth she turned, the honey warming in her closed hand, and watched Kamila step back through the gate and take up the old mirrored line again from the top, half a beat behind nobody, on the wrong foot, exactly as before, and midway down the long side, without breaking stroke, one gloved hand rose from its careful carriage and touched, briefly, the cold curve of her own cheek, and returned to its place, and the line went on.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Eleven photographs. She moved the last one into place, the hockey game, the gold crowd, his mouth in her hair, and typed the caption, which took her four seconds, because she had known it for weeks: the date, a heart, a ring. Ten characters and two small pictures. The rest of the world could bring its own words; she had always trusted readers.</p>

<p>Her thumb settled over the button, and pressed.</p>

<p>Arms came around her from behind before the screen had finished changing, and his chin found the top of her head, and they stood like that in the kitchen light while somewhere out in the morning the news began to travel at the speed news travels.</p>

<p>"So the loud one cried on a river," he murmured into her hair, "and the quiet one gave you honey."</p>

<p>"That's them."</p>

<p>"And the honey lives in the cupboard now, and I'm under instructions about spoonfuls."</p>

<p>"Two." Anna leaned back into the warmth of him and closed her eyes. "Never more. You'll learn." She turned the phone face-down on the table, done with it, and felt him laugh, low, through her spine. "They were my youth, those two. The whole spring of me. Everything I am was rehearsed on them first."</p>

<p>He turned her gently by the shoulders then, and of the day's twelve photographs, the twelfth was never taken; it happened slowly, in the long gold of the kitchen morning, unposted, uncaptioned, filed where she kept the things that were hers alone, and it lasted the way held things last, and when it ended she was smiling against him and the phone on the table had already begun, very quietly, to sing.</p>

<p>She let it sing.</p>
