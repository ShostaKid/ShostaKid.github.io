<p align="center"><em>Lento con gran espressione</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The first time Anna noticed Sasha's hands was not during a competition, not during practice, and not even on the ice.</p>

<p>It was during a warm-up session in the gymnasium at Khrustalny, in the dead of a Moscow winter, when all the girls from Tutberidze's group had gathered in the small room that smelled of old rubber mats and wood polish. Anna was stretching by the barre, her body folding into the familiar ache of an arabesque, when she caught sight of the blonde girl across the room wrapping her own fingers in athletic tape. Sasha did it carelessly, the way she did most things at the time, tearing the tape with her teeth and winding it around her knuckles with quick, impatient motions that had no concern for symmetry or neatness. There was nothing elegant about it, nothing remarkable at all, and yet Anna found herself watching long past the point of coincidence, tracking the way those small, roughened fingers moved with a confidence that bordered on recklessness.</p>

<p>Anna did not understand why she was staring. She told herself it was because of the tape, that she was simply observing a fellow skater's warm-up habits, the way any serious athlete might study the routines of those around her. But the truth was something quieter and more frightening than that, something she would not recognize for another two years, because it had no name yet and because naming it would have changed everything.</p>

<p>She looked away before Sasha could notice, and the moment passed like a half-note played too softly for anyone else to hear.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>They were not friends in the beginning. They were too different for that, and the structure of competitive figure skating did not encourage closeness between girls who would inevitably stand on the same podium and receive different metals. Anna was the quiet one, the girl who chose Tchaikovsky and Rachmaninoff while others reached for pop anthems, the one whose coaches called "the thinker" because she would spend long minutes at the edge of the rink simply watching the ice before stepping onto it, as though she needed to understand the surface before trusting it with her blades. Sasha was the opposite in nearly every way: loud where Anna was silent, explosive where Anna was measured, the kind of athlete who attacked a quadruple jump the way a sparrow launches itself from a branch, with absolute faith that the air would hold her even when every law of physics argued otherwise.</p>

<p>They orbited each other at Khrustalny like two celestial bodies caught in the gravity of the same rink, close enough to feel each other's presence at all times but never quite colliding. Anna would arrive early and leave late, her programs rehearsed with a meticulousness that left nothing to chance. Sasha would burst through the doors mid-morning with her hair half-tied and her laces already loosened from the walk over, and somehow produce a quad toe loop on her second attempt as though she had been born rotating. The other girls whispered that it was unfair, that no one should be able to jump like that without the discipline Anna showed, but Anna herself never said a word about it, because she had already begun to understand something that the others had not.</p>

<p>Sasha was not careless. She was fearless. And there was a difference between the two that was as vast as the distance between the earth and the furthest star in Moscow's winter sky.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The shift happened during a training camp in St. Petersburg, in the kind of small hotel room that paired skaters together without asking whether they wanted to share space with their fiercest competitor. Anna had prepared herself for an uncomfortable week of polite avoidance, of sleeping on her designated side of the room and keeping her headphones in as a wall against conversation. But Sasha, who had never met a boundary she did not instinctively want to test, had dismantled that wall within the first forty minutes.</p>

<p>"You're special, you know."</p>

<p>Anna remembered looking up from her book at the blonde girl sprawled lazily across the opposite bed, watching her with those sky-blue eyes that always seemed to be searching for something just beyond the edges of the visible world. The statement had no context, no preamble, and Anna's ability to anticipate what people would say, the "future-reading" skill she had cultivated through years of reading judges' faces and competitors' body language, had failed her completely.</p>

<p>"What do you mean?"</p>

<p>"Your taste in music," Sasha had said, as if that explained everything. "You always choose something gentle or overly classical for your programs. I could never do that. For me, skating needs dopamine, and dopamine only comes when the music is powerful enough to shake the walls. How do you even do it?"</p>

<p>Anna did not answer the question, because the honest answer would have been something she could not yet articulate, something about the way a slow melody forces the body to find its own rhythm rather than borrowing one, the way a pianissimo passage demands more courage from a skater than any thundering crescendo because there is nowhere to hide when the music is quiet. Instead, she had smiled and teased Sasha about her sudden interest, and Sasha had teased her back, and somehow the evening had carried them to a moment that Anna would replay in her mind more often than any competition program she had ever performed.</p>

<p>She had asked Sasha to dance.</p>

<p>Not on ice, not in skates, but right there in the small hotel room with its single swaying ceiling lamp and the sounds of the ancient city on the Neva drifting through the window. Anna had connected her phone to the speaker and chosen a piece she had been listening to for weeks, a nocturne built on a soft piano and a violin that stretched its notes like a long, aching breath. She had extended her hand and waited, and the seconds between the offer and Sasha's acceptance had felt longer than the free skate at any Grand Prix Final.</p>

<p>But Sasha had stood up, and Sasha had crossed the room, and Sasha had placed her small, tape-scarred hand into Anna's palm.</p>

<p>They danced without choreography and without rules, letting the violin guide their steps the way the ice guided their blades. Forward, then back. A small drift to the right, then to the left as the bow changed direction. Anna did not lead and Sasha did not follow; they moved together the way two notes played simultaneously become a chord, each one distinct but impossible to separate once they have sounded. Somewhere between the second passage and the third, Sasha's fingers had woven themselves through Anna's, and neither of them acknowledged it, because acknowledging it would have meant giving the moment a weight that might have broken its fragile, trembling beauty.</p>

<p>Anna remembered the exact point when Sasha pulled her closer, close enough that Anna could feel the blonde girl's pulse through their interlaced hands, close enough to catch the faint scent of cheap hotel shampoo in golden hair. She remembered Sasha's breath slowing, her rigid, athletic frame softening for perhaps the first time since Anna had known her, as though the music had found the single key that unlocked every tension the Quad Queen had ever held. She remembered the violin drawing its final stroke, merging with a high note from the piano, and the way their breathing met in the silence that followed.</p>

<p>Anna remembered all of it, because it was the night she understood what the unnamed thing was, the quiet, terrifying knowledge she had carried since the gymnasium, since the moment she watched a reckless girl wrap tape around her knuckles and could not look away.</p>

<p>She was in love with Alexandra Trusova.</p>

<p>And she had understood, with the certainty of someone who reads music the way others read weather, that this love would bring her no peace at all.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>What followed was a long and private season of small, accumulated sufferings that Anna bore with the same composure she brought to the ice. Loving Sasha was not a single wound but a slow accumulation of paper cuts, each one too minor to justify complaint but together forming a constellation of pain that mapped itself across every corner of her daily life.</p>

<p>It was the way Sasha would drape an arm over Anna's shoulders after practice, casual and proprietary, and Anna would feel every point of contact burning through the thin fabric of her jacket as though Sasha's skin were made of something hotter than human warmth. It was the late-night phone calls that lasted hours, during which Sasha would talk about everything and nothing, her voice dropping into a low, sleepy register that made Anna press the phone harder against her ear, as though she could absorb the vibration into her own body and carry it through the night. It was the competitions where they stood beside each other on the podium, medals cold against their chests, and Anna would look at the girl beside her and think: <em>I would trade every gold for one honest word from you.</em></p>

<p>But she never said it. She never said any of it, because Anna Shcherbakova was a girl who understood tempo, and the tempo of this particular piece was one that could not be rushed without destroying the melody entirely. Some feelings exist only in pianissimo. Some confessions can only be whispered to the ceiling of a dark room when the person you love is asleep in the next bed and will never hear you.</p>

<p>And so Anna held the secret inside her chest the way a pianist holds a sustained pedal, letting the notes ring and ring and ring until the vibration becomes indistinguishable from silence.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The Beijing Olympics changed everything, and changed nothing.</p>

<p>Anna won the gold, and Sasha won the silver, and neither medal contained the thing they actually needed. Anna remembered standing on the highest step of the podium and feeling the anthem move through the arena like a tide, and knowing that she should be experiencing the culmination of every sacrifice she had ever made, every broken bone and early morning and aching muscle, and instead feeling a hollow ache in her chest because the person she wanted to share the moment with was standing one step below her, blue eyes glassy and jaw clenched against a tremor that had nothing to do with the cold.</p>

<p>Later, in the hallway behind the venue where the cameras could not reach, Anna had found Sasha sitting on the floor with her back against the wall, her costume still damp with sweat, the silver medal resting on her lap like something she did not know what to do with. The corridor was empty and fluorescent-lit and smelled of concrete, the least romantic setting imaginable, and Anna had sat down beside her without a word, close enough that their shoulders touched.</p>

<p>They stayed like that for a long time, silent, listening to the muffled sounds of celebration filtering through the walls. At some point Sasha's head had tilted until it rested against Anna's shoulder, and Anna had felt the weight of it, the warm, exhausted heaviness of it, and she had thought: <em>I would carry you, if you let me. I would carry this sadness and every sadness that comes after it, if only you would let me.</em></p>

<p>"Thank you," Sasha had whispered, so quietly that Anna barely caught it.</p>

<p>"For what?"</p>

<p>"For sitting here. For not saying anything."</p>

<p>Anna had swallowed, and pressed her cheek against the top of Sasha's golden hair, and closed her eyes. In the fluorescent stillness of that corridor, surrounded by the ghosts of their greatest achievement and their most private grief, she had allowed herself, for just one breath, to pretend that this was enough. That this proximity, this borrowed warmth, this silence that said more than any words ever could, was a form of love she could survive on.</p>

<p>But it was not enough. It was never going to be enough.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The months that followed were the cruelest ones Anna had known, because they were the months in which Sasha began to drift away. Not suddenly, not with any dramatic severance, but with the slow, inevitable pull of a current that carries someone toward a shore you cannot reach. There were fewer late-night calls, fewer arms draped casually over shoulders, fewer moments of shared silence that had once made Anna feel less alone in the world. Sasha started spending more time with Mark, and then later with Makar, and the spaces she left behind in Anna's life filled up with nothing at all.</p>

<p>Anna watched it happen the way a musician watches the final movement of a concert approach, knowing that the piece must end, that every beautiful thing carries within it the exact measure of its own conclusion. She did not cry, because Anna Shcherbakova was not a person who cried easily, and because crying would have meant admitting that she had lost something she never truly possessed. Instead, she skated. She poured every ounce of the love she could not speak into programs that grew more heartbreaking with each season, choosing music that wept in the places where her voice could not, letting the ice receive the confessions her lips refused to make.</p>

<p>The judges called it artistry. The commentators called it maturity. Only Anna knew that what they were watching was a girl slowly learning to live with a wound that would never fully close, a girl pressing her blade into the ice and carving, over and over again, the shape of a feeling that had no other place to go.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>And now, in the grey light of a Moscow dawn, with the wedding reminder glowing on her phone and the remnants of a beautiful, hateful dream still clinging to her skin like the ghost of a hand she once held, Anna Shcherbakova sat on the edge of her bed and breathed.</p>

<p>In a few hours, she would dress herself in something elegant, something befitting the Olympic champion and devoted best friend everyone expected her to be. She would arrive at the venue with a smile that no one would think to question. She would sit in the front row and watch Sasha walk toward a man who loved her openly, loudly, in all the ways Anna had never dared, and she would clap and she would smile and she would say all the right things at the right moments, because Anna Shcherbakova had always understood tempo better than anyone.</p>

<p>She would do all of this. She would carry this love the way she had always carried it, silently, invisibly, pressed beneath the ribs where no one could see.</p>

<p>But for now, in this one small moment before the day began, she allowed herself to sit in the darkness and let the tears fall, just once, without music, without performance, without any audience at all. They traced warm paths down her cheeks and dropped onto the back of her hand, onto the same palm that had once held Sasha's fingers in a hotel room in St. Petersburg, and each tear felt like a quarter note in the slowest, most unbearable nocturne Anna had ever played.</p>

<p>Outside, Moscow was beginning to stir. The first light of morning crept across the rooftops, pale and indifferent, as it always was. Somewhere in this city, Sasha was waking up beside the man she had chosen, probably laughing at something he said, probably nervous in the way that made her talk too fast and fidget with her hair.</p>

<p>And Anna loved her still. Quietly, impossibly, in the only tempo she had ever known.</p>

<p><em>Lento. Con gran espressione.</em></p>

<p><em>And without end.</em></p>
