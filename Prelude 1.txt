<p align="center"><b>Prelude</b></p>
 
<p align="center"><em>Andante amoroso e dolce</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>
 
<p>"Have you ever tried writing something before?"</p>
 
<p>Sasha lifted her head from the sketchbook propped against her knees and looked toward the smaller girl curled up on the other end of the couch. Anna was watching her with that particular expression she wore when she had been observing someone for longer than was strictly casual, her hazel eyes half-lidded and faintly amused, a warm cup of tea cradled between both palms as though she were holding a small, fragile animal.</p>
 
<p>"What do you mean?"</p>
 
<p>"I mean exactly what I said."</p>
 
<p>"But I do write to you. All those messages I send you at three in the morning when I can't sleep definitely count as literature."</p>
 
<p>"Incoherent voice notes about your cat are a completely different category, Sasha. What I'm talking about is stories. Real ones, with sentences and structure. I'd bet anything they'd be amazing, especially coming from someone who sees the world the way you do."</p>
 
<p>Sasha gazed at Anna for a moment with that look of bewildered amusement she always wore when the Olympic champion said something she had not anticipated, which was often, because Anna Shcherbakova possessed the quiet, devastating ability to say things that landed in Sasha's chest before her brain had time to build a defense against them. Then the blonde girl let out a short laugh and bent back down to her sketchbook, her pencil resuming its quick, restless strokes across the page.</p>
 
<p>"My words aren't built for that, Anneshka. I'm glad you like my texts, but they weren't born to carry the kind of beautiful, poetic thoughts you have. I'm the one who jumps quads. You're the one who makes people cry with a single arm movement set to Tchaikovsky. We have our roles."</p>
 
<p>"Why not?" Anna set down her tea and shifted closer along the couch, reaching out to rest her hand over Sasha's, the one still gripping the pencil. Her fingers were small and calloused from years of gripping the boards during practice, and they closed around Sasha's knuckles with a gentleness that was more persuasive than any argument. "You can't be sure of anything if you haven't even tried, can you? Besides, stories on paper aren't always poetic. And what I'm looking for is exactly that raw, honest energy in the way you talk, that bluntness you have where every word hits like a jump landed clean." Anna smiled, the kind of smile that made her eyes narrow into crescents and sent Sasha's pulse into a rhythm that no coach had ever choreographed. "Isn't that also exactly the way you love me?"</p>
 
<p>Sasha silently watched the girl she loved for a long while, the way she always did when Anna dismantled her defenses so effortlessly that resistance felt not just futile but foolish. She studied Anna's neatly parted dark hair, the hazel eyes bright with a quiet excitement that made them look almost golden in the lamplight, and the way Anna's small body was folded into one of Sasha's oversized hoodies, the sleeves hanging past her fingertips, the hem reaching her thighs, making her look even smaller than she already was, like something precious that the world had wrapped in too much fabric for safekeeping.</p>
 
<p>A faint whistling sound came from the window. The wind was blowing outside, pressing against the frosted panes of their Moscow apartment, producing low, murmuring tones and carrying the bitter chill of the far north through every gap in the old building's frame. The birds had long since disappeared at the beginning of winter, seeking shelter somewhere warmer and wiser than this city. The space around them now held only the soft groan of wind against glass, the hum of the heater in the corner, and the slow, steady heartbeat of the girl pressed close against Sasha's arm. It would not be long before the snow fell, and outside everything would turn pure white, the kind of white that reminded Sasha of competition ice before the first skater's blade touched it.</p>
 
<p>Winter had truly arrived.</p>
 
<p>"You're a stubborn girl, Shcherbakova," Sasha let out a soft sigh, but she still closed the sketchbook and pulled a small notebook from the coffee table, the one Anna used to jot down music ideas for her programs.</p>
 
<p>"A stubborn little champion for an equally stubborn Quad Queen," Anna giggled, and the sound of it somehow made the dreariness of the winter night feel lighter, the way a single held note can change the entire colour of a passage. She shifted closer until her shoulder was tucked beneath Sasha's arm, letting herself feel the warmth radiating from the taller girl's body. "Alright then, let's see what these 'blunt' words of yours are capable of."</p>
 
<p>"…I want to write about winter," Sasha said after a moment of thought, uncapping a pen that had been lying forgotten on the table for weeks.</p>
 
<p>"Sounds fitting for you, oh blunt Quad Queen. Hmm, let me think… something powerful, maybe, to match you…"</p>
 
<p>"I want to write about something romantic." Sasha's fingers began to move across the paper, the pen pressing harder than a pencil would, leaving dark, decisive lines that looked more like the beginning of a declaration than the beginning of a story.</p>
 
<p>"Romantic?" Anna smiled, tilting her head against Sasha's shoulder to look up at her. "You surprise me, Trusova. Just a few minutes ago you said you couldn't write anything poetic like me. Or did you secretly learn time travel at Khrustalny and go back to change your words without telling me?"</p>
 
<p>"I want… to be like you." Ignoring the teasing, Sasha continued letting the pen move across the lined page, her handwriting messy and forward-leaning, the letters cramped with the same impatient energy that drove her across the ice. "I want to write about… the taste of winter."</p>
 
<p>"The taste of winter? Like the taste of snow? Don't tell me you're actually planning to go outside and eat snow off the balcony railing again. I have pictures from last time, and I will use them."</p>
 
<p>"The taste of winter… will be very sweet."</p>
 
<p>"Sweet? But—"</p>
 
<p>Sasha never gave her the chance to finish the sentence.</p>
 
<p>Anna's small, soft lips had already been claimed at some point, so gently that it took her a full breath to understand what was happening, and by then Sasha's hand had found the curve of her jaw, tilting her face upward, and the pen lay forgotten on the open notebook, and the winter outside had gone very, very quiet.</p>
 
<p>"Indeed… winter is really sweet."</p>
