<p align="center"><em>Andante, una corda</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The hospital released her on a Thursday morning, with a folder of documents in careful German and a list of instructions she did not intend to follow, and Anna refused the taxi that Lena had offered three times to call, because the apartment was twenty minutes away on foot and walking, at least, was still something she knew how to do.</p>

<p>Her boots found the rhythm without being asked. Left, right, left, along the wet cobblestones, the old count settling into her body the way it always did. Anna had been walking to this count her entire life. It was the first thing anyone had ever taught her, before edges, before jumps, before the alphabet: how to fall down and get up on her own feet, without waiting, without looking around, without reaching for a hand that might not be there. Get up alone, the voices of her childhood said, one coach after another, one winter after another. If you learn to wait for someone, you will wait your whole life.</p>

<p>A wind came down the narrow street to meet her, carrying on its back the whole scent of a life being lived: cinnamon and clove from the market square, woodsmoke, orange peel, the warm animal sweetness of bread, all of it gathered up from the old town's morning and delivered, door to door, like mail. The bakery on the corner had propped its door open and the heat rolled out in golden waves, and every person who passed through those waves slowed without meaning to, heads turning toward the trays inside the way plants turn toward light. In the square, wooden stalls had assembled themselves overnight under strings of yellow bulbs, a low constellation brought down and hung on wires, and steam rose from the Glühwein vats and the chestnut pans and a wheel of raclette held to its flame, climbing into the gray morning with everything it owned.</p>

<p>The wind reached her, and went through her, and came out the other side carrying exactly what it had brought. Cold air going in. Warm air going out. Nothing declared at the border.</p>

<p>She had crossed a continent for this. That was the joke the square was telling, gently, to no one: in September she had stood on the bridge with her suitcase and promised herself a bigger life, a life she would taste with her own mouth, earned by her own feet, owed to no one. And here it was. The bigger life, ladled out steaming in every direction, generous past all deserving, and it passed through her the way light passes through glass, touching nothing, leaving no color behind.</p>

<p>By one of the stalls, a woman in a red scarf lifted a paper cup of mulled wine to her lips, closed her eyes, and hummed. A small sound, low in the throat, barely a breath, gone in a second, and the woman herself did not notice she had made it, because no one who makes that sound ever notices. A child ran past with sugar on his fingers, licking each one with the seriousness of accounting. Two students shared a rösti out of a folded box, feeding each other the crisp edges, laughing when the cheese pulled into strings between them.</p>

<p>Anna stood in the middle of the square and stared at a stranger in a red scarf the way you stare at someone wearing your stolen coat. That sound had lived in her own throat once. She had not even known it was hers until it was gone, until the doctors gave its absence a Latin name, and now here it was in the December air, casual, unearned, spent by a woman on a two-franc cup of wine, and the whole square was full of it, ordinary and constant as weather, the flavor of a life brimming over in every direction but inward.</p>

<p>Left, right, left. She walked on, and the low sun came out over the rooftops as she went, pouring its last gold across the old town, generous the way December light only knows how to be an hour before dying. It laid itself over every window, every spire, every stranger's shoulder, warm and indiscriminate, and it found nothing in her to hold on to. The lake surfaced between two buildings, wearing its share of the light easily, the way it had worn September. Ahead of her, the long blue shadow of the Rathaus reached all the way across the street, and her own shadow went before her into it, small and quick on the cobblestones, and the great shadow took it mid-stride, the whole of it, and did not give it back.</p>

<p>So this was what standing on your own feet came to, in the end. Falling, and not one hand in the entire gold city reaching out to close.</p>

<p>She had understood it fully for the first time in the hospital, lying under fluorescent lights with a needle in her arm and no one's name on the emergency contact form. The arithmetic of her whole upbringing had finally shown her its other side, the side the coaches never mentioned: a person who needs no one cannot be caught. She had spent twenty-two years perfecting the fall taken alone, and Zurich had simply given her a longer drop than any ice ever had, three months of it, slow enough to watch, and at the bottom there had been no boards, no coach, no blue eyes finding hers across a rink. There had been a bathroom floor, and a roommate's scream, and the ceiling of an ambulance.</p>

<p>She thought: I did the wrong thing.</p>

<p>She let herself think it fully, without softening it. She should have done the opposite of everything. She should have held on. When the argument came she should have crossed the kitchen instead of the border, should have put her stubbornness into staying instead of leaving, should have understood that Sasha had been offering, for two entire years, to be close enough to catch her. The message rose in her again, the one from September, the one she knew by heart because deleting a thing does not unwrite it. I made it. The lake is beautiful. I wish you were. Four words she had cut before they could finish themselves.</p>

<p>And then, before the thought could keep its warmth, the other voice arrived, the one that had been living in her all autumn, the one that sounded reasonable because it always used her own logic against her.</p>

<p>Hold on to Sasha. And offer her what, exactly? What would Anna be, beside her, now?</p>

<p>The needle site in the crook of her arm stung as her sleeve pulled, a small hot point flaring with the swing of her step, the hospital's signature not yet dry on her.</p>

<p>Ballast, the voice answered. A patient to be managed. An anchor dressed as a girlfriend, dragging at her with every gray day, teaching her the weight of things. Sasha was speed. Sasha was appetite, forward motion, the loudest laugh in every room, a person built entirely out of momentum, and love would not change the physics: one of them rising, one of them sinking, both of them holding on. No. Letting her go had been correct after all; the reasons had simply arrived late. The kindest thing a sinking person can do for a swimmer is unlace their fingers. So it was right, the voice concluded, gently, the way it always concluded, it was right and it is done and everything has already occurred, and occurrence runs in one direction only, like this street, like this count, left, right, left, all the way to a door she would open on an empty</p>

<p><em>Subito silenzio.</em></p>

<p>Her feet stopped.</p>

<p>In front of her building, on the cobblestones where the delivery trucks parked at six every morning, stood a suitcase, and holding the suitcase was a girl in a coat far too thin for Switzerland, breath fogging, chin lifted at the exact angle of someone who has been rehearsing an opening line for eight thousand kilometers and has just watched every version of it evaporate.</p>

<p>Sasha.</p>

<p>The name arrived in her before any decision to think it, and behind the name came everything else at once, colliding. Why is she here. How is she here. Something in Anna leapt, purely, shamelessly, the leap of a drowning hand closing on the one thing floating, and something else in her recoiled in the same instant, because they had ended, she had ended it, the verdict was still warm from the walk home, and the person she had spent three months writing to and deleting was standing at her door in the flesh, unerasable. Perhaps it was joy. Perhaps it was terror. Perhaps, and this was the unbearable part, it was both at once and indistinguishable, the way hot water and cold burn the same in the first second, and her body could not tell whether it had been rescued or caught.</p>

<p>Then twenty-two years of training arrived, half a breath behind her heart, and did what training does.</p>

<p>Anna straightened. Spine long, shoulders back, face arranged into the pleasant unreadable calm that had won her everything and cost her everything. It was a clean performance. It was, she knew somewhere underneath it, the performance of a girl four kilograms lighter than her posture, with a hospital band under her sleeve and her eyes already too bright, iron laid thin over black water, and she raised her chin and wore it anyway, because it was the only costume she had left.</p>

<p>"You shouldn't be here," she said.</p>

<p>"Good morning to you too." Sasha's voice was the same. Too loud for the quiet street, too alive for December. Her eyes were moving over Anna with a speed her stillness contradicted, the coat that fit differently now, the raw cheeks, the plastic band at the wrist that Anna's sleeve had not quite hidden. "You're white as the lake in January. Please tell me somebody discharged you. Tell me you didn't just put on your coat and walk out of a hospital, because that is exactly a thing you would do."</p>

<p>"I was discharged."</p>

<p>"And then you walked here. No taxi. In this cold." It wasn't knowledge; it was her eyes, doing what they had always done, reading an entry in half a second. "Of course you did. God forbid Anna Shcherbakova arrive anywhere leaning on something."</p>

<p>"Who called you?" Anna kept her voice level, filed flat. "Nobody in this country has your number. Somebody called you. Who?"</p>

<p>"Does it matter?"</p>

<p>"It matters."</p>

<p>"Then figure it out later, you're the smart one." Sasha set the suitcase down. The small sound of it meeting the cobblestones was very final, a thing being put down that did not intend to be picked up again soon. "Anya. Four days ago my phone rang. Unknown number, Swiss code. A girl I have never met in my life was crying into it in two languages, and the only words I understood on the first try were your name and the word hospital. You want to know what I asked her? I didn't ask why she had my number. I didn't ask what happened. I asked which airport." She spread her hands, simple as arithmetic. "So here I am. That's the whole story. Your turn."</p>

<p>"My turn." Anna heard her own voice and did not like it, the thinness in the iron, and pushed on anyway, because the voice from the walk home was still in her ear, feeding her the lines, and the lines were for Sasha's good, and that made them possible to say. "All right. Go home. I mean it. Whatever Lena told you, it's handled. There is nothing for you to do here."</p>

<p>"You're here."</p>

<p>"That's what I said."</p>

<p>It came out flatter and crueler than she intended. She watched it land, watched Sasha absorb the hit without stepping back, and the voice inside her approved: good, be cruel, cruelty is faster, cut the line before she ties it to you. And her hands, traitors, had wrapped themselves around her own elbows as if the wind had picked up. The wind had not picked up.</p>

<p>"Listen to me." She heard the volume rise, heard the performance begin to spend its last reserves. "You're looking at me like I'm the person you knew. I'm not her. That girl won things. That girl was going somewhere. This one gets accused of cheating because her English is too good for her passport, this one hasn't finished a meal since October, this one wakes up in hospitals with no name on her emergency form. I am not a project, Sasha, I'm a weight, and you, you're all speed, you've always been all speed, and if you stay you will spend a year hauling me and calling it love, and I will have to watch you get slower and grayer and learn what I weigh, and I would rather lose you than watch that. So go home. Letting you go was the one thing I've done right this whole year. Don't take it away from me."</p>

<p>The street was very quiet. Sasha looked at her for a long moment, and when she finally spoke, her voice had dropped out of its loudness into the register underneath all the noise, the one Anna had heard maybe three times in two years.</p>

<p>"There you are," Sasha said softly. "That's the most Shcherbakova thing you've ever said to me. Half a ghost, standing in the cold, planning my rescue. From you." She stepped over the suitcase, closing the distance by half, unhurried, giving Anna all the time in the world to retreat, and Anna did not retreat, and hated that she could not name why. "You want to talk about weight? Let me tell you what three months of being weightless is like. A warm apartment. Good tea. Nobody needing me for anything at all. I was so light, Anya, I barely touched the ground, and I have never in my life been so useless. So don't stand there rationing me for my own good. I've done the math you're doing. I've lived inside the answer. It's lighter, and it's worse."</p>

<p>"Sasha..."</p>

<p>"No, you had your speech. Here's mine, it's short." She stopped an arm's length away, close enough now that Anna could see she had been crying at some point over the ocean and had scrubbed the evidence badly. "Same deal as August, facing the other way. Tell me to go. Look at me, say the word, properly, and I'll get on the plane, and you can stand on your own feet forever. I said it to you once and you obeyed so fast the door didn't finish closing. Your turn. One word. I'm listening."</p>

<p><em>Fermata.</em></p>

<p>The street held its breath. Somewhere behind the buildings a tram sighed through its turn. Anna stood in the ruins of her posture and reached for the word, one syllable, she had said far harder things to far harder people, and she opened her mouth, and the word was there, it was right there, and what came out instead was:</p>

<p>"I made it."</p>

<p>Sasha blinked. "...What?"</p>

<p>"I made it. The lake is beautiful." Anna's voice had gone somewhere strange, quiet and even and completely out of her control, reciting. "I wish you were."</p>

<p>"Anya..."</p>

<p>"It's a message. From September. My first morning, I stood on the bridge with my suitcase and I typed it and I deleted it, and I have typed it every day since. Every single day, for three months. In lecture halls. In the market. In the hospital, with the needle still in, I typed it with one thumb and deleted it, and it never finishes, it can't finish, because the last word of it is here, and you were never..." The evenness cracked down the middle, and everything underneath it was suddenly audible. "And now you are. You're here, and I have been rehearsing go this whole time, and my mouth keeps sending the message instead. So you tell me what that means. Because I'm so tired, Sasha. I'm so tired of being the strong one, and I don't understand anything anymore."</p>

<p><em>Tre corde.</em></p>

<p>"I got it," Sasha said, and closed the last of the distance.</p>

<p>Anna's knees went the way they had gone on landings she knew were lost mid-air, and Sasha caught her, both arms, braced and sure, and Anna broke. In the open street, in front of God and the trucks and the whole indifferent city, she came apart the way she had not allowed herself to come apart even on the bathroom floor, without dignity, without management. Her fists found the front of that ridiculous thin coat and closed on it, both of them, the way a child grips, the way you grip the boards when your legs are done, and the coat smelled of airports and of a Moscow winter and underneath that, faint and undeniable, of two whole years of her life. She sobbed into it, great heaving pulls that bent her forward, three months of them, six months of them, twenty-two years of them, and Sasha took all of it standing, one arm locked across Anna's back, the other hand cradling the back of her head through the wool cap, swaying them both by slow degrees, the way water sways a pier it has decided to keep. Her chin was pressed into gray-brown hair, and her own eyes were shut hard, and she said nothing at all for a long time, and then, into Anna's hair, only once, so quietly it was almost weather:</p>

<p>"I'm here."</p>

<p>The suitcase waited on the cobblestones, patient as a decision already made.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Lena opened the apartment door, took in the scene on the landing, and her face performed several operations in rapid succession: surprise, recognition, guilt, and finally the specific expression of a comparative literature student calculating whether it is possible to be retroactively somewhere else.</p>

<p>Anna looked at her roommate. Anna looked at Sasha. The arithmetic took four seconds, which was three more than usual, so she blamed the hospital.</p>

<p>"Lena."</p>

<p>"I have," Lena announced, already retreating, "very strong opinions about Brahms that urgently require expression," and her door closed, and a moment later the violin started up through the wall, loud and terrible and utterly unrepentant.</p>

<p>"She does that every evening," Anna said. "Eight to nine."</p>

<p>"It's eleven in the morning."</p>

<p>"She's improvising."</p>

<p>"I like her," Sasha decided, "she has the survival instincts of a whole federation," and walked into the kitchen as though she had lived there for years, opening cupboards, taking inventory, frowning at the emptiness of them. Anna stood in the doorway of her own apartment and watched a girl from Moscow colonize her kitchen, and did not have the strength to stop her, or perhaps the desire; perhaps those had always been the same thing.</p>

<p>Sasha filled the kettle and set it on the flame. While it heated, she moved through the small room touching things, cataloguing, the way she scoped out any new rink before she trusted it, and stopped at the shelf.</p>

<p>"You didn't finish the chocolate."</p>

<p>"No."</p>

<p>"There's one square gone. One. Since when, September?" She turned, holding the wrapper up like evidence. "Anya. You. Chocolate. In what universe."</p>

<p>"It's a long story."</p>

<p>"I brought a suitcase."</p>

<p>Anna sat down at the small table because her legs suggested it. Sasha took the kettle off the flame in the half-second before it could begin to scream, poured two cups, found the honey behind the tea where honey always hides, set one cup in front of Anna, and then, instead of drinking her own, folded her arms on the table, rested her chin on them, and looked up with the flat, patient, unbudging attention that Anna had seen defeat coaches, choreographers, federations, and gravity.</p>

<p>"So. Zurich. From the beginning, and not the postcard version."</p>

<p>"The program is good." Anna wrapped both hands around the cup, borrowing its warmth. "That part is true. The lectures, the library, the professors. Everything I came for."</p>

<p>"Mm."</p>

<p>"The city is beautiful. You saw the lake."</p>

<p>"I saw the lake." Sasha did not move, chin on her arms, eyes steady. She had never once in her life been fooled by a clean short program, and she made no attempt to hurry the long one. The kettle ticked as it cooled. Through the wall, Lena's Brahms wandered into a wrong key and stayed there out of principle.</p>

<p>Anna turned her cup a quarter turn on its saucer.</p>

<p>"There's a girl in my project group," she said. "Margaux." A pause, and then, because the wrapping was easier to hand over than the thing: "She's very polite."</p>

<p>"Polite."</p>

<p>"Very. It's a local specialty." Another quarter turn. "The first meeting, I brought my notes. Printed, referenced, weeks of work, you know how I..." She stopped. Started somewhere easier. "She read them for a while. And then she asked me, in front of everyone, who translated them for me. I said I wrote them. And she smiled, and she said, and you'll appreciate this part, she said: your English is really good." Anna lifted her eyes and delivered the rest lightly, the way you place a punchline, the way you hand someone a knife handle-first so no one can say you cut them. "For a Russian."</p>

<p>Sasha's chin came up off her arms, very slowly. She did not laugh. The joke Anna had wrapped it in sat between them on the table, unlaughed at, and thinned, and disappeared.</p>

<p>"And the others in the group," Sasha said. "While this was happening."</p>

<p>"Oliver laughed. Sofie studied the table. Sofie has studied the table a great deal this semester, she'll get a distinction in it." The lightness was fraying at the edges now, and Anna heard it fraying and could not stop it. "It became a routine. Every meeting. Always small, always smiling. Are things like this in Russia. Sorry, I assumed you wouldn't know. Little doses, Sasha, that's the craft of it, each one too small to report, you'd sound insane saying it out loud, she asked me if we have libraries." Quarter turn. "You can't complain about a dose that small. That's the design. The sum of them is..." Her hand made a small motion and gave up. "The sum of them is October."</p>

<p>"And November?" Sasha's voice had gone quiet, which on her was not calm; it was the sound of weather being held offshore by hand. "Lena said November got worse. What was November?"</p>

<p>Anna looked into her cup.</p>

<p>"They flagged the project," she said. "My sections."</p>

<p>"Flagged."</p>

<p>"There was an email. There's a review scheduled. Concerns about academic integrity, regarding my..." Quarter turn. The saucer made its small ceramic sound in the quiet. "There is a suspicion, formally lodged, that I did not write my own words. That the English is too fluent. For someone of my background." She said the last words the way you set down something heavy, in pieces, because carrying it whole was no longer possible. "I learned that language in buses and locker rooms and hotel rooms, Sasha. Half of it I learned from you reading poetry at two in the morning like it owed you money. Every word in that project is mine. I have never in my life stolen so much as an edge in a warmup, and they put the word integrity in an email, about me, and I read it eleven times looking for a different meaning, and then I stopped being able to eat."</p>

<p>The violin had gone quiet without either of them noticing. Sasha reached across the table, and her hand came down gently over Anna's, and stopped the cup mid-turn, and held it still, and held it, warm and heavy, until the stillness had traveled all the way up Anna's arm.</p>

<p>"You wrote every word," Sasha said.</p>

<p>"I wrote every word."</p>

<p>"That's what I thought." She gave the hand one small squeeze, and only then let the other thing show, banked and orderly, the way she banked it before a second half. "What's her name. Full name."</p>

<p>"Why?"</p>

<p>"No reason. People ask names. It's polite. I hear it's a local specialty."</p>

<p>"Sasha. Sit down."</p>

<p>"I'm sitting." She was, technically. "Look how seated I am. Full name, the coordinator's email, and the review date, and then I promise I will drink my tea like a civilized person."</p>

<p>"You are not coming to an academic integrity review."</p>

<p>"I'm extremely calm," Sasha said, in the voice of a kettle taken off the flame one half-second before the scream, "and we can discuss what I'm coming to later. Drink your tea. It's going cold, and I put honey in, the way you take it."</p>

<p>And because her hand was still warm from being held, Anna drank.</p>

<p>Sasha watched her. Anna knew what she was watching for. Two years of shared kitchens, hotel breakfasts in eleven countries, one spoon and two mouths, and Sasha had learned her the way Anna learned scores: the small hum, low in the throat, barely a breath, the sound Anna made without knowing she made it when something landed sweet on her tongue. Sasha was leaning into the arrival of it, chin back on her folded arms, waiting the way you wait for a note you have heard a thousand times.</p>

<p><em>Sospeso.</em></p>

<p>It did not come.</p>

<p>Anna set the cup down. And she could have performed the hum, she had thought about it in the half-second the cup was at her lips, and she was so tired of performing that instead she looked at the table and said, quietly, "I can't taste it."</p>

<p>"The honey? Lena has a whole jar, I can..."</p>

<p>"Anything, Sasha. I can't taste anything."</p>

<p>The kitchen went still.</p>

<p>"Since when?"</p>

<p>"November. It's called ageusia. Everything is temperature and texture and nothing else. The chocolate stopped first, then coffee, then all of it. Your tea is warm, and it's wet. That's the entire report." She turned the cup, caught herself doing it, stopped. "The doctors say it's the stress. That it may come back when I'm better. May."</p>

<p>"Why didn't you call me?" It came out with a rough edge, and underneath the roughness, unmistakable, hurt. "November, Anya. Six weeks. You sat in this kitchen for six weeks eating cardboard and you didn't..."</p>

<p>"And say what?" Anna's head came up. "Hello, I know you told me to go, I know you're living your life, but food stopped working and I thought of you? What is the etiquette for that call, Sasha? Because I looked. There isn't one."</p>

<p>"The etiquette is you call and I answer." Sasha's voice cracked its loudness open, and what was inside it was not anger at all. "That's the whole etiquette. That's been the whole etiquette since we were fifteen, and no argument in any kitchen ever changed it, and the fact that you didn't know that is my fault as much as yours, so fine. Okay." She dragged both hands down her face, and when they came away she was already rolling up her sleeves, grief converting to motion the only way she had ever known how to process anything. "We're not spending tonight on the six weeks. Where does Lena keep that honey. No, I saw it, sit."</p>

<p>"What are you doing?"</p>

<p>"Starting over." The jar came down onto the table with a small decisive clink. She unscrewed the lid, loaded one spoon with a slow amber rope of it, unhurried, ceremonial. "Close your eyes."</p>

<p>"Sasha, it doesn't work like that. It's not a switch, you can't just..."</p>

<p>"Close your eyes, Shcherbakova."</p>

<p>Anna closed her eyes. The spoon touched her lip, tilted; sweetness that she knew was sweetness only by memory sat on her tongue, warm from the room, thick, a texture wearing the ghost of a name. She swallowed. She opened her eyes and shook her head once, and watched for the discouragement to land on Sasha's face.</p>

<p>It didn't land. Nothing had ever landed on that face except ice, and she had always gotten up.</p>

<p>"Fine. Honey, zero." Sasha licked the spoon herself, frank and shameless. "Still works on my end, so your supply is fine, it's the receiver. Tomorrow, lemon."</p>

<p>"Lemon."</p>

<p>"Then salt. Then that poor chocolate, since it's been sitting on the shelf for three months being ignored, which I understand is the custom of this household." She pointed the spoon at Anna, a verdict with honey still on it. "We're going to go through this whole city one spoon at a time, every market stall, every terrible Swiss cheese, until something answers you. I don't care if it takes until spring. I've built whole seasons out of worse odds than a tongue."</p>

<p>"That is not medicine, Sasha."</p>

<p>"It's not medicine, it's training. Medicine is what they gave you at the hospital. Training is what you do every day anyway, because sitting still and waiting to get better was never once in your life your discipline. Or mine." She leaned forward on her elbows, and all the loudness in her gathered itself into something small and sure. "One spoon a day. You don't have to taste it. You just have to show up to the rink. Deal?"</p>

<p>Anna stared at her. The plan was absurd. It was medically illiterate, logistically vague, and delivered with the confidence of a girl who had once attempted five quads in a single program because somebody said it couldn't be done, and Anna felt something move in her chest that had not moved since September, felt it climb, and it came out as a laugh, wet and cracked and unpracticed, the first one in three months, at a spoonful of nothing.</p>

<p>"There she is," Sasha said quietly, and reached across the small table, and tucked a strand of hair behind Anna's ear with two fingers, and left her hand against her cheek a moment longer than the errand required, and Anna, who had a doctrine against leaning, leaned.</p>

<p>Through the wall, Lena's violin found, for four consecutive notes, something very close to beauty, and lost it again immediately, and neither of them minded at all.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Night came early, the way December nights do in Zurich, folding itself over the narrow street by five.</p>

<p>They did not talk about August. They would have to, eventually; there was a conversation waiting for them with fourteen letters in it, and another one, further off, containing the name Margaux and whatever Sasha was so extremely calm about, and beyond that a suitcase in the hallway whose contents implied questions about visas and futures that neither of them had the strength to open tonight. Tonight there was soup, which Anna ate for the warmth and Sasha narrated for the flavor, leaning over her own bowl like a commentator over a rink: "Attention, the carrot. Sweet, but it's an honest sweetness, it's not showing off like the honey. The pepper comes in late, right at the back of the throat, very rude, no warning at all. You'd like the pepper. The pepper is me." And it was, in the strictest medical sense, nothing, and it was the best meal Anna had eaten in three months.</p>

<p><em>Smorzando.</em></p>

<p>Later, on the narrow bed that was not built for two and accommodated them anyway, because they had spent years fitting into spaces not built for what they were, Anna lay with her ear against Sasha's chest in the dark.</p>

<p>"Sasha."</p>

<p>"Mm."</p>

<p>"...Nothing. Stay."</p>

<p>The arm around her tightened, unhurried, certain, gathering her in by one more degree, and lips came to rest against her hair. "Sleep. I'm right here, my Anna."</p>

<p>The heartbeat under her ear was slow and even. Out of habit older than love, Anna began to count it, one, two, the lifelong metronome, the discipline that had held everything up, and somewhere between one beat and the next, warm under a strange city's blankets, she lost the count on purpose for the first time in her life.</p>

<p>Nothing fell apart.</p>

<p>Outside the window, above the cobblestones and the sleeping delivery trucks, the snow finally decided to fall.</p>
