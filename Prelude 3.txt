<p align="center"><em>Andante sostenuto, sotto voce</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The rink at six in the morning belonged to no one, which was the building's polite way of saying it belonged to whoever wanted it badly enough to arrive before the lights did. Kamila came up the back stairs with her bag on one shoulder and the street's cold still folded into her breath, and she told herself, the way she told herself every morning she arrived this early, that this was discipline, that champions were made in the hours nobody watched, and that the flutter under her sternum was only the climb.</p>

<p>She heard the ice before she saw it.</p>

<p>Through the tunnel the sound arrived ahead of the light: one long drawn line of steel on ice, unhurried, sustained, the sound of an edge that had nowhere it needed to be and the whole morning to get there. Kamila stopped in the tunnel mouth with her hand on the cold rail and listened the way a person listens at a door they have already decided not to knock on.</p>

<p>Anna was already there. Of course Anna was already there; her senior greeted the ice before the maintenance man had found his keys, and everyone at the club knew it the way they knew which door stuck in winter, as a fact of the building itself. Only one bank of lights had been switched on, the far one, so half the rink lay in a pale gold wash and half in blue shadow, and Anna skated the border between them without once looking down at it, crossing from the lit country into the dark one and back as if she held papers for both.</p>

<p>Kamila stayed at the boards. The dark half. She had a reason ready in case anyone asked, warm-up space, the posted schedule, an honest early start, and the reason was true, and it was also a coat she had buttoned over the real thing, and she knew about the coat, and she wore it anyway.</p>

<p>The long edges came first. Held, and held, each one a breath drawn out past the point where an ordinary lung would have asked for mercy, the blade writing a single unbroken line into the fresh ice while the boards ticked with cold and the vents exhaled somewhere high in the dark. Then the crossovers, patient, even, left over right over left, each stroke landing exactly where the last one promised it would, and Kamila discovered without deciding to that her breathing had settled into them, in for four strokes, out for four, as if her body had been waiting all along for someone to set the pace so it could stop pretending it kept its own.</p>

<p>Older colleague. That was the phrase for it, the one that fit on forms and in interviews. Anna was her senior, three national titles her senior, the skater whose warm-up Kamila had copied at twelve from a grainy video without telling anyone; standing at the boards of the same rink was the ordinary privilege of the same club, and there was a word for what it felt like, and Kamila kept that word in a drawer labeled "respect," and every morning the drawer shut a little less easily than the morning before.</p>

<p><em>Poco a poco accelerando.</em></p>

<p>The steps began. Where the crossovers had ticked, the turns now spilled, twice the sounds in the same breath and then twice again, edges flickering from outside to inside faster than the eye could file them, so that the ice stopped reporting single strokes and began reporting weather, a bright continuous scatter like sleet against a window, and Kamila's careful counted breathing lost its place in it and gave up counting and simply followed.</p>

<p>Then the pattern opened out. One deep curve, the whole rink gathered into it, and the sound narrowed back to a single held note that grew and grew the way a wave does when it has already chosen its shore.</p>

<p><em>Sospeso.</em></p>

<p>Silence. The ice said nothing. For the space of half a second there was no Anna anywhere the ice could speak of, no edge, no weight, no line, only the vents breathing in the ceiling and a girl at the boards who had forgotten she owned lungs, and Kamila's heart, unasked, offered to keep the count in the ice's place, one, and,</p>

<p>the landing came down on its beat like something the morning had promised and kept, one clean report and the long ride out of it, and the world resumed its account as if it had never been interrupted, as if flight were a rumor nobody could prove.</p>

<p><em>Come prima.</em></p>

<p>The long edges again. Unhurried again. As if nothing. What was she doing here, really, in the blue half of an empty rink at six in the morning, watching a program she could have watched at a competition like everyone else, from a seat, with a ticket, with the safety of a crowd around her?</p>

<p>The rail's cold had worked through her glove while she stood there; her fingers ached along the bone, small and bright, the ache of holding on to something that was never going to warm up first.</p>

<p>She did not answer the question. She filed it in the drawer with the word, and the drawer, this time, did not quite close.</p>

<p>On the ice, Anna gathered the last pattern in. The spin rose, narrowed, blurred, a spindle of pale gold where she had drifted into the lit country to end it, and every skater alive finishes facing the judges, it is built into the body before the triples are, ribs turned to the seats where the marks come from, arms opened to the people who decide.</p>

<p>Anna came out of the spin facing the boards.</p>

<p>The dark half. The exact stretch of it where a girl stood who had not made a sound since the tunnel, and Anna held the finish there, arms open to an audience of one, a heartbeat longer than an empty rink required, her breath standing in the cold air between them like a small white flag.</p>
