<p align="center"><em>Allegretto, a due voci</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Anna lowered her arms without hurry, the way a held note is allowed to end rather than made to, and pushed off toward the far boards for her water bottle, and the rink returned to its ordinary account of one skater breathing and one compressor humming, and the silence she left behind in the dark half of the building was not empty. It was a question. It had Kamila's name on it, and it waited the way fresh ice waits, patient, white, keeping the whole morning open.</p>

<p>Kamila sat down on the bench in the shadows and answered it the only way she knew how, which was with her hands. Boot, tongue, lace. Cross, pull. Cross, pull. The small rhythm of getting ready, so old in her fingers it ran without supervision, and this morning it ran quick, because her fingers had understood before the rest of her was consulted that whatever happened next had to happen before daylight made the rink public and turned the two of them back into a schedule.</p>

<p>She stepped through the gate in the dark half, and she did not warm up with her own pattern. She had one, assigned, sensible, drilled into her legs by good coaches, and she left it on the bench with her guards. What she laid down instead was a line she had never once skated in front of another living person.</p>

<p>She gathered speed out of the left corner, three strokes and the third one deeper, changed edge exactly where the blue shadow gave way to gold, and rose into half a turn in the air, no more than that, a small lift an audience would forgive and forget, and came down rolling backward into a spiral that took the long way around the corner, patient, old-fashioned in its manners, a phrase from a junior program filmed eleven years ago by somebody's shaking hand and uploaded sideways to the internet, where a nine-year-old in Kazan had found it and watched it until the player wore a groove in the winter.</p>

<p>From the far boards, the sound of a water bottle being set down very carefully, the way things are set down when a person has stopped paying attention to their hands. Kamila did not look over. Looking over was not survivable. She held the spiral out to its end and heard, instead of a voice, strokes.</p>

<p>The girl in the gold half gathered speed out of the right corner, three strokes and the third one deeper, changed edge exactly where the gold gave way to blue shadow, and rose into half a turn in the air, no more than that, turning the other way, and came down rolling backward into a spiral that took the long way around the opposite corner, patient, exact, mannerly, wearing a small smile that had arrived from somewhere and had no intention of leaving.</p>

<p>It was her line. It was her own line handed back across the ice, every left made right, every curve opened where hers had closed, and Anna skated it the way you repeat a question you already know the answer to, and Kamila's edge wobbled for the first time all morning, a hiccup in the writing, because being seen is one thing and being read back is another country entirely.</p>

<p>"There was a video," she said, too fast, to the ice more than to anyone. "Years ago. Juniors, the rink with the green boards. Someone uploaded it flipped, the whole file, mirrored, and I didn't know, so I. I was nine." A push that went nowhere in particular. "I only watched it a few times."</p>

<p>Anna drifted past her, backward, unhurried, close enough that the air moved.</p>

<p>"A few times," she said. "You're the worst liar in this building."</p>

<p><em>Poco a poco stringendo.</em></p>

<p>And then the phrase would not stay finished. Anna took it up again from her end, quicker now, and it came out shortened, tightened, the spiral clipped to a comma, an invitation with a clock on it.</p>

<p>Kamila answered before she had decided to. Left where Anna went right, in where Anna went out, half a beat behind and closing.</p>

<p>Anna turned and ran the line backward, facing her, skating her own choreography in reverse just to keep her eyes where they were, which was on Kamila, eyebrows up, mouthing the count at her like a secret being handed over the border.</p>

<p>On the tight turn Kamila's edge caught, one bad centimeter, and a hand arrived under her elbow for exactly one beat, warm even through two gloves, and was gone before it could be thanked, and the phrase never stopped moving.</p>

<p>They chased. There is no politer word for it and no reason to want one. The gap between entry and answer closed from a beat to a half-beat to a breath, curve against counter-curve, the border of the light running down the middle of the rink and the two of them plaiting across it until the lit country and the dark one were only weather, only something the morning was doing overhead while the real geography got written underfoot. Kamila's lungs burned. Somewhere in her chest a drawer stood open, and the label had come off in the cold, and she did not go looking for it.</p>

<p>"I know, by the way," Anna said, conversational, entirely unbreathless, drawing level on the outside. "You warm up half a beat behind me. Every morning. On the wrong foot." A push. "Three years. I thought you did it on purpose, to be symmetrical."</p>

<p>"I didn't know I was doing it at all."</p>

<p>"I know," Anna said. "That was my favorite part."</p>

<p><em>Stretto.</em></p>

<p>And then there was no gap left to close. Shoulder to shoulder down the long axis of the rink, Anna's right stroke and Kamila's left landing in the same instant, the small lift taken together and turning opposite ways in the air like a coin and its reflection, mirrored from the hips down like a figure folded out of one piece of paper, and the ice, which all morning had reported first one skater and then two, gave up the distinction entirely: two edges cutting one sound, one long bright line of it, laid down the seam where the light met the dark. Eleven years of learning the pattern alone in the wrong direction, and the wrong direction, it turned out, had been the right one the whole time, because a mirror only looks like a mistake until the original steps up beside it, and then it looks like the other half.</p>

<p>The phrase ran out where phrases run out, in the corner where it had always ended, eleven years ago on a shaking camera and this morning on a shared edge, and neither of them prepared anything after it, so they carried their speed together into the last curve and let the ice spend it for them, slower, and slower, the single sound thinning to a whisper, the whisper to a breath.</p>

<p><em>Fermata.</em></p>

<p>They stopped at center ice, closer than colleagues stand, not touching, their breath climbing in two white columns that did not stay two for long, and neither of them said the word, because the word was down on the ice already and had been all morning, written left-handed and right-handed over the whole white page, crossing and recrossing along the seam of the light until no reader alive could have told whose line was whose.</p>
