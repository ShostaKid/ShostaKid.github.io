<p align="center"><em>Adagio, ma con due voci</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p><em>Attacca.</em></p>

<p>The knock came in three, evenly spaced, each one given its full value before the next was permitted, a hand that had never in its long existence hurried a door.</p>

<p>She opened it on a man standing very straight in the rain he had brought with him, hat in hand, water running off the brim in a thin patient stream onto her landing, and the first thing he did was apologize for the hour, and the second thing he did was apologize for the weather, which was, Furina thought, the single most Fontaine sentence ever constructed, an apology from a man for the crime of having a heart in public.</p>

<p>"It is unseasonal," Neuvillette said. "I regret the inconvenience."</p>

<p>"To me, or to the city?"</p>

<p>"To you," he said, after a pause that had rather more in it than the words did. "Principally."</p>

<p>She stood aside and made a small flourish of the doorway, all wrist, the gesture of a hostess in a play about hostesses, and he came in the way a large formal thing comes into a small warm one, taking care with the coat rack, with the low beam, with the entire architecture of a life that had been built at human scale by someone who had spent five centuries at another.</p>

<p>"Sit," she said. "There. No, that one, the other one has opinions about people."</p>

<p>He sat. The chair, which was a perfectly ordinary chair and had never been asked to bear the Iudex of Fontaine, made no complaint, and Neuvillette arranged himself in it with his hands on his knees like a man giving evidence, and the rain went on saying what it was saying against the window.</p>

<p>Furina turned to the counter and performed tea.</p>

<p>It was one of her better performances, she thought. Bright, brisk, narrated throughout, the leaves discussed as if they were guests of some standing, and it lasted exactly as long as it needed to, which was until she reached into the cupboard and had to take down the second cup, the one she had put back an hour ago, and her hand paused on it for the length of a held breath before it came down, and she covered the pause with a joke about the weather, and set them both on the table, and sat.</p>

<p>Behind her, the rain leaned against the glass a fraction harder.</p>

<p>He had seen the pause. Of course he had seen it; he had spent an age of the world watching people's hands while they said untrue things, and the whole shape of his profession was knowing which silence in a testimony was the load-bearing one. He said nothing about it. He turned his cup a quarter turn on its saucer, and looked at it, and Furina recognized the gesture with a small internal lurch, because it was hers, it was the thing she did before the harder sentences, and she had never once considered that it might be catching.</p>

<p>"I have come to ask how you are," he said.</p>

<p>"Splendid," she said, instantly, the way you catch a dropped plate. "Terribly busy. This building has a residents' association and I have views."</p>

<p>"Furina."</p>

<p>"There is a dispute about the stairwell. It is a scandal. I may write a monograph."</p>

<p><em>Sotto voce.</em></p>

<p>"I have listened to testimony for five hundred years," Neuvillette said, and his voice had not risen at all, had in fact come down, come nearer, lost some architecture and gained something else. "I know what it sounds like when a witness answers a question they were not asked."</p>

<p>The teaspoon touched the saucer. Once. She did not set it down again after that; it stayed in her hand, and her hand stayed still, and the clock in the other room went on alone, counting a tempo nobody in the kitchen was keeping any more.</p>

<p>"That is a very unfair use of a courtroom voice in a private residence," she said, "and I would like it noted."</p>

<p>"So noted."</p>

<p>"And struck from the record."</p>

<p>"No," he said, gently, which was somehow so much worse than if he had said it any other way.</p>

<p>The rain came down harder. Not violently; it simply thickened, the way a held note thickens when the player leans into it, filling the room's silence so completely that the silence stopped being a thing either of them could hide inside, and Furina looked at the window, and then, for the first time since he had come in, at him.</p>

<p>"You know I can read that," she said quietly. "The sky. I have had rather a long time to learn."</p>

<p>Neuvillette was still. It was a stillness with a great deal of movement underneath it, the way deep water is still.</p>

<p>"Then you have me at a disadvantage," he said, "which is, I find, entirely just."</p>

<p>"What does it say?"</p>

<p>"You know what it says."</p>

<p>"I would like," said Furina, and her voice did something on the way through that she had not authorized, "to hear it in the other language. The one with words in it. I have had five hundred years of being told things by weather."</p>

<p>He set his cup down. He looked at his hands for a moment, and when he spoke his sentences had lost their scaffolding entirely, and what was left was short, and low, and had no distance in it whatsoever.</p>

<p>"I saw you," he said. "In the court, and in the streets, and at every ceremony where you laughed on cue. I saw the seam in it. Every day, for five hundred years, I saw exactly what it cost you, and I said nothing, because I believed my silence was the shape my duty took." A pause; the rain waited in it. "I was mistaken. It was the shape my cowardice took, and it wore my duty's coat, and I have been unable to look at the sea since without knowing the difference."</p>

<p>Furina did not perform anything.</p>

<p>That was the remarkable part, the part she would turn over later at odd hours: there was no moment in which she decided to stop. The performance simply was not there, the way a hand you have been clenching is suddenly open and you cannot say when, and what came out of her was small and unlovely and true.</p>

<p>"I used to look for you," she said. "In the gallery. Every session. I told myself it was to check that you were watching the proceedings correctly."</p>

<p>"I know," said Neuvillette. "I sat where I did so that you would be able to."</p>

<p><em>Fermata.</em></p>

<p>Nothing moved for a moment. Two cups, two people, one rain.</p>

<p>Then Furina laughed, once, wetly, and pressed the back of her wrist against her eyes with the outrage of a woman whose evening has been ruined by a guest, and said that this was unconscionable, that she had built a very good life here with a stairwell dispute in it and he had walked in and put water all over her landing, and Neuvillette said that he would see to the landing, and meant it, and would, and neither of them was talking about the landing, and both of them were, and the two things did not cancel; they sounded together, low and bright at once, and held.</p>

<p><em>Calando.</em></p>

<p>The rain eased. Neither of them mentioned it. It thinned over the roofs and let go of the glass by degrees, the drops slowing on the pane, gathering, running down at last into single unhurried lines, and somewhere past the harbour the cloud opened just enough for the late light to come through, and it came through the wet window and lay across the table in two long bars, one over each cup.</p>
