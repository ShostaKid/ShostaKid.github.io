<p align="center"><em>Adagio, ma con due voci</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The apartment was small in the way she had chosen, four rooms and no throne in any of them, and Furina de Fontaine performed her evening in it flawlessly for no one at all.</p>

<p>She set two cups on the table and remembered, on the second one, and put it back. She narrated the weather to the window. She delivered a small witticism to the kettle and gave it the pause a good line deserves, and the kettle, being water and metal, offered the only honest review she had received in some considerable time, which was none. Five hundred years is a long run. The body does not strike the set the night the show closes; it keeps hitting its marks in an empty house, turning to the wings for a scene partner who was written out, and Furina had learned to let it. There was a grace in playing to no audience, she had decided. It was the only performance in which no one could be disappointed.</p>

<p>She had counted the audience once, in the years right after. The trick was to stop counting. The trick was the teaspoon, the way it touched the saucer on the same small beat each time she set it down, a sound she could keep and keep and keep, a metronome the size of a fingernail, proof that time was passing in an orderly fashion and would continue to, that the next minute would resemble this one, that nothing enormous was going to be asked of her tonight.</p>

<p>The teaspoon touched the saucer. The clock in the other room answered it. Two small dry sounds, indoors, in tempo, the whole architecture of an evening she had built to be survivable.</p>

<p>She did not look at the sky. She had made a rule of it. The sky, in this city, said too much.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Across the water, in a chamber built to hold the truth still, Neuvillette set down his pen.</p>

<p>He had been reading the same line of a report for the better part of an hour, which was unlike him; the Iudex read the way glaciers move, once and completely and without needing a second pass. But the sentence would not resolve into meaning, because underneath it, in the place where he kept the things he did not say in court, a question had been standing all evening with the patience of a plaintiff who knows the docket will reach them eventually.</p>

<p>He wondered how she was.</p>

<p>He had wondered it, he realized, every evening for some time now, and had every evening found a reason the wondering could wait, a decree, a hearing, the dignified paralysis of a being who had five hundred years of formality between himself and the simple act of crossing a bridge. He had watched her carry the whole weight of a nation's judgment while the nation looked away, had passed her in corridors and inclined his head the precise correct number of degrees, had said the right things at the right remove, and had never once said the true thing, which was smaller than all of it and heavier than all of it together: <em>I saw you. The entire time. I saw.</em></p>

<p>He stood. The decision did not feel like a decision; it felt like the moment a long-held note is finally allowed to change, the relief of a suspension resolving after it has hung past all endurance.</p>

<p>And the sky, which in this city had never learned to keep his confidence, told on him at once.</p>

<p>It began as almost nothing over the rooftops, a softening of the air, a darkening of stone, the first fine rain coming down not in drops a person could count but in a hush, a held breath let out slowly across the whole of Fontaine, and it moved as he moved, gathering as he crossed the bridge, quickening along the streets that led to the small apartment with no throne in it, until it arrived at her window a little ahead of him and set the first tapping against the glass.</p>

<p>Inside, the teaspoon stopped against the saucer, and did not come back down.</p>

<p>Furina de Fontaine, who had made a rule of not looking at the sky, turned her head toward the window and read the whole confession running down it, every word, in the one language in all the world she had never had to learn, because she had watched five hundred years of this city's weather and knew precisely one thing about it that its own Iudex was still learning: it only ever wept for one reason, and never for itself.</p>

<p>She set the teaspoon down without a sound.</p>

<p>She did not perform the next moment. There was no one to perform it for, or there was about to be the only one who had never counted as an audience, and for the space of a single breath, standing in a small bright room while the dark came sweet and wet against the glass, Furina let her own face do exactly what it felt, unrehearsed, unwatched, "chờ," waiting, and did not name it, and did not need to.</p>

<p>The rain found the window and stayed.</p>
