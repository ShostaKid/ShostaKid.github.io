<p align="center"><em>Allegro con brio, ma con un cuore inquieto</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The first month in Zurich was bright.</p>

<p>Anna arrived in late September when the city was still holding onto the last warmth of summer, and the lake reflected a sky so blue and so clean that she stood on the Quaibrücke for twenty minutes on her first morning simply looking at it, her suitcase beside her and her phone in her hand and the draft of a message to Sasha still glowing on the screen: <em>"I made it. The lake is beautiful. I wish you were—"</em> She deleted the last four words, then deleted the rest, then put the phone in her pocket and picked up the suitcase and walked toward the university, because Anna Shcherbakova had always been better at walking forward than at looking back, and looking back was a luxury she had decided, three weeks and one terrible argument ago, that she could no longer afford.</p>

<p>The apartment the university had assigned her was small and clean, on the fourth floor of a building near Bellevue, with a window that overlooked a narrow cobblestone street where delivery trucks rumbled past every morning at six. Her roommate was a German girl named Lena who studied comparative literature and played the violin badly but enthusiastically every evening between eight and nine, and who greeted Anna on the first day with a firm handshake and a packet of Swiss chocolate and the words "I hope you don't mind noise, because I have very strong opinions about Brahms and I express them at volume." Anna had smiled and broken a square from the chocolate bar, letting it melt on her tongue, tasting the bitterness of the cacao and the sweetness of the cream and the faint, smoky undertone that Swiss chocolate carries like a signature, and she had thought: <em>this is what a new beginning tastes like.</em></p>

<p>She enrolled in the comparative linguistics programme, attended her first lecture in a hall with vaulted ceilings and wooden benches that smelled of old paper, and took notes with a pen she held too tightly, the way she always did, pressing the ink into the page as though she were trying to make the words permanent enough to replace the ones she had lost. The professors spoke in English with various European accents, and Anna's English was good, better than most of the other international students, because she had spent years learning it through music lyrics and competition interviews and, more recently, through late-night conversations with a girl who used to lie beside her and read English poetry aloud in a voice that was too loud and too fast and too full of life to ever be mistaken for anything other than Sasha's.</p>

<p>She did not think about Sasha. She had made a decision not to think about Sasha, and Anna Shcherbakova was a person who kept her decisions the way a musician keeps time, with discipline and precision and the private, quiet knowledge that the moment she stopped counting, everything would fall apart.</p>

<p>The first month was bright. Anna told herself this, and for a while, it was almost true.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The second month was when the cracks appeared.</p>

<p>They came not as a single fracture but as a slow accumulation of small injuries, each one too minor to justify complaint but together forming a pattern that Anna recognized because she had spent her entire career learning to recognize patterns on ice, the hairline stress fractures in a programme that looked perfect from the judges' table but that the skater could feel beneath her blades, the tiny deviations in rhythm that told her a fall was coming before the body knew it.</p>

<p>It started with the group project.</p>

<p>There were four of them: Anna, a French girl named Margaux, a British boy named Oliver, and a Dutch girl named Sofie. The project was a comparative analysis of linguistic borrowing between Slavic and Romance languages, a topic Anna found genuinely interesting, and she had prepared her section with the meticulous thoroughness that she applied to everything, arriving at the first group meeting with printed notes and highlighted references and a timeline of proposed milestones.</p>

<p>Margaux looked at the notes, looked at Anna, and said, in a tone that was perfectly polite and perfectly cutting, "That's very thorough. Did you have someone translate these for you?"</p>

<p>"I wrote them myself," Anna said, confused.</p>

<p>"Right. It's just, the phrasing is a bit unusual in places. A bit... Russian, you know?" Margaux smiled the kind of smile that is designed to look friendly while communicating something that is not friendly at all. "No offense. Your English is really good. For a Russian."</p>

<p>Oliver laughed. It was a small laugh, the kind that people produce when they want to signal allegiance without taking responsibility for what they are aligning with. Sofie said nothing, which was its own kind of statement.</p>

<p>Anna said nothing either, because she did not trust her voice not to crack, and because she had learned, years ago on the ice, that the most effective response to a judge who has already decided your score is to simply skate the programme and let the blade marks speak for themselves.</p>

<p>But the blade marks did not speak here. This was not ice. This was a seminar room in a Swiss university where the rules were different and the judges did not hold up numbers and the falls were not physical but left bruises in places that no one could see.</p>

<p>It did not stop. It never stopped. It only evolved, the way a melody modulates through keys, always recognisably the same tune but wearing different harmonics each time it returned. Margaux's comments became a fixed feature of the group meetings, always wrapped in the language of helpfulness, always plausibly deniable, always carrying the specific, targeted cruelty of someone who had identified a weakness and was applying pressure to it with surgical consistency. "Are things like that in Russia?" when Anna expressed an opinion. "Sorry, I just assumed you wouldn't know, since your education system is so different" when Anna cited a source. "Oh, you celebrate that holiday? How interesting, I've always wanted to understand how Russian people think" when Anna mentioned missing home.</p>

<p>The other students in the programme did not intervene. Some of them smiled the way Oliver smiled, small and complicit. Others looked away, the way Sofie looked away, which was a different kind of complicity that felt, in some ways, worse, because at least the ones who smiled were honest about what they were doing.</p>

<p>And then there was the food.</p>

<p>It happened so gradually that Anna did not notice it until it had already settled into her body like a tenant who moves in while the landlord is sleeping. One evening in early November, Lena brought home two bowls of pho from the Vietnamese place on Niederdorfstrasse, the one that Anna had loved on her first visit because the broth was rich and peppery and reminded her, in some inexplicable way, of the borscht her grandmother used to make. Lena set the bowl in front of her, and Anna lifted the spoon to her mouth, and the broth entered, and there was nothing.</p>

<p>Not "nothing" as in "bland." Nothing as in absence. As in the complete, total failure of her tongue to register any sensation beyond temperature and texture. The liquid was hot and wet and that was all. No salt, no pepper, no umami, no depth. She chewed a piece of beef and tasted only the rubbery resistance of protein between her teeth. She ate a sprig of cilantro and it was green cardboard.</p>

<p>"Is it okay?" Lena asked, already halfway through her own bowl. "You look like you've seen a ghost."</p>

<p>"It's good," Anna said, and continued eating, and did not mention that her mouth had become a room where flavour used to live and had moved out without leaving a forwarding address.</p>

<p>The ageusia did not go away. It spread across her entire palate like a fog rolling in from the lake, erasing one category of taste after another until eating became a mechanical act stripped of all pleasure, a chore performed solely to prevent the body from shutting down. She ate because she had to, the way a machine takes fuel, without joy, without anticipation, without the small daily miracle of a flavour landing on the tongue and triggering a cascade of memory and emotion. The Swiss chocolate that had tasted like a new beginning on her first day now tasted like wax. The coffee from the café near the lecture hall was hot brown water. The apples Lena left on the kitchen counter were cold, wet spheres with no identity.</p>

<p>Anna did not tell anyone. She did not go to a doctor, because going to a doctor would have required explaining that she had stopped tasting food, and explaining that would have required acknowledging that something inside her was broken, and acknowledging that would have meant admitting that the strength she had built her identity around was not an infinite resource but a battery that had been draining since September and was now, by the middle of November, running on nothing but habit and the fear of what would happen when it finally died.</p>

<p>She ate less. Then less. Then barely at all, because eating without taste was like speaking without sound, a motion that performed the shape of the thing it was supposed to be while delivering none of its substance. Lena noticed. Lena always noticed, because Lena was a literature student who paid attention to details the way Anna paid attention to music, instinctively and without being able to stop.</p>

<p>"Anna, you've lost weight."</p>

<p>"I've been busy. I forget to eat sometimes."</p>

<p>"That's not the same thing as eating and not tasting anything."</p>

<p>Anna looked at her roommate sharply. Lena held up both hands.</p>

<p>"I saw you eat the chocolate I bought you yesterday. You chewed it like it was medicine. The old Anna would have closed her eyes and made that little humming sound she makes when something tastes good. You haven't made that sound in weeks."</p>

<p>Anna said nothing, because there was nothing to say that was not either a lie or a confession, and she was too tired for either.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The third month was when the floor gave way.</p>

<p>It happened on a Tuesday, which was an ordinary day, because breakdowns do not wait for significant dates or dramatic settings. Anna had received an email from the programme coordinator informing her that her group project submission had been flagged for "concerns about academic integrity," specifically regarding the sections she had written. Margaux, it appeared, had raised the concern, suggesting that Anna's English was "suspiciously fluent for someone of her background" and that certain passages might have been produced with the assistance of translation software or by someone else entirely.</p>

<p>The accusation was false. Every word was her own, earned through years of disciplined study. But the accusation did not need to be true to do damage. It only needed to exist.</p>

<p>Anna sat at her desk, read the email three times, and then went to the bathroom and turned on the water, because water made noise and noise covered the sounds that a girl makes when she is coming apart at the seams and does not want her roommate to hear. She sat on the cold tile floor with her back against the wall and her knees drawn up to her chest and she cried, properly, fully, the kind of crying that does not stop when the tears run out but continues in dry, heaving convulsions that feel like the body is trying to expel something lodged too deep to reach.</p>

<p>She picked up her phone. Her thumb hovered over a name she had not opened in three months, a contact whose last message read <em>"Fine. Go then."</em> Two words. Fourteen letters. The entire architecture of two years of love demolished in a sentence shorter than most text messages.</p>

<p>She almost pressed call.</p>

<p>She didn't.</p>

<p>Because Anna Shcherbakova was a girl who had spent her entire life learning to be strong enough to stand alone, and calling Sasha now, broken and humiliated and tasteless in every sense of the word, felt like admitting that the strength she had built her identity around was not strength at all but merely the absence of anyone close enough to see her fall.</p>

<p>She put the phone down. She turned off the water. She went to bed with wet eyes and an empty stomach and a mouth that could no longer taste the salt of her own tears.</p>

<p>The next morning, Lena found her unconscious on the bathroom floor.</p>

<p>The ambulance came. The paramedics asked questions that Lena answered because Anna could not. Dehydration, malnutrition, acute stress response. They took her to Universitätsspital Zürich, where doctors attached an IV to her arm and a monitor to her chest and spoke to her in careful, clinical German that Lena translated into the kind of gentle English that people use when they are trying not to scare someone who is already terrified.</p>

<p>While Anna slept in a hospital bed under fluorescent lights that buzzed with the same indifferent frequency as every institution that does not care about the crises unfolding beneath its roof, Lena sat in the corridor and did the only thing she could think of that might actually help.</p>

<p>She opened Anna's phone.</p>

<p>She did not read the messages. She did not look at the photos. She scrolled through the contacts until she found the name that Anna murmured in her sleep sometimes, the name that Lena had heard through the thin wall of their shared apartment on nights when the water was running and the crying was loud enough to penetrate even Brahms, the name that Anna had never spoken aloud in waking hours but that her unconscious mind refused to surrender.</p>

<p><em>Sasha.</em></p>

<p>Lena pressed the call button.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Eight thousand kilometres away, in a Moscow apartment that was not hers, Sasha's phone rang.</p>

<p>She was in the kitchen making tea, a habit she had acquired from Makar, who believed that every problem in the world could be improved, if not solved, by the application of hot water to dried leaves. Makar himself was in the living room, watching a replay of last week's hockey match with the comfortable inattention of a man who already knew the score. It was a quiet evening, the kind of evening that Sasha had learned to fill with small routines and familiar sounds, because quiet evenings were the most dangerous ones, the ones where the silence left room for the memory of a voice that was not here and the ghost of a warmth that the tea could not replicate no matter how hot it was.</p>

<p>The number on the screen was Swiss. Sasha did not know anyone in Switzerland.</p>

<p>She answered anyway, because Sasha had always been the kind of person who answers, who opens the door, who picks up the phone, who moves toward the unknown rather than away from it, because standing still had never been an option for a girl who had built her entire career on the principle that forward motion was the only acceptable direction.</p>

<p>"Hello? Is this... Sasha? Alexandra Trusova?"</p>

<p>The voice was unfamiliar, young, speaking English with a German accent and the particular tightness of someone who has been crying recently and is trying very hard to sound composed.</p>

<p>"Yes. Who is this?"</p>

<p>"My name is Lena. I'm Anna's roommate. Anna Shcherbakova. I'm calling from Zurich."</p>

<p>The kitchen went very still. The kettle continued to heat on the stove, its low hum the only sound in a room that had, in the span of a single sentence, changed its entire atmospheric pressure.</p>

<p>"What happened," Sasha said, and it was not a question.</p>

<p>"She collapsed this morning. She's in the hospital now. She's stable, they say she'll be released in a day or two, but..." Lena paused, and in the pause Sasha could hear the sound of a hospital corridor, the squeak of rubber soles on linoleum, the distant beeping of a monitor that was counting someone's heartbeat in a city Sasha had never visited. "She hasn't been well for a long time. Weeks, maybe months. She stopped eating properly. She lost her sense of taste entirely, she can't taste anything at all, and she won't talk to anyone about it. She cries every night when she thinks I can't hear. And she..." Another pause, longer this time. "She says your name in her sleep. Every night. I thought you should know."</p>

<p>Sasha closed her eyes. Behind her eyelids she saw nothing, just darkness, the kind of darkness that exists inside a body that is processing something too large for the space it has been given. The kettle began to whistle. She did not move to take it off the stove. She stood in the kitchen with her phone pressed against her ear and her free hand gripping the counter so hard that the edge left a white line across her palm, and she listened to the sound of a German girl describing, in careful, frightened English, the slow disintegration of the person Sasha loved most in the world.</p>

<p>"What's the address," Sasha said.</p>

<p>Lena gave it to her. Bellevue, fourth floor, cobblestone street, delivery trucks at six in the morning. Sasha committed every detail to memory with the same instinctive precision she used to commit jump entries and footwork sequences, because this was a programme now, the most important programme of her life, and she would not miss a single element.</p>

<p>"Don't tell her I called you," Lena said. "She would never forgive me."</p>

<p>"I know." Sasha's voice was steady. Everything about her was steady, the way she was always steady when she had made a decision, because Alexandra Trusova did not deliberate, did not weigh options, did not construct elaborate cost-benefit analyses of the possible outcomes. She simply chose, and then she moved, and the world rearranged itself around her trajectory the way air rearranges itself around a body in rotation. "Thank you, Lena."</p>

<p>She hung up. She stood in the kitchen for exactly ten seconds, counting them the way she used to count the seconds before a jump, the small private ritual that separated the decision from the execution. On the stove, the kettle screamed. In the living room, the hockey commentator described a goal that Makar had already seen. Outside the window, Moscow's December sky was the colour of old iron, heavy with snow that had not yet decided to fall.</p>

<p>Sasha took the kettle off the stove. She poured the water into the sink. She put on her jacket, picked up her phone, and walked into the living room where Makar sat on the couch, his face illuminated by the television's blue glow, relaxed and unsuspecting and fundamentally decent in the way that people are decent when they have not yet been asked to compete with a ghost.</p>

<p>"Makar."</p>

<p>He looked up. Something in her voice, or perhaps in her posture, or perhaps in the way she was standing with her jacket already on and her jaw set in the particular angle that meant a decision had been made and was no longer open for discussion, caused him to sit up straighter and mute the television.</p>

<p>"What's wrong?"</p>

<p>Sasha looked at him. She looked at the apartment that was warm and the couch that was soft and the life she had built in the three months since Anna left, the life that was easy and uncomplicated and perfectly adequate for a girl who had decided to stop looking back. She looked at all of it, and she felt, with the absolute clarity that comes from being a person who has never once in her life confused comfort for love, that none of it was enough, had never been enough, would never be enough, because the girl she loved was in a hospital eight thousand kilometres away, unable to taste her own tears, and every second Sasha spent in this apartment was a second she was not spending in motion toward the only place in the world she actually needed to be.</p>

<p>"I'm going to see her."</p>

<p>Makar did not ask who. He did not need to. He had known, from the very first night Sasha stayed over, that there was a room inside her he would never be invited into, a room whose door was always slightly ajar and whose light was always on and whose occupant had a name that Sasha never said aloud but that lived in every silence between them the way a rest lives in a piece of music, invisible but structurally essential, holding the shape of everything around it.</p>

<p>He looked at her for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, and turned the television back on.</p>

<p>Sasha walked out the door.</p>
