<p align="center"><em>Allegretto giocoso con tempo di valse, poi Lento come prima e Smorzando</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Anna Shcherbakova had dressed for a thousand performances in her life, and so her hands knew what to do even on the mornings her heart did not. Foundation, then the eyes, then the hair pinned back with the severity of competition days, and last of all the smile, applied in the mirror with the same care as everything else, checked from three angles, adjusted at the corners, set. The dress was dove-gray and elegant and chosen weeks ago precisely because it asked for no attention. An Olympic champion learns early that there are days when you are the program, and days when you are the ice, and today, from the first bell to the last, Anna's job was to be the ice: smooth, bright, and holding everyone else up.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>She found the bride an hour before the ceremony, in a bright side room smelling of hairspray and lilies, where a small storm of bridesmaids had just been chased out by Sasha's mother, and Sasha's mother had then chased herself out to go cry somewhere with better acoustics, and so it was, for a little while, only the two of them and a mirror.</p>

<p>"Anya. Thank God. Come here, nobody in this building can work these buttons, Marina broke a nail and quit, my mother's hands are shaking, I told her, mama, you cried at my juniors medal, pace yourself, we have a whole day," and Sasha was turning her back to her even as she talked, sweeping the loose red-gold hair off her neck with one forearm, presenting the long white row of buttons that ran down her spine like a task designed by someone who wanted best friends to have something to do with their hands.</p>

<p>Anna's hands were, as it happened, famous for their steadiness.</p>

<p>She began at the top. The buttons were satin, small as baby teeth, each with its stubborn little loop, and she worked them one at a time while Sasha talked, because Sasha was incandescent today and the incandescent must talk or burst: about Makar's dream last night, in which he lost the rings in a fish tank; about the cake, which was two tiers larger than any reasonable cake; about her cousin who had flown in from Vladivostok with a baby that screamed in a perfect fifth, "I'm telling you, Anya, that child will be an opera singer or a fire alarm, there's no third option." The joy came off her in heat, fast and bright and demanding to be met, and Anna met it, laughed where the laughs belonged, supplied the answers on the beat that the day required. If her eyes went down to the buttons a half-second before each laugh finished, the mirror was busy with the bride, and had no chance to see it.</p>

<p>"Do you remember," Sasha said, tilting her head as Anna reached the buttons between her shoulder blades, "the training camps in Petersburg? You used to iron everything. Everything. I once caught you ironing socks. I decided that day you were either a genius or a serial killer."</p>

<p>The lamp swayed. A small hotel room, a speaker being angled just so, midnight leaning against the window. It came from nowhere, the way such things always came, invited by nothing but a city's name, and it flickered once behind Anna's eyes and was pressed down, smoothly, professionally, while her fingers closed the next loop without missing their rhythm.</p>

<p>"The socks were a special case," Anna said. "You had a competition."</p>

<p>"I had a competition." Sasha laughed at the ceiling. "You know what I remember about those camps? Sleeping like the dead. I've never slept like that since. Something about knowing you were across the room, probably. Like having a guard dog who also does laundry."</p>

<p>"I'm putting that on my résumé."</p>

<p>"Put it in your toast and I'll kill you."</p>

<p>The last button closed at the small of her back. Anna smoothed the satin flat with both palms, once, the way you press a page you have finished writing, and then Sasha turned around, and there she was: white from throat to floor, flushed, lit from inside, twenty-six years of fearlessness poured into a shape lace could hold, and for a moment neither of them said anything at all.</p>

<p>"Honestly," Sasha said, and her voice dropped out of its brightness into something smaller, the register she used maybe once a year. "Tell me honestly, Anya. How do I look?"</p>

<p>Anna looked at her. She took her time, because Sasha had asked honestly, and honesty was the one gift she could still afford, and she gave it whole: "Like the best thing that will ever happen to him. And he will know it every day for the rest of his life, because if he ever forgets, I'll remind him personally."</p>

<p>Sasha's eyes went bright and dangerous. "Don't. Don't, the makeup took an hour, don't you dare." She laughed it back, fanning her face, and grabbed the veil off its hook. "Here. You do it. Your hands don't shake."</p>

<p>So Anna pinned the veil, one pin at a time, into hair she could have navigated blind, and settled the cloud of it over the bare shoulders, and if her fingers rested there half a breath after the work was finished, well, the mirror only showed a maid of honor making everything perfect, which was, after all, her entire job today.</p>

<p>"There," Anna said. "Done. Go get married, Trusova."</p>

<p>"Ignatova," Sasha corrected, radiant, trying the word on the way she used to try new jumps, hungry for the landing. "God. In an hour it'll be Ignatova. Can you imagine?"</p>

<p>Anna smiled, and picked a stray thread off the white satin, and did not say that she could. That was the whole trouble. That she had imagined it, in exact detail, in her sleep, months before Sasha herself had ever tried the word aloud.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The banquet hall on the river had filled by noon with more noise than Anna had heard in one room since Beijing. Makar's family alone could have staffed a small festival: uncles laughing from the diaphragm, aunts in competing perfumes, a grandmother directing the seating chart with her cane like a woman who had annexed territory before and would do it again. Skaters and coaches and half the federation moved through gold light, over white tablecloths, along a white runner laid down the aisle like a sheet of fresh ice no one had skated yet.</p>

<p>Anna moved through the afternoon the way she moved through short programs: element to element, clean. When Makar's mother seized both her hands and cried that the whole family had watched her win her gold, Anna laughed and answered warmly, and her thumb, out of anyone's sight, pressed a small circle into her own palm, once, the way she did in the last minute before taking the ice. When two junior girls ambushed her for photographs, she crouched to their height, teased them about their edges, straightened one of their collar bows with two fingers, and let them run off shrieking to show the picture to their mothers, and only the empty air in front of her, for a quarter of a second before the next guest arrived, saw the smile settle into something that had to be lifted again by hand.</p>

<p>Nobody caught it. Weddings are generous that way: everyone is too busy being happy to audit anyone else's happiness.</p>

<p>The ceremony was brief. Anna sat in the front row with her hands folded, present, composed, entirely fine, and then the registrar smiled at the couple and pronounced, for the first time in the eyes of the state, the names of the married: Makar Ignatov. Alexandra Ignatova.</p>

<p>She had not braced. That was the mistake; she had braced for the rings, the kiss, the applause, all the obvious artillery, and the name came through the one gap in the wall. Alexandra Ignatova, and before Anna's reason could stand up, the candlelit hall was there, uninvited, laid over the daylight like a second exposure: chandeliers that had never existed, a gown of white and diamonds, a serenade she had been forbidden to remember, her own sleeping mind pronouncing this exact name months in advance like a sentence passed in a closed courtroom. She put it down. She was practiced at putting it down; it was, by now, a discipline, like stretching. She fixed her eyes on the real hall, the real light, the real Sasha laughing at something Makar whispered, and she applauded with the room, in tempo, and if her hands came together a fraction harder than applause requires, the sound of two hundred other pairs was there to cover it, and did.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>They gave her the microphone after the first course, because she was the best friend, and the best friend speaks.</p>

<p>Anna stood, and the hall turned toward her the way arenas had turned toward her for a decade, and she felt the old settling that comes before a free skate, the breath that starts the body's clock. She raised her glass.</p>

<p>"When I met Sasha, we were girls at Khrustalny, and everyone told me to watch out for her." A ripple of laughter, warm, already on her side; the room wanted to laugh today and would accept any invitation. "They said she was reckless. They were wrong, and it took me years to understand how wrong. Sasha has never been reckless a day in her life. She is fearless, and those are different things. Reckless people don't look before they leap. Sasha looks, sees exactly how impossible the thing is, and leaps because it is impossible. Five quads in one program, when the whole world said the air wouldn't hold her."</p>

<p>She paused, and let the hall murmur its agreement, and did not say: I have kept that observation in my chest for ten years like a jewel in a drawer, and I am giving it away today, in public, in a toast, because a drawer is no place for a jewel and I have run out of private uses for it.</p>

<p>"She told me once that she could never skate to my kind of music. Too quiet, she said. Sasha needs music with dopamine in it. Loud enough to shake the walls, or it doesn't count." She turned to Makar then, big, beaming Makar in his good suit, flushed with happiness and champagne, and raised her glass one degree higher. "So, Makar. You heard her. Be loud. Be what shakes her walls, every day, for the rest of your lives. She'll settle for nothing quieter."</p>

<p>The hall came to its feet, glasses catching the chandelier light, someone whistling from the federation table, Makar's uncles roaring, and before the noise had crested Sasha was already up and around the table and had Anna in both arms, wedding dress and all, laughing into her shoulder, smelling of hairspray and champagne and, underneath it, of herself.</p>

<p>"You," Sasha said, muffled, delighted, "are a menace. I told you not to make me cry."</p>

<p>"I was contractually obligated." Anna held on, and laughed, warm, full volume, exactly the loudness the moment demanded of her, and the hall was applauding them both now, best friends embracing, the oldest and safest image a wedding owns, and into that bright unanimous noise Anna said, "Congratulations, Sasha Trusova."</p>

<p>A half beat.</p>

<p>"Ignatova," she corrected herself, pulling back with a small laugh, one hand pressed to her own sternum in mock horror, "Ignatova, forgive me, two hours and I'm already behind the times," and the nearest tables roared, because it is the oldest joke of every wedding day, everyone stumbles on the new name, an uncle bellowed that he'd been married thirty years and still got it wrong, and Sasha laughed hardest of all, thrilled, correcting her with a wagging finger, Ig-na-to-va, and the moment sailed on, ratified by all that laughter, deniable, delightful, gone.</p>

<p>Anna let go of the embrace half a breath before Sasha did. No one measures such things at weddings. She stepped back into the applause, lifted her glass to the couple, and sat, and her thumb pressed one small circle into her palm under the tablecloth, and the hall moved on to the cake.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Then the lights lowered, and the band took their places, and the floor was cleared, and the first dance began.</p>

<p>Anna had promised herself she would watch it the way everyone else watched it. She lasted four bars.</p>

<p>It was not that she chose to remember. It was that the scene refused to stay single: Makar led Sasha out under the chandeliers into the cleared center, every table turning its chairs, and over the daylight-real hall the other one flickered, unbidden, the candlelit one, the forbidden one, laid across it like a reflection on glass that will not move no matter how you angle your head. A crowded hall. Sasha in white. A floor emptied for two people. Anna blinked, and fixed her eyes on the actual chandeliers, the actual dress, and the reflection stayed, because she had dreamed it from the inside, and the inside does not blink away.</p>

<p>Forward, back, and then a spin. They moved as if they were one.</p>

<p>Anna sat with her spine straight and her hands folded and watched. Makar was a good dancer, better than a big man had any right to be; Sasha laughed up at him as they turned, her skirt flaring white under the lights. And the count began in Anna's head without her permission, left, right, right, left, one step forward, two steps back, the old count, the dreamed count, arriving on its own the way a tune you hate arrives when someone whistles the first bar. She tried once, honestly, to stop it. Then she gave that up and simply endured it, each step called half a beat early in the silence of her skull and each step landing on schedule, and there is no lonelier way to watch a dance than that: seated, composed, knowing every figure before the floor does, and unable to be wrong even once.</p>

<p>The waltz brightened and quickened, and the two of them quickened with it. Anna watched Sasha's fingers, somewhere in the second turn, weave themselves through Makar's without either of them acknowledging it, the small unconscious lacing that turns a hold into a belonging. She watched him draw her in on the next phrase, close, closer, close enough that two pulses meet through joined palms. She knew that distance. She had stood inside it once, in a hotel room above the Neva, with a lamp swaying and the winter at the window, and she put the room down, again, and it rose, again, patient as water finding a crack, while her face watched the dance with the pleasant open warmth of a woman enjoying her best friend's wedding, which she was; that was the trick nobody warns you about, that you can be enjoying it, truly, and drowning, truly, in the same held breath.</p>

<p>And then, as the music gathered toward its final phrases, Sasha closed her eyes and breathed.</p>

<p>One slow inhale, mid-turn, her face lifting, her whole frame softening for a moment into the hands that held her, and the hall saw a bride dissolved in happiness. Anna saw a tense girl in a hotel room, years ago, letting go of her armor one bar at a time, unlocking under a quiet melody she had sworn was too boring to dance to. That breath had a birthplace. Anna had held the hand that taught it. And here it was, given away entire, easily, to a man who would never know it was secondhand, by a woman who did not know it either, and the only person in the hall who knew its origin was sitting at the head table with her hands folded, being fine.</p>

<p>Makar dipped her, at the final chord, low and sure, catching her with his left arm, exactly there, exactly so, and Anna looked down and discovered her own hands already together, already moving, the first clap in the hall, a full beat ahead of everyone, before the band had even released the couple. The room erupted around her half a second later and buried the evidence. No one had noticed the early entrance. No one else had dreamed it.</p>

<p><em>Let's just say the best friend ought to lead the applause.</em></p>

<p>The newlyweds straightened, flushed and laughing, forehead to forehead in the storm of noise, and their entire universe had shrunk down to the reflection in each other's eyes. Anna could see it from the head table, the way you can see a lit window from a dark street: the warmth, the smallness, the completeness, the door.</p>

<p>She clapped until her palms hurt, and meant it, and that was the strangest part of all.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>She left before the bouquet.</p>

<p>Not rudely; champions know how to exit a rink. A word with Sasha's mother, a kiss on the bride's cheek, Makar's enormous hands folding hers as he promised, solemnly, drunkenly, delightedly, to be loud, and then the tall doors, and the cloakroom, and her coat. Behind her the band had struck up something fast, and the hall's brightness followed her down the corridor as sound: the full roar of it at the doors, then a warm blur through the walls, then, past the outer doors, only the lowest thread of it, cheerful and persistent, like a party remembered rather than heard, and then, at the bottom of the steps, not even that.</p>

<p>The Moscow night received her without comment.</p>

<p>She walked. The cold worked its way through the dove-gray dress and she let it, her heels striking the frozen pavement in a slow, even, unaccompanied rhythm, all the way down to the empty embankment. Somewhere behind her, in a hall full of gold light, the celebration went on at full brightness, and would go on for hours, and did not need her, and never had. It worked perfectly, she thought, without her; halls like that proved it nightly, all over the world, to one guest at a time.</p>

<p>Above her, a crescent moon embraced the streets of Moscow with its tenderness, while the winds carried the crisp, chilling breath of the deepening winter. In the northern sky of the ancient city, tiny constellations cast faint glimmers of light, trying to escape the monotony of the night. Those specks of light lingered in her hazel eyes, as they had lingered on the eve of a wedding, as they had lingered over the Neva years ago, still seeking a place to hide, a sanctuary for a young woman's smallest, unspoken desires.</p>

<p>They were still looking. They had been looking for years. Anna walked beneath them with her collar up and her hands in her pockets, and she could have told them, by now, that the sky was very large and very old, and that there was no such place; and that a person could live anyway. Could iron the socks, and pin the veils, and fasten forty small buttons down a white spine with steady hands; could toast, and laugh, and lead the applause; could carry a thing with no ending through an entire city of lit windows, for years, in the cold, in good order, and never once set it down.</p>

<p>Her heels kept their slow time on the stone, fainter and fainter along the river, until the night held only the last of them, and then their echo, and then the cold.</p>

<p><em>Lento, come prima.</em></p>

<p><em>E senza fine.</em></p>
