<p align="center"><em>Furioso e agitato, poi cedendo in pianissimo</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Silence.</p>

<p>Not the comfortable kind. The kind that follows a question nobody wants to answer, the kind that fills a room the way smoke fills a jar, pressing against every surface until there's no air left for anything else.</p>

<p>Sasha's words hung between them. <em>Who exactly were you performing for, Anna?</em> The accusation sat on Anna's chest heavier than Sasha's body pinning her to the sofa, heavier than the blindfold, heavier than every bite mark throbbing on her skin. For several seconds the only sound was their breathing, Sasha's fast and ragged above her, Anna's shallow and uneven beneath.</p>

<p>Then Anna spoke.</p>

<p>"Take off the blindfold."</p>

<p>Her voice came out low and stripped of everything that usually padded it, the composure, the measured calm, the careful modulation that made lesser people assume Anna Shcherbakova was incapable of genuine anger. This was not that voice. This was the voice beneath the voice, the one that existed only when every other layer had been bitten through.</p>

<p>"Sasha. Take it off. Now."</p>

<p>Sasha hesitated. One second, which was one second longer than she had hesitated about anything all evening. Then she reached behind Anna's head and pulled the knot loose, letting the black fabric fall between them.</p>

<p>Anna's eyes met hers, and Sasha felt every gram of confidence she had built over the past hour evaporate like breath on cold glass. The hazel eyes were red. Not beautifully red, not cinematically red. The ugly, swollen red of someone who has been crying behind a piece of cloth while the person she loves tore into her skin and called it a lesson. And they were looking at Sasha with an expression that was furious and devastated and so completely, mercilessly honest that Sasha wanted to look away but couldn't, because she owed Anna this. She owed her the discomfort of seeing exactly what she had done.</p>

<p>"You want to know who I was performing for?" Anna said. Her wrists were still above her head where Sasha had pinned them, but Sasha's grip had loosened, and Anna made no move to pull free, because the restraint was no longer physical. It was the weight of what she was about to say. "You. It was always you, you absolute idiot."</p>

<p>"Anna..."</p>

<p>"Semenenko asked me to do a pair routine for the ice show gala. A charity event, Sasha. For children's hospitals. He needed a partner who could match his skating level, and he asked me because I was available, and I said yes because it was for sick kids, and at no point during any of it did I think about him the way I think about you, because I don't think about anyone the way I think about you, because you have ruined me for every other human being on this planet and you don't even have the decency to know it."</p>

<p>The words came out fast, run together, the verbal equivalent of a jump combination where each element launches from the landing of the previous one with no pause between. Sasha opened her mouth to respond, something sharp, something defensive, but Anna was not finished.</p>

<p>"But you didn't ask me, did you? You didn't walk over after the show and say 'Hey, what was that about?' You didn't send a message. You didn't even give me one of your jealous looks, the one where your nostrils flare and you stare at me like you're trying to set me on fire with your eyes, which, by the way, I always notice, and which..." Anna's voice faltered for half a beat before steadying. "The point is, you decided to build the whole trial by yourself, convict me without evidence, and sentence me to a bunny suit and a blindfold and your teeth. In what universe is that a reasonable response to a charity ice show?"</p>

<p>Sasha stared at her. The sofa was very quiet. The bite marks on Anna's shoulder were already darkening, angry red crescents against pale skin, and her chestnut hair had come loose from the ponytail and was falling across her face in strands that stuck to the tear tracks on her cheeks. The bunny ears had tilted sideways, giving her the look of a rabbit that had survived something terrible and was now giving its predator a very calm, very thorough piece of its mind.</p>

<p>"A charity event," Sasha repeated. Her voice had lost all its edges.</p>

<p>"For children's hospitals."</p>

<p>"And you said yes because..."</p>

<p>"Because sick children, Sasha. Sick. Children."</p>

<p>Silence again. But different this time, not the smoke-in-a-jar kind but the kind that comes after something has been punctured and the pressure is escaping, a slow, deflating quiet that left room for the smaller, truer feelings that had been compressed beneath the jealousy all evening.</p>

<p>Sasha sat back. Her hands released Anna's wrists entirely, falling to her own lap, where they lay open and empty. Without the momentum of anger, without the righteous fury that had powered every bite and every accusation, she looked suddenly smaller, younger, like a girl who had built an elaborate fortress out of rage and was now standing in the wreckage of it, holding nothing but the blueprint.</p>

<p>"I saw the photos," Sasha said, and her voice cracked on the second word in a way that she would have been mortified by five minutes ago but that she no longer had the energy to hide. "Someone posted them online. You were smiling at him. That smile, Anna. The one you do with your eyes. The one where your whole face changes and you look like..." She stopped. Swallowed. "You looked like you were happy."</p>

<p>"I was performing, Sasha. It's what I do. It's what we both do. We step onto ice and we make people believe things that aren't real. That's literally our profession."</p>

<p>"I know that."</p>

<p>"Then why..."</p>

<p>"Because I can never tell." The confession came out quiet, almost inaudible, pushed through teeth that were clenched against the tremor in Sasha's jaw. "That's the thing, Anna. I can never tell with you. You're so good at it, so good at making everything look real, that sometimes I watch you perform and I think, what if she's this good with me too? What if the way she looks at me is just another programme she's running, another score she's chasing, another smile she can turn on and off because she learned how to do it before she learned how to do anything else?"</p>

<p>The words fell into the space between them and landed with the weight of something that had been carried for much longer than one evening. This was not about Semenenko. It had never been about Semenenko. Semenenko was the surface, the visible crack in the ice that you point to when you don't want to talk about the structural failure underneath.</p>

<p>The structural failure was this: Sasha loved a woman who was the best performer she had ever seen, and she could never be completely certain where the performance ended and the person began, and that uncertainty was a splinter she had been carrying for so long that it had become part of the architecture of her love, inseparable from it, aching only when pressed.</p>

<p>Anna looked at Sasha for a long time. The anger had not left her face, but it had been joined by something else, something softer and more complicated, the expression of a person who has just been handed the real reason behind a punishment that seemed senseless and has discovered that the real reason is heartbreaking.</p>

<p>She reached up with hands that were still shaking from the evening's ordeal and took Sasha's face between her palms. Her fingers were cold, the way fingers get when the blood has been redirected by adrenaline and fear, and she held Sasha's face the way she held everything that mattered to her: firmly, precisely, with the quiet authority of someone who has decided that this particular thing is not going to fall.</p>

<p>"Look at me."</p>

<p>Sasha looked. Her blue eyes were wet, which was something Anna had seen perhaps twice in all the years she had known her, because Alexandra Trusova did not cry, Alexandra Trusova jumped quads and dyed her hair red and attacked the world with the blunt confidence of someone who had never met an obstacle she couldn't throw her body at, and crying was the one thing her body did not know how to do gracefully.</p>

<p>"This," Anna said, and her thumbs brushed the tears from Sasha's cheeks with the same careful precision she brought to everything, "is not a performance. This is me, sitting on your sofa in a bunny suit with bite marks all over my body, crying, holding your stupid face, and telling you that I love you so much it makes me feel insane. There is no programme for this. There is no score. There is no audience. There is just you and me and this ridiculous outfit and the fact that you owe me a week of free meals and I intend to collect every single one."</p>

<p>Sasha stared at her. The bunny ears were now hanging completely off one side of Anna's head. The fishnet stockings had bunched at her knees. The bite marks formed a constellation across her shoulder and collarbone that would take days to fade. She looked absurd. She looked wrecked. She looked like the most beautiful thing Sasha had ever seen, and the combination of all three made something in Sasha's chest crack open with a sound so quiet that only the person holding her face could have heard it.</p>

<p>"I'm sorry," Sasha whispered. The words came out rough and graceless, nothing like the smooth, teasing voice she had used all evening. "I saw the photos and I just... I couldn't think. I could only feel, and what I felt was so ugly that I wanted to put it on your skin so you could feel it too, and that's..."</p>

<p>"Stupid."</p>

<p>"Yeah."</p>

<p>"And painful."</p>

<p>"Yeah."</p>

<p>"And you're going to do it again within a month."</p>

<p>"...Probably."</p>

<p>"And when you do, you're going to use your words instead of your teeth."</p>

<p>Sasha nodded, the motion small and shaky beneath Anna's palms.</p>

<p>"Good." Anna leaned forward and kissed Sasha's forehead, once, with the gentle finality of someone pressing a seal into warm wax. "Now take this stupid outfit off me before I lose all circulation in my legs."</p>

<p>"Can I keep the ears?"</p>

<p>"Absolutely not."</p>

<p>"But you look so cute in..."</p>

<p>"Sasha."</p>

<p>"Fine."</p>

<p>Sasha reached behind Anna's head and unclipped the bunny ears, and they fell onto the sofa between them like a prop from a play that had run its course. Anna pulled the stockings off with the brisk efficiency of a woman who has been patient long enough, and Sasha helped her out of the bodysuit without commentary, her hands careful now, gentle in the way they should have been from the beginning, fingers avoiding the bite marks with a tenderness that was its own kind of apology.</p>

<p>When it was done, Anna sat on the sofa in nothing but her own clothes, the bite marks visible above her collar, her hair loose and tangled, her face still blotchy from crying. Sasha sat beside her, shoulders touching, looking at the bunny costume crumpled on the floor with the expression of someone reviewing the wreckage of a decision they are already deeply embarrassed by.</p>

<p>"For the record," Anna said, leaning her head against Sasha's shoulder, "the smile you saw in those photos? The one with my eyes? I do that when I'm thinking about you. Semenenko just happened to be standing in the frame."</p>

<p>Sasha turned to look at her. Then she buried her face in Anna's hair and laughed, a short, broken, honest sound that had nothing performative in it at all.</p>

<p>"I love you," Sasha said into Anna's hair. "And I'm sorry. And you're getting two weeks of free meals. Rose's Palace. Whatever you want."</p>

<p>"Three weeks."</p>

<p>"Anna..."</p>

<p>"Three weeks, or I'm keeping the photos of you buying a bunny suit at the store and I'm posting them everywhere."</p>

<p>"...Three weeks."</p>

<p>Anna smiled. The real one. The one with her eyes.</p>
