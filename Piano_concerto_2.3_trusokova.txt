<p align="center"><em>Allegro, con gusto</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The delivery trucks came down the narrow street at six, the way they came every morning, and for the first time since October, Anna was awake before them.</p>

<p>She heard them from the kitchen, where she sat with coffee and half a roll, and she ate the roll, all of it, for the texture and the warmth and the small stubborn principle of the thing, and when the rumble of the trucks had passed under the window she realized she had been chewing in time with it, and smiled at herself, and finished the coffee. Some mornings announce themselves. This one had been circled on the calendar in red for six weeks, and it had arrived the way Sasha arrived: early, loud, and refusing to be dreaded.</p>

<p>Sasha herself was conducting breakfast. This involved four burners, two pans, a volume of narration the kitchen had never previously required, and a plate landing in front of Anna with syrniki stacked like medals.</p>

<p>"Eat. Both of them. You can't demolish a committee on half a roll."</p>

<p>"I'm not demolishing anyone. It's a review. There will be minutes taken."</p>

<p>"There will be minutes taken," Sasha agreed, dropping into the opposite chair with her own plate, "of the demolition. Now. One more time. The plan."</p>

<p>"I talk," Anna said. "You sit there."</p>

<p>"I sit there being famous," Sasha corrected. "It's a real job. Federation pays me for it and everything." She pointed her fork across the table. "And if any of them so much as leans toward you wrong?"</p>

<p>"You continue sitting there."</p>

<p>"Continuing to be famous. Extremely calm. In a chair." Sasha nodded, solemn as a general reviewing troops. "It's a good plan. I hate it. Eat your syrniki."</p>

<p>Lena emerged at half past eight in her pajamas, took in the war-room atmosphere, and returned three minutes later wearing real clothes and holding out a blazer, charcoal gray, sharp at the shoulders. "For luck," she said. "It's the one I defended my thesis proposal in. It has never lost." Then, at the door, with the terrible gentleness of a second at a duel: "Give them hell, Anya. Politely. In perfect English."</p>

<p>From behind her bedroom door, as they left, the violin struck up something that was almost, very nearly, a march.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The review was held in a seminar room on the third floor, with radiators ticking and a long table and three people behind it: the programme coordinator, a professor of linguistics Anna knew by his footnotes, and an administrator whose folder was very thick. Sasha took the visitor's chair by the wall, crossed one ankle over her knee, and became, instantly and completely, a photograph of serenity. Anna sat alone at the near side of the long table, where the accused sit, and folded her hands, and found, to her mild astonishment, that they were steady.</p>

<p>Three months ago she could not walk into a building unaccompanied. Somewhere between a doorstep and a spoonful of honey, that had changed, and she noted the change the way you note good ice: gratefully, briefly, and then you skate.</p>

<p>The coordinator cleared his throat and began the procedural preamble, concerns had been raised, the committee's role, no conclusions predetermined, and Anna listened politely until the word arrived, the one from the email, and met it head-on.</p>

<p>"Integrity," she said, "yes. I've read the file. May I begin?"</p>

<p>She laid it out the way she had once laid out competition programs: element by element, no wasted motion. The full revision history of her sections, every draft, every timestamp, four months of a document growing sentence by sentence at two in the morning, "which the committee will note is not how machines write, but is unfortunately exactly how I do." Her essays from October, from November, the same voice throughout, the same habits, the same overfondness for semicolons that Professor Brandt had marked twice in the margins. And then, because paper can only say so much, she took a pen from her bag and set it on the table, aligned with the edge, and looked up at the three of them.</p>

<p>"Or, if the committee prefers something faster: choose any subject. Any subject at all. I will sit here and write you five hundred words on it, longhand, while you watch. My English took me eleven years to build. It would be a shame if no one got to see it work."</p>

<p>The linguistics professor's mouth did something it had clearly been told not to do.</p>

<p>The coordinator shuffled paper. There was, he allowed, also the matter of context; the committee had received supplementary statements from members of the project group; one in particular, and here he adjusted his glasses, described a pattern of remarks directed at Miss Shcherbakova over the course of the semester which, if accurate, the university would take extremely seriously. He read two of the remarks aloud. In the quiet seminar room, in an official voice, stripped of their smiles, they sounded exactly like what they were.</p>

<p>Anna had wondered, walking here, how she would feel hearing it. Sofie, of all people. Sofie who had studied the table all semester and then, apparently, in the end, had written it all down. She kept her face still and her voice level.</p>

<p>"They're accurate," she said. "I can supply the dates."</p>

<p>It was here that Sasha spoke, for the first and only time, from her chair by the wall, in a tone of purest academic curiosity, the tone of a tourist asking about local customs.</p>

<p>"Forgive me, one small question. When a project is flagged like this, does the committee review every student whose written English is unusually good? Or only the ones with certain passports?" She smiled, warm as a stadium. "I ask because I travel a great deal for my work, and it's always so interesting, what different institutions consider suspicious."</p>

<p>Nobody answered her. Nobody had to. The administrator wrote something down, and the coordinator did not look at the wall again for the remainder of the session, and eleven minutes later the word withdrawn had been said twice, the word apologize once, and the thick folder was closed with the particular firmness of a door being shut by someone who wishes it had never been opened.</p>

<p>In the corridor afterward, Margaux was waiting for the group's debrief with Oliver beside her, and there was a moment, passing them, when the whole architecture of politeness stood balanced. Anna stopped. She looked at Margaux pleasantly, the review's verdict still warm in her bag, and said, in her eleven-year English, with her coach's daughter's diction:</p>

<p>"Your Russian must be excellent. For a linguist."</p>

<p>Then she walked on, and did not look back, and beside her Sasha made a sound like a kettle that has decided, magnanimously, not to scream.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>They celebrated with nothing, at first. That was the strange sweetness of the afternoon: there was nothing to do, no verdict to dread, no email sitting in the inbox like a stone, and so they walked home the long way, along the lake, where the winter light lay flat and silver on the water, and bought groceries like a household, arguing about dill.</p>

<p>The evening's spoon was scheduled for six o'clock, because Sasha ran the program like a season and the program did not take holidays. They had worked down the list all week: lemon, salt, a horrifying Alpine cheese that Sasha had eaten most of in the name of science. Zero, zero, zero. Anna reported each result like a lab assistant, and Sasha marked each one down in an actual notebook and was discouraged by none of them, and tonight, item seven, the notebook said, in Sasha's aggressive handwriting: chocolate.</p>

<p>The bar came down off the shelf where it had lived since September. One square gone; the wrapper soft with age. Sasha broke off a piece with ceremony, set it on the saucer, and slid it across the kitchen table.</p>

<p>"Eyes closed, Shcherbakova. You know the drill."</p>

<p>Anna closed her eyes and put the chocolate on her tongue, and waited for nothing, the way she had waited for nothing seven times this week, texture and temperature and the ghost of a name, and she was already composing the report, zero, mark it down, when, faint and far off and unmistakable, like a light coming on in a window across a valley, something answered.</p>

<p>Bitter. Then, behind the bitterness, opening slowly, sweet.</p>

<p>She sat very still with her eyes closed. Her throat closed before her mind had ruled on anything; the tears arrived first, ahead of every word she might have chosen, spilling warm down both cheeks while the small square dissolved on her tongue with its faint, impossible, returning voice, and across the table she heard Sasha's chair scrape, heard the beginning of alarm, "Anya? Hey, hey, what's, "</p>

<p>and then it came, up through the tears, without permission, without design: the hum. Low in the throat, barely a breath, the little sound she had never once made on purpose in her life.</p>

<p><em>Risolto.</em></p>

<p>Sasha stopped moving entirely. One second. Two. Then she came around the table in no seconds at all, and Anna was pulled up and in, saucer abandoned, chair toppling somewhere unimportant, two arms closing around her hard enough to hold a landing, and Anna sobbed into her shoulder, and laughed into it, both at once, indistinguishable, chocolate and salt now, both of them tasting of exactly what they were, and neither of them said a single word, because there was nothing to announce. The sound had already said it. Delivered, to the right address, after three months in the mail.</p>

<p>They stood in the kitchen a long time, swaying by slow degrees, while the radiator ticked and the chocolate wrapper sat open on the table like a letter finally read.</p>

<p>Dinner, when they eventually got to it, took three hours, because Anna insisted on tasting everything twice, and Sasha insisted on narrating anyway, out of tradition, and Lena, arriving home at nine to find the kitchen wrecked and both of them incandescent, assessed the scene with one slow look and said, "I heard nothing, I saw nothing, I am Switzerland," and took her dinner to her room, from which, shortly, the violin offered something that was, for four whole bars, unambiguously joyful.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The last week went by the way bright weeks do, at double speed. They ate the city one stall at a time, the market, the bakery with the propped-open door, the Glühwein stand where Anna took one sip and made the sound and a stranger in a red scarf smiled at her for no reason she would ever know. Flavor came back the way spring comes back, not all at once, patchily, one window lighting up at a time, and Sasha kept the notebook updated with the fanatical precision of a coach in a comeback season, and every evening the list of zeroes grew shorter.</p>

<p>Then the suitcase, which had stood unremarked in the hallway for three weeks, was standing at the airport, and the week was over.</p>

<p>They had done the practical talk on the drive: the training schedule, the visa dates, the tickets in March. At the mouth of security, under the departure board's soft clatter, all of it ran out at once, and there was only the ordinary impossible fact of the barrier, and Sasha with her boarding pass in her teeth while she rearranged her backpack, entirely unbothered, entirely herself, and Anna, watching her, felt the question climb into her throat and stall there, three months of borrowed courage suddenly nowhere to be found.</p>

<p>"Sasha."</p>

<p>"Mm." The boarding pass came out of her teeth.</p>

<p>"In June, the programme ends." Anna looked at the departure board, which required no looking at. Her ears were going warm, and she was twenty-two years old and an Olympic champion and could not, apparently, survive one sentence. "And I thought. After. If you wanted. We could, the apartment in Moscow, or here, or anywhere, I don't care where, I just." She surrendered, quietly, to the smallest version: "Wait for me to finish. And then come live with me. Properly. For good."</p>

<p>For a moment Sasha said nothing at all, and Anna made herself lift her eyes from the board, braced for teasing.</p>

<p>What happened instead was flight. The backpack hit the floor, and two hands closed on Anna's waist, and the airport tilted as Sasha lifted her clean off the ground and spun her, once, in front of security and the departure board and a hundred strangers with their sensible luggage, laughing up at her with her whole face, holding her in the air the way she held nothing else in the world.</p>

<p>"Then I'll be waiting for that day," Sasha said.</p>

<p>She set her down. She kissed her, brief and sure, picked up the backpack, walked backward through the barrier gesturing largely about texting from the gate, collided gently with a bollard, recovered with an athlete's dignity, and was gone behind the glass.</p>

<p>Anna stood a moment in the concourse, lighter than the building's air.</p>

<p>Then she took out her phone, opened a message thread three months deep, and typed, with steady hands, the whole of it, all the way to the end, every word finished this time:</p>

<p><em>I made it. The lake is beautiful. I wish you were here.</em></p>

<p>Send.</p>

<p>She was still smiling at the screen when it buzzed against her palm, one word, from the far side of the glass:</p>

<p><em>Soon.</em></p>

<p align="center"><em>Fine.</em></p>
