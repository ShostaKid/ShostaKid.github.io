<p align="center"><b>Fugue</b></p>
<p align="center"><em>Allegretto e sotto voce, poco a poco dolce</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>
 
<p>When Sasha pulled away, winter rushed back in.</p>
 
<p>Not all at once, because nothing about this evening had been sudden, but in the way cold returns when a door has been left open for just a moment too long: first at the edges, then along the skin, then settling into the small spaces between two people who are no longer touching. The absence of Sasha's lips left a faint, stinging chill on Anna's mouth, and the smaller girl pressed her lips together as though trying to seal something precious inside before it could escape into the draught coming through the old window frame.</p>
 
<p>Sasha said nothing. She simply turned back to the notebook, as though the kiss had been no more than a pause between sentences, a rest written into the score that she had played exactly as marked. The pen found its way back into her grip, and Anna watched those fingers close around it the same way they closed around everything: quickly, firmly, with the confidence of someone who had spent her entire life learning to hold on to things that spun.</p>
 
<p>Anna watched her write.</p>
 
<p>She had always loved watching Sasha's hands, ever since that morning at Khrustalny years ago when she had first noticed them wrapping tape around their own knuckles with a carelessness that was, she now understood, not carelessness at all but a kind of faith. Those hands were not elegant the way a pianist's hands are elegant; they were strong and squared at the knuckles, the nails bitten short, the fingers scarred in places where blades had nicked them during falls that would have ended anyone else's season. Yet the script they produced, messy as it was, had a forward momentum that reminded Anna of the way Sasha moved on ice: always leaning into the next moment, never lingering on the one behind.</p>
 
<p>"You're not going to let me see?" Anna murmured, settling her chin onto Sasha's shoulder. She could feel the muscles shifting there with each stroke of the pen, small rolling movements beneath the thin cotton of Sasha's t-shirt, and she let herself register every one of them the way she might register the individual notes inside a chord, each one distinct, each one necessary.</p>
 
<p>"When it's done."</p>
 
<p>"That's not fair. I'm the one who made you write in the first place."</p>
 
<p>"Exactly. You don't get to read the result until it's finished. That's the deal."</p>
 
<p>"There was no deal, Sasha."</p>
 
<p>"There is now."</p>
 
<p>Anna fell quiet, not because she accepted the logic but because something in the tone of Sasha's voice, low and focused in a way she rarely heard from the girl who was usually the loudest person in any room, told her that this moment was not one to interrupt. Outside, the wind shifted, and the low murmur against the windowpane softened into something thinner, steadier, almost like breathing. The lamp in the corner of the room flickered once and then held, casting their combined shadow across the far wall: one shape, two heads leaning close, indistinguishable.</p>
 
<p>Anna pressed closer, not to read what was being written but to listen to the act of writing itself. The scratch of pen on paper had its own rhythm, a tempo she had not expected from someone who moved through life at allegro or faster. The strokes came in quick clusters separated by pauses just long enough for thought, and in those pauses Anna could hear Sasha breathing, could feel the slight tension in her shoulder that meant she was searching for a word, could sense the exact moment when the word arrived because Sasha's whole body would ease, just fractionally, like a skater finding their centre of gravity after a shaky landing.</p>
 
<p>It was, Anna realized, the first time she had ever seen Sasha create something slowly. Everything about Alexandra Trusova was built for speed: her jumps, her decisions, her laugh, the way she fell in love, the way she texted at three in the morning without checking for typos. But here, in the circle of lamplight with winter pressing against the windows and Anna's heartbeat pressed against her arm, Sasha was taking her time, and the care she was giving to each sentence was so unlike her usual restless velocity that Anna felt something shift behind her ribs, something that ached the way a bruise aches when you press it, a pain you seek out because it proves something is real.</p>
 
<p>"Your hand is shaking," Anna whispered.</p>
 
<p>"It's cold."</p>
 
<p>"The heater is right there, Sasha."</p>
 
<p>"Then it's your fault for breathing on my neck."</p>
 
<p>Anna smiled against Sasha's shoulder but did not move away, and Sasha did not ask her to, and the pen continued its quiet, careful work across the page. The pauses between strokes grew longer as the writing progressed, as though each new sentence cost more than the last, but the lines themselves grew steadier, more deliberate, the handwriting smoothing from its usual chaotic scrawl into something that approached, if not elegance, then at least a kind of honest attention.</p>
 
<p>The heater hummed. The wind sighed. The lamp held its small, warm circle around them like a spotlight on an empty rink, and Anna let her eyes close, let the rhythm of pen on paper carry her to that place between waking and sleep where every sound becomes music and every warmth becomes a person. Sasha's shoulder was firm beneath her cheek, and the muscles there moved in a slow, steady pattern, and Anna thought: <em>this is what it sounds like, to be written about by someone who doesn't know she's writing a love letter.</em></p>
 
<p>She did not know how long she drifted. Minutes, perhaps, or the length of one long, sustained note. When she surfaced, the scratching had stopped. The room was quieter than it had been all evening, so quiet she could hear the pen rolling slowly across the notebook's open spine.</p>
 
<p>Sasha's hand rested flat on the page, palm down, as if protecting what was underneath.</p>
 
<p>"Finished?" Anna asked, her voice soft from the edge of sleep.</p>
 
<p>"…I don't know." The admission came out in a tone Anna had almost never heard from Sasha, uncertain and unguarded, stripped of the bravado that the world had come to expect from the girl who once told a journalist she wanted to compete against the men. "I've never written anything like this. I don't know if it's done or if it's just… all I have."</p>
 
<p>"Then let me see."</p>
 
<p>A silence stretched between them, long enough for the heater to cycle through one full breath of warm air. Then Sasha lifted her hand from the page and slid the notebook toward Anna, carefully, the way one might slide something across a table when the contents are both a gift and a confession.</p>
 
<p>Anna sat up. She lifted the notebook and tilted it toward the lamplight, her hazel eyes scanning the page. The handwriting was still Sasha's, still forward-leaning and slightly too large, but the lines were spaced with an unfamiliar care, each one given room, as though Sasha had understood instinctively that these words needed space around them to breathe.</p>
 
<p>She read.</p>
 
<p>It was not long. There was no grand flourish, no sweeping metaphor, nothing that resembled the lyrical programs Anna built for herself each season. The sentences were blunt and plain, the way Sasha talked when she forgot to perform for the cameras. They spoke of a rink in early morning, before anyone else arrived, when the ice was smooth and untouched and the cold air tasted like metal and silence. Of a sound that was not music but might as well have been, the clean ring of a single blade cutting a curve into fresh ice. Of hands that were too small to belong to someone so dangerous, and yet held on tighter than anyone else ever had. Of the colour hazel, which was not brown and not gold but something in between that changed depending on the light, and how one particular pair of hazel eyes was the only thing that had ever made the world seem worth slowing down for.</p>
 
<p>The last line read: <em>"Winter tastes like you, and I don't have the right words for why. But I think you already know."</em></p>
 
<p>Anna held the notebook for a long time after she had finished reading. Her fingers were trembling, and not from the cold that seeped through the window, not from the late hour, not from the fatigue of a training day. Something behind her ribs felt too large for the space it occupied, as though her chest were a room that had suddenly been asked to hold the entire sky.</p>
 
<p>"It's not very good," Sasha said quietly, her eyes fixed on the window where frost was drawing its own slow patterns across the glass. "The sentences are too short. It doesn't flow the way your programs flow. It's just—"</p>
 
<p>"Shut up."</p>
 
<p>Sasha turned, and whatever she saw in Anna's face made every word in her throat go silent. Anna's hazel eyes were bright, not with tears, not exactly, but with something that lived in the same neighbourhood as tears, something that caught the lamplight and scattered it the way ice catches sunlight and throws it in directions no one can predict.</p>
 
<p>"It's the most beautiful thing anyone has ever given me," Anna said, pressing the notebook against her chest with both hands, the way she used to hold her medal in the seconds after a score was announced, back when medals still felt like the most important thing in the world. "And if you ever call yourself 'not a words person' again, I will personally delete every quad Lutz tutorial on your phone and replace them with Tchaikovsky lectures."</p>
 
<p>Sasha stared at her. Then, slowly, so slowly that Anna could track the movement the way a musician tracks a ritardando approaching its final beat, the corners of Sasha's mouth rose into a smile that was neither the cocky grin she wore for cameras nor the restless laugh she used to fill silence, but something rare and unguarded that Anna had seen perhaps three times in all the years she had known her.</p>
 
<p>"You're crying, Anneshka."</p>
 
<p>"I am <b>NOT</b> crying." Anna wiped her eyes with the oversized sleeve of Sasha's hoodie, which only smeared the dampness across her cheeks and made everything worse. "It's the cold. My eyes are watering because this apartment has the worst insulation in Moscow."</p>
 
<p>"Sure."</p>
 
<p>"Stop smiling."</p>
 
<p>"I'm not smiling."</p>
 
<p>"You are the worst liar in the entire history of Russian figure skating, and that is a competitive field." The words came out tangled between something that was definitely not a sob and something that was almost certainly a laugh, and Anna clutched the notebook tighter and pressed her face into Sasha's shoulder, because if she looked at that smile for one more second she would lose every last shred of the composure she had spent her entire career building, and she refused, absolutely and categorically refused, to give Alexandra Trusova the satisfaction.</p>
 
<p>Sasha did not move. She let Anna stay there, curled against her arm, the notebook pressed between them like a secret they had agreed to keep. Outside, the snow was falling now, the first real snowfall of the season, blanketing Moscow in a silence so deep it swallowed even the wind and the distant hum of traffic and the occasional cry of a bird that had been too stubborn to fly south. The lamp flickered once more, then steadied, casting its warm circle over two figures on a couch who might have been mistaken, from a distance, for a single shape.</p>
 
<p>After a long while, Anna's voice rose from somewhere against Sasha's collar, muffled and warm and still faintly trembling.</p>
 
<p>"Write me another one."</p>
 
<p>"Now?"</p>
 
<p>"Now. Tomorrow. Every winter." She lifted her face just enough for Sasha to see the last traces of a smile still tangled with something she would never, under any circumstances, admit were tears. "I want a whole book of winters, Sasha. Every single one."</p>
 
<p>The Quad Queen looked at the girl who had never needed quads to make the world hold its breath. Then she reached for the pen, uncapped it with her teeth the way she used to tear athletic tape at Khrustalny, and pressed its tip against a fresh page.</p>
 
<p>"Whatever you say, stubborn little champion."</p>
 
<p>And outside, the snow continued to fall, quietly, patiently, covering Moscow in the kind of white silence that exists only at the beginning of winter, when the world has not yet decided whether the cold will be cruel or kind, and two girls on a couch in an old apartment had already made their decision for it.</p>
