<p align="center">***</p>

<p align="center"><em>What’s in my name to you — what good?</em><br />
<em>It is to die like mournful rumble</em><br />
<em>Of waves that reach the shore and tumble,</em><br />
<em>Like sounds of night die in a wood.</em></p>

<p align="center"><em>My name is in your notes among</em><br />
<em>Some marks devoid of life, reminding</em><br />
<em>Of epitaphs with letters winding</em><br />
<em>In an unfathomable tongue.</em><br />
</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Sasha noticed it the moment Anna walked out of the clinic.</p>

<p>Not the swollen cheek. Not the slight slur in her speech from the anaesthetic that hadn't fully worn off. Those were surface details, the kind any person paying half-attention would catch. What Sasha noticed was the half-second delay between her question and Anna's smile, the way Anna's fingers kept drifting toward her own face before catching themselves, and a particular brightness in the hazel eyes that wasn't quite tears but lived close enough to borrow their light.</p>

<p>Sasha handed her the pharmacy bag. Anna took it. Their fingers brushed, and Anna flinched, just barely, just enough.</p>

<p>"How was it?" Sasha asked, linking her arm through Anna's as they walked to the car.</p>

<p>"Fine. She was very professional." Anna's voice was steady, composed, every syllable placed with the care of a skater marking choreography in a practice session. "I have to go back in two weeks."</p>

<p>"Was she nice to you?"</p>

<p>"Yes. Very thorough."</p>

<p>Three sentences. All correct. All true. And all performing the same function: a wall built of accurate bricks that nonetheless concealed everything behind it. Sasha had been reading Anna's walls for years. She could tell load-bearing from decorative. These were load-bearing.</p>

<p>She opened the car door, helped Anna in, walked around to the driver's side. The engine started. The radio came on, something soft that Anna had left playing, a cello piece, slow and low, the kind of music that fills a car without asking anything of the people inside it. Sasha let it stay.</p>

<p>They drove through Moscow's early winter streets in a quiet that was shaped like a conversation neither of them was having. Anna leaned against the window with her eyes half-closed, the anaesthetic lending her a softened, younger quality, and Sasha watched the road and thought about the thing she had been thinking about for three years.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>She had known since the morning after.</p>

<p>Not the details. Not the sequence of events, not the darkness, not the taste of cheap vodka on lips that were searching for someone else. She did not know that Anna had walked into their dormitory at two in the morning and kissed a girl who was not Sasha, believing that the shape on the couch was the shape she loved. She did not know about Kamila's hand sliding beneath Anna's shirt, or the moment it stopped, pulled back, withdrew with the particular violence of a conscience catching up to a body that had moved too fast.</p>

<p>She knew none of this, because no one had told her and she had never asked.</p>

<p>But she knew something had changed, the way you know a room has been rearranged even when you can't name which chair has moved. She knew because of the morning after, when she was standing at the stove in her crop top frying eggs, and Anna stumbled into the kitchen still half-asleep and asked, with the casual curiosity of someone who did not yet know the weight of what she was saying, "Where did you go last night? And why were you drinking on the sofa?"</p>

<p>Sasha had blinked. She hadn't gone anywhere. She had gotten up once to use the bathroom and come straight back to bed. As for drinking on the sofa, she had no idea what Anna was talking about. So she said the truth, the simple, innocent, devastating truth, in the same easy voice she used to say everything: "That wasn't me. That was Kamila."</p>

<p>Three words. And then she watched Anna's face.</p>

<p>The change lasted less than two seconds before composure replaced it, but two seconds was more than enough. Sasha saw the colour drain from Anna's cheeks. She saw the hand rise, involuntarily, to touch her own lips. She saw the exact moment when Anna's eyes found Kamila on the sofa across the room, found the blonde hair she had mistaken for Sasha's in the dark, and understood what she had done, and the expression that crossed her face in that understanding was something Sasha had never seen before and that contained, in its brief existence, everything she needed to know without knowing any of the specifics.</p>

<p>Kamila looked up from her medical textbook, glanced at both of them, nodded once as if confirming Sasha's words, and returned to her reading. The stillness of someone who is working very hard to appear normal and whose effort is itself the evidence that normal has been breached.</p>

<p>Then Anna said she had a stomach ache and needed to leave early. And Sasha, still holding the pan of fried eggs, still standing at the stove in the apartment she had never left, watched her girlfriend gather her things and go, and she did not ask questions, because the answers were already written across Anna's face in a handwriting that Sasha could read as clearly as she read the ice before a jump.</p>

<p>She never confronted Anna. She never confronted Kamila. She simply absorbed what she had seen and placed it in the part of herself where she stored things that were too complex to process through language but too important to discard, the physical archive of a girl who had always understood the world through her body first and her brain second.</p>

<p>When Kamila moved out three weeks later, Sasha helped her carry the last box. They stood in the parking lot, November air sharp on their faces, and Kamila looked at Sasha with an expression that contained the same compound of emotions Anna had worn that morning, guilt and grief and something unnamed that connected all three of them in a shape none of them had chosen.</p>

<p>"Take care, Kamila," Sasha said.</p>

<p>"You too." A pause. Then, quietly: "Take care of her."</p>

<p>Take care of <em>her</em>. Not "take care of each other." Not "see you around." Take care of her. Three words that transferred a responsibility from one pair of hands to another, gentle and final. Sasha watched the car until it turned the corner and disappeared, and she stood in the cold for a while after that, her breath making small clouds, thinking about what it meant to be entrusted with something by the person you should, by every conventional logic, consider a rival.</p>

<p>She had never thought of Kamila as a rival. That was the part that nobody would have expected, and that Sasha herself did not fully understand until much later. Rivals are people who want the same thing and compete for it. Kamila had never competed. She had simply loved, silently, from the wrong side of a wall she never tried to climb, and when the accident on the couch brought her briefly, catastrophically, to the other side, she had climbed back over immediately and walked away and asked only that someone else do the thing she could not.</p>

<p>How do you hate a person like that? You don't. You carry the knowledge of them the way you carry a note you found in someone else's handwriting inside a book you borrowed, a note that was not addressed to you and that you were not meant to read but that you read anyway, and that changed the way you understood the book, and that you placed back exactly where you found it because the book did not belong to you even if the reading of it did.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The car pulled into the apartment building's lot. Sasha turned off the engine. The cello piece ended, leaving a silence that was shaped like the last note's absence, a space where sound had been and was no longer. Anna stirred, blinked, touched her swollen cheek.</p>

<p>"Soup?" Sasha asked.</p>

<p>"Please."</p>

<p>Inside, Sasha cooked while Anna took her antibiotics and changed into the oversized hoodie she always wore when she felt vulnerable, the grey one with the stretched-out collar that made her look smaller than she was. They ate at the kitchen table with the unhurried rhythm of two people who had shared enough meals to no longer need to fill the gaps between spoonfuls with conversation. The soup was good. The silence was complicated.</p>

<p>"Sasha?"</p>

<p>"Hm?"</p>

<p>"Thank you for finding me a good dentist."</p>

<p>"Of course. Kamila's the best."</p>

<p>The name landed on the table between them like a coin placed down in payment for something. Anna's spoon paused for a fraction of a second, then resumed. Sasha registered the pause the way she registered all of Anna's micro-movements, automatically, without commentary, filing it in the physical archive alongside everything else.</p>

<p>She had sent Anna to Kamila on purpose.</p>

<p>This was the fact that Sasha had been carrying since the phone call two days ago, when Anna first complained about the tooth and Sasha, without hesitation, without the appearance of calculation, said "I know someone, let me call her." She had Kamila's number because they had never fully lost contact. Not frequent communication, not friendship exactly, but the occasional message on birthdays, a congratulation when Kamila completed her residency, the minimal, respectful maintenance of a connection that both of them understood was held together not by affection for each other but by their separate, permanent orientations toward the same person.</p>

<p>She could have chosen any dentist in Moscow. She chose Kamila, and the reason was not revenge, not testing, not any of the dramatic motivations that stories assign to people who discover their partner's secrets. The reason was simpler and harder to explain: Sasha believed in facing things.</p>

<p>It was the athlete in her. When a jump goes wrong, you don't avoid it in the next practice. You go back to it, immediately, while the fall is still fresh in your muscles, because the body learns avoidance faster than it learns correction, and every day you spend not attempting the jump is a day the fear grows stronger. Anna had been avoiding Kamila for three years. The avoidance had become structural, built into the architecture of her daily life, a permanent detour around a street she was afraid to walk down. Sasha understood, with the instinctive physical logic of someone who had spent her career throwing her body into rotations that terrified her, that avoidance does not resolve fear. It preserves it. It keeps the fear fresh and sealed, like a specimen in a jar, unchanged by time, waiting.</p>

<p>She did not know what would happen when Anna sat in Kamila's chair. She did not script the outcome. She simply removed the detour and trusted both of them to walk the street.</p>

<p>Now Anna had walked it, and she had come home different, and Sasha sat across the kitchen table and registered the difference and chose, as she had chosen every day for three years, to hold what she knew without requiring Anna to know that she knew it.</p>

<p>This was the part that cost the most. Not the knowledge itself, which was a fixed weight she had long since learned to carry, but the daily, active decision not to set it down. Every morning Sasha woke beside Anna and chose not to say "I know about the couch." Every evening she watched Anna's face for the micro-expressions that told her the buried thing was still alive and chose not to dig it up. Every time Kamila's name surfaced in conversation, Sasha tracked Anna's reaction and chose to let it pass without comment, because commenting would have forced Anna to either lie or confess, and Sasha refused to put the person she loved in a position where both options caused damage.</p>

<p>It was exhausting. It was also, Sasha believed, the truest form of love she was capable of: not the love that demands full transparency, not the love that insists on knowing everything and being known in return, but the love that sees clearly and holds gently and does not require the other person to perform their wounds for an audience, even an audience of one.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>They went to bed early. Anna was asleep within minutes, the anaesthetic's residual drowsiness pulling her under with the swift, complete authority of something pharmaceutical. Sasha lay beside her in the dark and listened to her breathe, the slow, even rhythm that she had been falling asleep to for years, the rhythm she would have recognized in a room full of sleeping people the way a musician recognizes a familiar melody played on an unfamiliar instrument.</p>

<p>Anna's breathing changed when she dreamed. It quickened slightly, the intervals between inhale and exhale shortening by a fraction, and sometimes her lips moved around sounds that were not quite words but carried the cadence of speech, the ghost of a conversation happening in a place Sasha could not follow. Tonight, the lips moved, and Sasha listened, and the sound that emerged was not a word but close to one, two syllables, soft and indistinct, dissolving into the pillow before they could fully form.</p>

<p>Sasha did not need them to fully form. She had heard them before, on other nights, shaped by the same mouth in the same half-conscious register, and she knew whose name they were reaching for the way she knew the exact moment before a blade touches ice after a triple axel, by feel, by the shift in air pressure, by the body's knowledge of something the mind has not yet confirmed.</p>

<p>She closed her eyes. Somewhere in Moscow, in a clinic that was now dark and empty, a woman with steady hands and a disciplined face was sitting alone, and Sasha did not need to see this to know it was happening, because some truths do not require evidence. They simply exist, the way gravity exists, invisible and permanent and shaping everything without asking permission.</p>

<p>Sasha pulled Anna closer. Not possessively, not anxiously, but with the calm, practiced hold of someone who has made a decision and is living inside it. Anna's body folded against hers, warm and small and carrying within it a history that Sasha knew and a secret that Sasha kept and a heart that beat for Sasha and also, in some quiet chamber that Sasha would never enter and never try to enter, beat for someone else.</p>

<p>That was alright. Hearts are not metronomes. They do not keep a single time. They speed up, they slow down, they skip, they stutter, they hold rhythms that no conductor has scored and that no audience will ever hear. Anna's heart held a rhythm that included Kamila, and Sasha's love was large enough to hold a heart that was not entirely hers, because entirely was never the point. The point was that Anna was here, breathing against her collarbone, alive and present and choosing this bed and this body and this life, and the choosing was what mattered, not the absence of alternatives but the presence of a decision, made fresh every morning, to stay.</p>

<p>Outside, the first snow of December began to fall over Moscow, quiet and unhurried, covering the city the way silence covers a truth that three people have agreed, without ever speaking to each other about it, to leave exactly where it is.</p>
