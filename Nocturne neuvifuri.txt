<p align="center"><em>Andante con gran amore e poco scherzando</em></p>
<p align="center">***</p>

<p>"You're strange, you know."</p>

<p>Neuvillette looked up from the legal brief on his desk and regarded the girl sitting cross-legged on his sofa. The Iudex was a perceptive man; he could often anticipate what someone was about to say before the sentence fully formed, allowing him to compose a measured response well in advance. But that ability had never functioned reliably around Furina, and this moment was no exception.</p>

<p>"In what sense?" he asked, one eyebrow rising by a precise, minimal degree.</p>

<p>"Ah, sorry, I didn't say that right," Furina laughed, waving a hand as if swatting away her own vagueness. She still had that habit of blurting the conclusion before laying any groundwork. "I mean you, specifically. You're a strange person."</p>

<p>"I will not pretend to find that illuminating, because I genuinely have no idea what you're referring to."</p>

<p>Furina shifted on the sofa with the theatrical restlessness of someone who had spent centuries performing stillness and now, in private, refused to hold any position for longer than thirty seconds. She tucked one leg beneath her and propped her chin on the armrest, watching Neuvillette through eyes that were the colour of the ocean in a region where the ocean was the only thing anyone had ever worshipped.</p>

<p>"Your taste in music. That's what I mean. You always choose these pieces that are so quiet and slow and serious, like the world is ending but politely. I could never listen to that. For me, a performance needs energy, something that grabs the audience by the throat and doesn't let go until they're on their feet. You need spectacle, you need drama, you need the crowd gasping. That's what makes a show. How do you sit through an entire evening of someone playing a single note for twelve seconds?"</p>

<p>Neuvillette did not answer immediately. Instead he studied Furina, who was staring at him with the particular intensity of someone who has asked a question and expects the answer to contain something they've been looking for without knowing they were looking. The tall, silver-haired man allowed himself a slight, controlled smile.</p>

<p>"Since when did the former Divine of Fontaine develop such a keen interest in my listening habits? Did something unusual happen today to prompt this line of inquiry?"</p>

<p>"Don't deflect. You know I've always been interested in you."</p>

<p>A faint sound escaped Neuvillette that might, under laboratory conditions, have been classified as a chuckle. Furina's ocean-blue eyes instinctively flicked toward the clock on the mantelpiece; it was nearing midnight. Beyond the tall windows of his apartment, where the autumn wind carried the first real chill off the harbour, a crescent moon hung above the rooftops of Fontaine, casting its pale light across the water that ran through every street and square of the city like a second circulatory system. On the harbour's surface, the reflections of distant lamps trembled in long golden columns, and above them, a scattering of stars pressed their faint light against the darkness as though searching for a gap in it, a place to settle, a sanctuary for the smallest and most unspoken of a young woman's wants.</p>

<p>Neuvillette returned his gaze to the room, where the only light came from the reading lamp on his desk, its warm glow catching the blue of Furina's eyes the way harbour light catches the surface of still water. For a moment, looking at those eyes felt less like an act of observation and more like an act of immersion.</p>

<p>"Hey, are you still here with me, Monsieur Neuvillette?"</p>

<p>Instead of answering, Neuvillette stood. He crossed the room to the old phonograph he kept beside the bookshelf, a device Furina had mocked on her first visit ("Who still owns one of these? Are you secretly two hundred years old?" and he had said nothing, because the answer was complicated). He selected a record from the shelf with the same deliberation he applied to selecting case law, placed it on the turntable, and positioned the needle before turning back to Furina.</p>

<p>"Would you like to dance?"</p>

<p>"What?" This time, it was Furina's turn to be caught without a script.</p>

<p>"You asked how I can appreciate music that doesn't seize the audience by the throat. I could explain, but I suspect an explanation would bore you within four minutes. A more effective pedagogy would be direct experience."</p>

<p>For a fleeting instant, a war broke out behind Furina's eyes. On one side, she understood his logic perfectly. If someone sat her down and lectured her on the emotional architecture of slow music for forty minutes, she would either fall asleep or start performing attention so convincingly that the lecturer would never know she had mentally redecorated the entire room. Experience was faster. Experience was honest. Everyone said so.</p>

<p>But on the other side, Furina was terrified. Not of Neuvillette, never of Neuvillette, how could she be terrified of someone who timed his tea steeping with a pocket watch and considered "moderately pleasant" to be a strong emotional statement? The terror was vaguer than that, born from something she couldn't name, something that lived in the space between herself and this tall, composed man who looked at her as though she were not performing whenever she was quite certain that she was. She was "afraid" of standing too close to him, "afraid" of the thought that those long, elegant fingers might fold around hers, "afraid" of feeling the steady rhythm of his heart against her own chest, because Furina had spent five hundred years on a stage, playing a role that required her to perform every emotion while feeling none of them, and now that the curtain had fallen and the audience had gone home, she no longer knew which of her feelings were real and which were choreography, and the prospect of standing close enough to another person to find out was the most frightening thing she had faced since the day she stopped being a god.</p>

<p><em>She had only wanted to tease him about his boring music. How on earth did it end up like this?</em></p>

<p>But the former Divine stood, and she crossed the room toward the silver-haired man waiting at its centre. Furina did not know what she was supposed to do, so she let her body decide. And her body, her heart, chose to walk toward Neuvillette.</p>

<p><em>Let's just say it was because she didn't want him standing there looking like a well-dressed lamppost.</em></p>

<p>"I should warn you, I don't actually know how to dance. Five hundred years of pretending to be a god and nobody once asked me to waltz," Furina said, her voice pitched for comedy, though her lips were dry enough that she ran her tongue across them without thinking. "Don't hold it against me if I step on your very expensive shoes."</p>

<p>"Don't worry. Just let your body relax and listen. I trust you'll find the rhythm without instruction," Neuvillette said, lowering the needle onto the record. "Let the music guide you."</p>

<p>He turned to face her and extended one hand, waiting. In that moment, Furina became acutely aware of how much taller he was, tall enough that she had to tilt her chin upward to meet his eyes, tall enough that from this distance she could catch the faint, clean scent of whatever he used on his hair, something that smelled like rain on warm stone. But that was certainly not why her heart was beating fast enough to register on seismographic equipment.</p>

<p>Furina placed her small hand into his palm, and his fingers closed around it, long and cool and precise, enveloping her calloused little fist the way water envelops a pebble. In that fraction of a second, Furina felt as though every drop of blood in her body had decided, unanimously and without consulting her brain, to relocate to her face.</p>

<p>The record began with a single note, held and gentle, a sound that entered the room the way dawn enters a window, not all at once but by degrees, filling the space without displacing anything already in it. Furina felt her shoulders drop a fraction. Her jaw unclenched. The note repeated, patient and unhurried, and each repetition loosened something in her that she hadn't realized was tight. Neuvillette seemed to sense it, because his grip on her hand softened, giving her fingers room, giving her room, and the music continued its quiet insistence, steady as breathing, dissolving the tension she carried in her frame the way warm water dissolves salt.</p>

<p>Furina inhaled slowly.</p>

<p>Then a second voice entered, deeper, a low string drawn long, vibrating through the room's air and into the floorboards and up through the soles of Furina's feet until she could feel it in her ankles, her calves, the base of her spine. It held its note for what felt like a very long time, long enough for her breath to slow, long enough for her pulse to find the sound and fall into step beside it, long enough for her body to begin moving before her mind gave permission.</p>

<p>Her feet shifted. Forward, following the melody as it climbed, then back as it descended. Small steps, tentative, the steps of a girl who had spent centuries executing grand gestures for an audience and was now, for the first time, making movements that nobody would see and nobody would judge and that existed for no purpose beyond the private, terrifying pleasure of being in motion with another person.</p>

<p>Neuvillette let her lead. He was not the centre; he was not directing. He simply moved when she moved, followed when she stepped, became the steady ground beneath a rhythm that was entirely hers to set. His hand held hers with exactly enough pressure to be felt and not enough to constrain, a presence rather than a force.</p>

<p>The music paused for a breath, and Furina closed her eyes. Behind her lids she saw the harbour at night, the trembling gold columns of lamplight on the water, the crescent moon above a city built on canals and secrets and the memory of a god who had loved it enough to die for it. And here, in this apartment with its old phonograph and its reading lamp and its shelves of legal volumes, everything was being recreated in miniature. The melody when she stepped forward. The steady bass note, low and reliable as the hand she was holding. The warmth of a body close enough to feel but not close enough to touch.</p>

<p>Furina opened her eyes and found that his fingers had woven through hers, interlaced, holding on with a gentle firmness that radiated warmth into her palm. When had that happened? She didn't care. She let her own fingers trace the lines of his hand, the long bones beneath the skin, the cool smoothness of his knuckles, the place where his pulse beat faintly against her thumb.</p>

<p>It wasn't a perfect hand. It was too cool, too precise, too much like the hand of a man who had spent his existence measuring the world rather than touching it. But it was real.</p>

<p>So real that Furina only wanted to hold it tighter, to memorize every ridge and valley before the music ended and the ordinary world returned and she had to go back to being a girl who didn't know which of her feelings were performance and which were not.</p>

<p>"Cheater," she whispered.</p>

<p>"Says the woman who engineered this entire conversation to arrive at precisely this outcome."</p>

<p>Furina didn't reply. She half-closed her eyes and continued to move. Following the music, following the rhythm that had somehow become indistinguishable from the pulse in her own chest. Forward, back, a slight drift to the right, then left as the melody turned. She drew him closer, not thinking about it, just doing it, the way a body seeks warmth without asking the mind's opinion first. To feel the steady pulse of the bass line. To feel it echoing inside his chest. To feel, for the first time in five hundred years, that a rhythm she hadn't chosen for herself could still be worth following.</p>

<p>The final note drew out, merging with a high, clear tone above it, two voices becoming one, and between them the breath of two people met in the resonance and held, and held, and held, until the needle reached the end of the record and the only sound left was the soft, repeating click of the phonograph turning in empty circles, and neither of them moved to stop it.</p>
