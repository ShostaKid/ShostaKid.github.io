<p align="center"><em>Allegro non troppo, con dolcezza e con un sorriso nascosto</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>A soft humming drifted through the corridor of the dormitory, tuneless and barely audible, the kind of sound a person makes when they are alone and have temporarily forgotten that walls are thin and that other people exist on the far side of them. It was past eleven, which at Khrustalny meant that the rink lights had been off for two hours, the coaches had retreated to their offices or their homes, and the building had settled into the particular hush of a place that spends its days full of motion and its nights full of the exhausted silence that follows it.</p>

<p>The humming was accompanied by the sound of a small electric kettle reaching its boil, clicking off with a plastic snap that echoed in the communal kitchen. A carton of milk was lifted from the refrigerator, the kind that came in a white box with blue lettering and a picture of a cow that looked mildly offended by its own commodification. A measure was poured into a saucepan, heated gently on the single working burner, and watched with the patient attentiveness of someone who understood that milk, unlike water, does not forgive inattention. A spoonful of honey was stirred in, then another, because one was never enough and three was too sweet, and the difference between "not enough" and "too sweet" was a margin so narrow that only a person who had been making this exact drink for months would know where it fell.</p>

<p>Kamila frowned at the saucepan and stopped humming. She did not like this kitchen. She did not like its fluorescent light that made everyone's skin look vaguely ill, or its linoleum floor that was always slightly sticky near the stove, or the way the refrigerator hummed at a pitch that clashed with whatever melody she was carrying in her head. But the kitchen was the only place in the dormitory with a working burner, and warm milk could not be made in an electric kettle, or at least it could not be made well, and Kamila had strong opinions about the difference between "made" and "made well" that she applied to exactly two domains in her life: figure skating and warm beverages for people she cared about.</p>

<p>The milk was ready. She poured it into a cup that was not paper and not plastic and not any of the disposable vessels that accumulated in the kitchen bin like the shed skins of a building that consumed and discarded without sentiment. It was a ceramic cup, small and round-bellied, with a chip on the rim and a pattern of pale blue flowers that had faded from years of washing but had not disappeared entirely, the way things that are genuinely part of an object never entirely disappear no matter how much time and use work against them. Kamila had found the cup at a flea market in Khimki during a rare free afternoon, wedged between a tarnished samovar and a stack of Soviet-era postcards, and she had bought it for forty roubles because it was real and solid and would not be thrown away after a single use, and because she needed something that would survive the repetition of what she was about to do with it.</p>

<p>She had been doing this for two months now. Every night, or nearly every night, when the dormitory settled and the corridor lights dimmed to their overnight setting and the only sounds were the building's mechanical breathing and the occasional creak of a floorboard beneath the weight of a girl going to the bathroom, Kamila would make warm milk with honey, pour it into the ceramic cup, and carry it to a room that was not hers.</p>

<p>Anna Shcherbakova's room was at the end of the corridor, the last door on the left, distinguishable from the others by the small sticker of a musical note that someone (Anna herself, Kamila suspected) had placed on the doorframe at eye level. The sticker was peeling at one corner, and Kamila had resisted the urge to press it back down on multiple occasions because pressing it back down would mean lingering at the door, and lingering at the door would mean risking being seen, and being seen would mean explaining why she was standing outside Anna Shcherbakova's room at midnight holding a cup of warm milk and a sketchbook.</p>

<p>The explanation would have been difficult. Not because it was complicated, but because it was simple, and simple things are sometimes the hardest to say aloud because they leave no room for the comfortable ambiguity that complicated explanations provide.</p>

<p>The door did not creak, because Kamila had borrowed a small bottle of machine oil from the rink maintenance closet three weeks ago and applied it to the hinges herself, at six in the morning, before anyone else was awake. The maintenance staff had not noticed. Anna had not noticed. Nobody had noticed, because nobody paid attention to door hinges, which was exactly the kind of invisible, meticulous, slightly obsessive act of care that Kamila Valieva excelled at and that she would have been mortified to have anyone observe.</p>

<p>The room was dark. The curtains were drawn, but a thin line of streetlight slipped through the gap where they did not quite meet, casting a pale stripe across the floor that climbed the edge of the bed and settled on the shape beneath the blanket. Anna lay on her side, facing the wall, her dark hair spread across the pillow in a way that Kamila had memorized from dozens of angles over dozens of nights but that still, every single time, made something behind her ribs tighten with a sensation she had been trying and failing to name since the first week of September.</p>

<p>She set the cup down on the small bedside table, carefully, the ceramic meeting the wood with a sound so faint it was less a clink and more a whisper of contact. Beside the cup she placed a small wrapped biscuit, the kind from the tin that the team nutritionist reluctantly allowed on rest days, because Kamila had noticed that Anna sometimes woke in the night and ate something small before going back to sleep, and the knowledge that she had noticed this, that she had been paying close enough attention to another person's nocturnal eating habits to identify a pattern, was one of many things about herself that Kamila found simultaneously mortifying and impossible to stop.</p>

<p>Then she stepped back. This was the part where she should leave. This was the part where she always left, retracing her steps to the door, slipping into the corridor, returning to her own room, and lying awake for twenty minutes replaying the evening's mission with the analytical rigour of a skater reviewing competition footage, identifying every moment where she could have been quieter, faster, less present.</p>

<p>But tonight she did not leave.</p>

<p>Tonight, her hand went to the sketchbook tucked under her arm, and she stood in the stripe of streetlight and looked at the sleeping girl and felt the pull of something stronger than caution.</p>

<p>Kamila had always loved to paint. It was the one thing in her life that existed entirely outside the ecosystem of competitive figure skating, the one activity that no coach had assigned, no choreographer had shaped, no judge would ever score. When she painted, the part of her brain that calculated rotations and tracked blade angles and monitored the precise distribution of weight across the ball of her foot went quiet, and a different part woke up, a part that did not measure or count but simply looked, and what it saw, it tried to hold.</p>

<p>She had started painting Anna in October, three weeks into the visits, when the warm milk had become routine but the looking had not. The first sketch was terrible, a hasty pencil drawing done from memory back in her own room, the proportions wrong, the hair too dark, the curve of the jaw inaccurate in a way that made the face look like a stranger's. She had torn it out and thrown it away. The second attempt was better. The third was almost right. By the fifteenth, she had stopped drawing from memory and started bringing the sketchbook with her, standing in the dark room, working by the thin stripe of streetlight, capturing what she could in the minutes she allowed herself before the risk of waking Anna outweighed the need to get the angle of her eyelashes correct.</p>

<p>The sketchbook was now half full. It contained no landscapes, no still lifes, no studies of fruit or flowers or the other safe, neutral subjects that art teachers assign to students who are not yet ready to draw what they actually want to draw. Every page was Anna. Anna sleeping, Anna's hand curled on the pillow, Anna's hair catching the streetlight, Anna's shoulder emerging from the blanket at an angle that Kamila had rendered in graphite and watercolour and, once, in a soft pastel that smudged across the opposite page and left a ghost of itself on the image facing it, so that two Annas looked at each other across the spine of the book, one sharp and one blurred, one real and one remembered.</p>

<p>Tonight, Kamila opened the sketchbook to a fresh page, took the short pencil from behind her ear, and began to draw.</p>

<p>She did not know that Anna was awake.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>Anna had been awake since the kettle clicked off in the kitchen.</p>

<p>Not because the sound was loud, it was barely audible through the corridor and the closed door, but because Anna Shcherbakova had spent years training her body to wake at the slightest deviation from silence, a skill honed by early-morning practice schedules and the particular hypervigilance of a girl who had always believed that the difference between winning and losing lived in the margins, in the seconds before full consciousness, in the ability to be alert when everyone else was still reaching for the snooze button.</p>

<p>She knew who was in the kitchen. She knew because of the humming, which was Kamila's and only Kamila's, a tuneless, barely-there vibration that Anna had first noticed in September and had since catalogued alongside every other small detail of Kamila Valieva's existence that her brain, without being asked, had decided was essential information: the way Kamila held her pencils (too close to the tip, the way artists hold them, not the way students hold them), the way she entered rooms (quietly, always quietly, as though she were apologizing for the displacement of air her body caused), the way she said Anna's name on the rare occasions she said it at all (softly, with a slight hesitation before the second syllable, as though the name were a door she was not sure she had permission to open).</p>

<p>Anna knew about the visits. She had known since the third night, when she had woken to the sound of ceramic on wood and opened her eyes a fraction to see a tall, slender figure retreating toward the door with the careful, silent steps of someone who has practised this exit many times. She had seen the cup on the bedside table, warm milk with honey, and she had seen the wrapped biscuit beside it, and she had understood, with the immediate, comprehensive clarity that was both her greatest strength and her most exhausting quality, exactly what these offerings meant and exactly who was making them and exactly why that person chose to make them in the dark, anonymously, without credit or acknowledgement, the way certain animals leave gifts at the dens of other animals they have chosen and hope to be chosen by in return.</p>

<p>She had never said anything. Not because she was afraid, and not because she did not want the visits to continue, but because she understood, with the particular empathy of a person who had spent her entire life performing composure in front of judges and cameras, that Kamila's silence was not shyness but a form of self-protection, and that exposing it would not bring them closer but would instead destroy the fragile, private architecture that Kamila had built around her feelings, the architecture of oiled hinges and tiptoed steps and cups of milk left on bedside tables in the dark, an architecture so delicate and so carefully maintained that Anna found it, if she was honest, the most beautiful thing anyone had ever built for her.</p>

<p>So she pretended to sleep. Night after night, she lay on her side with her eyes closed and listened to Kamila enter, set down the cup, pause, and leave. She drank the milk after the footsteps faded, always surprised by how precisely the honey was measured, always warmed by the temperature, which was never too hot and never too cool but exactly the warmth of something that has been carried carefully from one room to another by hands that were determined not to let it lose a single degree along the way.</p>

<p>But tonight was different. Tonight the footsteps did not retreat after the cup was set down. Tonight they paused, the way they always paused, but then they stayed, and Anna heard a sound she had not heard before: the soft, fibrous whisper of paper being turned, followed by the faint scratch of pencil on a page.</p>

<p>Anna's heart rate, which she had spent years learning to control in high-pressure situations, accelerated to a tempo that no breathing exercise could regulate. She kept her eyes closed. She kept her body still. She kept every muscle in the exact position it had been in when the door opened, because if she moved, if she breathed differently, if she so much as shifted the angle of her hand on the pillow, Kamila would know she was awake, and Kamila would stop, and Anna did not want her to stop, because whatever Kamila was doing with that pencil and that paper was the secret inside the secret, the innermost room of the architecture she had been building for months, and Anna wanted to let her finish.</p>

<p>She lay still and listened to the sound of being drawn.</p>

<p>It was, she discovered, an extraordinarily intimate experience. Not because of the content (she could not see what Kamila was drawing and would not know until much later) but because of the quality of attention it implied. A photograph takes a fraction of a second. A drawing takes minutes, sometimes longer, and during those minutes the person drawing must look, truly look, with a sustained and focused gaze that most people never direct at another human being because sustained looking is, in most social contexts, an act of aggression or desire or both. But the looking that Anna could feel, even with her eyes closed, even through the darkness, was neither aggressive nor acquisitive. It was careful, patient, reverent, the looking of someone who is trying to understand a thing by reproducing it, line by line, the way a musician learns a piece by playing it slowly enough to hear every note individually.</p>

<p>Kamila was learning her. That was what the pencil on the paper meant. She was learning Anna the way she learned everything: quietly, thoroughly, without asking permission, because asking permission would have meant admitting that the learning mattered, and admitting that the learning mattered would have meant saying the thing that the warm milk and the oiled hinges and the midnight visits had been saying for months without words.</p>

<p>Anna did not know how long she lay there. Long enough for the pencil's rhythm to change several times, alternating between quick, confident strokes and slower, more hesitant ones that paused and resumed and paused again, as though the hand holding the pencil kept encountering details that required more care than the previous ones. Long enough for the streetlight to shift slightly as a cloud moved across the sky outside. Long enough for the milk on the bedside table to cool from warm to lukewarm, which Anna could tell from the change in the scent, the way heated milk smells different from room-temperature milk, sweeter when warm, flatter when cool.</p>

<p>Then the pencil stopped.</p>

<p>A silence followed that was different from the silences of previous nights. It was not the silence of departure. It was the silence of someone standing still and looking at something they have just made and feeling, in the gap between completion and evaluation, the full weight of what the making has revealed about the maker.</p>

<p>Anna opened her eyes.</p>

<p>She did it slowly, the way she did everything, with control and precision, letting her eyelids rise just enough to see through the dark fringe of her lashes without fully committing to wakefulness. And through that narrow aperture she saw Kamila standing in the stripe of streetlight, sketchbook open in one hand, pencil hovering in the other, her face illuminated on one side and shadowed on the other, her dark hair pulled back in a loose braid that was coming undone at the end, and her expression carrying something that Anna had never seen on the face of this quiet, careful, paint-loving girl who moved through the world as though she were trying to leave no footprints.</p>

<p>It was the expression of someone who has just finished a drawing and realized, looking at it, that the drawing is a confession.</p>

<p>Kamila's eyes were still on the page. She had not noticed that her subject was now looking back at her. Anna could see the slight tremor in the hand holding the pencil, the way Kamila's lower lip was caught between her teeth, the barely visible rise and fall of her breathing, faster than normal, the breathing of a person who is standing in a room with a sleeping girl and holding a sketchbook full of that girl's face and understanding, perhaps for the first time, that what she has been doing for two months is not a hobby.</p>

<p>Anna should have closed her eyes. The kind thing to do, the thing that would have preserved the architecture and allowed them both to continue pretending, was to close her eyes and let Kamila leave and drink the milk after the door closed and never mention any of it. That was the safe choice. The composed choice. The Anna Shcherbakova choice.</p>

<p>"Kamila."</p>

<p>The name left her mouth before the choice was made, soft and unhurried, spoken in the voice she used when she was not performing for anyone, the voice that existed only in the small hours when the building was quiet and the judges were asleep and the only audience was a girl with a sketchbook who had just realized she was no longer alone.</p>

<p>Kamila froze. The pencil stopped. The sketchbook snapped shut with the reflexive speed of a diary being closed by a teenager who has just heard a parent's footstep in the hall. Her eyes, wide and dark in the streetlight, found Anna's, and what passed between them in that moment was not a conversation but a collision, the gentle, devastating impact of two people who have been circling each other in the dark for months finally, accidentally, occupying the same point in space.</p>

<p>"I..." Kamila's voice was barely a whisper. "I woke you. I'm sorry, I..."</p>

<p>She was already backing toward the door, the sketchbook pressed against her chest like a shield, her body executing the retreat she had rehearsed a hundred times. Two steps, three. Her free hand reached behind her for the door handle. Anna could see the blush even in the darkness, the way it spread from Kamila's cheeks to the tips of her ears, and the sight of it, that involuntary, uncontrollable redness on the face of a girl who controlled everything else about herself with surgical precision, made Anna feel something so tender and so fierce that it frightened her.</p>

<p>"Kamila," she said again, and the name stopped the retreat the way a held note stops a melody, suspending everything in the space between sound and silence. Anna sat up slowly, the blanket pooling at her waist, and looked at the girl in the doorway. "Sit with me."</p>

<p>Kamila stood very still, caught between the momentum of her exit and the gravity of the invitation, and the seconds that passed were the longest seconds Anna had experienced since the last time she waited for a score at the boards, the kind of seconds that contain entire decisions inside them.</p>

<p>Then Kamila came back.</p>

<p>She crossed the room with the uncertain, hopeful steps of a person who has been invited into a space she had only ever visited as a ghost, and she sat on the edge of the bed, carefully, keeping a precise distance between her body and Anna's, the kind of distance that communicates both respect and terror in equal measure. The sketchbook was still pressed against her chest. Her breathing was audible.</p>

<p>"The milk," Anna said, picking up the ceramic cup from the bedside table and wrapping both hands around it the way she always did when she drank it, the way Kamila had never seen because she was always gone before the drinking happened. "You always get the honey exactly right. How?"</p>

<p>"Two spoonfuls," Kamila said automatically, then looked stricken, because the precision of the answer revealed the extent of the practice, and the extent of the practice revealed everything.</p>

<p>Anna smiled. It was a small smile, the kind that lived more in her eyes than on her mouth, the kind that Kamila had tried to capture in graphite eleven times across eleven different pages and had never quite gotten right because smiles, unlike sleeping faces, are alive, and living things resist being held still.</p>

<p>"Show me," Anna said, and tilted her head toward the sketchbook.</p>

<p>Kamila's arms tightened around it. For a moment Anna thought she would refuse, would stand, would flee, would dismantle the architecture herself rather than let anyone see what was inside it. But then something shifted in Kamila's expression, a small, quiet surrender, the same micro-expression that Anna had seen on her face before a particularly difficult jump combination, the moment where fear gives way not to confidence but to the decision that the attempt is worth more than the safety of not trying.</p>

<p>Kamila opened the sketchbook.</p>

<p>Anna looked.</p>

<p>Page after page of herself, rendered in pencil and watercolour and soft pastel, sleeping in positions she recognized and positions she did not, her hair catching light that she had never seen from this angle, her hand curled on a pillow she had touched every night without thinking about the shape it made, her shoulder emerging from a blanket she had pulled up for warmth without considering that the line it created against her neck was something someone might want to preserve. The drawings were not perfect. Some of the proportions were slightly off, and the shading was sometimes too heavy, and there was one page where a watercolour had bled beyond its intended border and stained the adjacent sketch. But they were alive. Every one of them was alive, carrying within its lines the specific, patient attention of a person who had stood in the dark and looked and looked and looked, and who had tried, with pencil and pigment, to hold what she saw long enough to understand why it mattered so much to her.</p>

<p>Anna turned the pages slowly. She did not speak. The milk cooled in her hands. The streetlight held its thin stripe across the bed, illuminating the sketchbook between them like a stage light that had been positioned, with improbable precision, to shine on exactly the thing that needed to be seen.</p>

<p>When she reached the last drawing, the one Kamila had been working on tonight, still unfinished, the pencil lines still fresh enough that a careless thumb would smudge them, Anna closed the book gently and placed it on the bed between them.</p>

<p>"You've been coming here every night," Anna said, and it was not a question.</p>

<p>"Yes." Kamila's voice was very small.</p>

<p>"Since September."</p>

<p>"Yes."</p>

<p>"And you never planned to tell me."</p>

<p>A silence. Then, even smaller: "No."</p>

<p>Anna set the milk down. She reached across the sketchbook, across the distance that Kamila had so carefully measured between them, and took the hand that was still holding the pencil. Kamila's fingers were cold, the way fingers get when the blood has been redirected to the heart by a nervous system that has decided the current situation constitutes an emergency, and Anna held them the way she held the ceramic cup, with both hands, wrapping her smaller fingers around Kamila's longer ones, pressing warmth into skin that needed it.</p>

<p>"Two spoonfuls," Anna said softly, and the words were not about honey.</p>

<p>Kamila looked at their hands. She looked at Anna. She looked at the sketchbook lying between them, full of a secret that was no longer a secret, full of evidence that she had spent two months building an architecture of love out of oiled hinges and warm milk and drawings made in the dark, and that the architecture, now exposed, was not collapsing but standing, standing because the person it was built for had known about it all along and had chosen, every night, to let it continue.</p>

<p>"You knew," Kamila said, and the realization arrived on her face like sunrise arriving on a landscape, slow and total and changing the colour of everything it touched.</p>

<p>"Since the third night."</p>

<p>"And you never..."</p>

<p>"No." Anna's thumb traced a slow circle on the back of Kamila's hand, the same motion a painter might use to blend a shadow into skin, gentle and deliberate and impossibly careful. "Because you weren't ready. And because I didn't want you to stop."</p>

<p>The room was very quiet. The dormitory breathed around them, floorboards settling, pipes humming, the refrigerator in the kitchen producing its off-key note that clashed with everything. Outside the window, the streetlight held steady, and somewhere in the building a door opened and closed, someone making a trip to the bathroom, and the normality of that sound, the mundane, unremarkable reality of other people living their lives on the far side of a wall, made the moment inside the room feel even more private, even more sheltered, a small warm space carved out of the larger, colder world by two girls and a cup of milk and a sketchbook full of sleeping faces.</p>

<p>Kamila's fingers, no longer cold, turned inside Anna's grip and interlaced with hers, and the pencil fell from her other hand onto the blanket, and neither of them picked it up, because the pencil had done its work and the work had been received and what came next did not require graphite or pigment or any medium at all except proximity and breath and the courage to remain in a room with someone who has seen the inside of your sketchbook and has not looked away.</p>

<p>They did not kiss. Not tonight. Tonight was not about the destination but about the door opening, the threshold crossed, the first step from architecture into inhabitation. Kamila leaned her head against Anna's shoulder, and Anna let her, and the weight of it, light and warm and real, settled against her collarbone the way a note settles into a chord, completing something that had been waiting for exactly this frequency to become whole.</p>

<p>"Stay," Anna said.</p>

<p>"Okay," Kamila said.</p>

<p>And they sat like that, hand in hand, on a bed in a dormitory at Khrustalny, while the milk cooled and the streetlight held and the sketchbook lay open between them like a bridge between two countries that had always shared a border but had only just decided to stop posting guards at it.</p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>The next morning, Kamila left before dawn, the way she always did, except that this time she left with the knowledge that the door she had been opening in secret was, and had always been, unlocked from the inside.</p>

<p>That evening, Anna found a fresh cup of warm milk on her bedside table. Two spoonfuls of honey. A wrapped biscuit. And beside them, the sketchbook, open to a new page, blank, with a short pencil resting in the spine.</p>

<p>She picked up the pencil and held it for a moment, feeling the warmth it still carried from the hand that had placed it there. Then she set it down, pulled the blanket up to her chin, and closed her eyes.</p>

<p>When the footsteps came, they were not tentative. They crossed the room with the quiet confidence of someone who no longer needed to pretend she was invisible, and when the bed dipped under a new weight, and when a body settled beside hers with a sigh that sounded like a person finally setting down something heavy, Anna did not open her eyes.</p>

<p>She simply smiled, and reached out, and found a hand in the dark that was already reaching back.</p>

<p>The ceramic cup sat on the bedside table, chipped and faded and permanent, holding warmth the way it had been made to hold warmth, patiently and without complaint, night after night, for as long as someone kept choosing to fill it.</p>
