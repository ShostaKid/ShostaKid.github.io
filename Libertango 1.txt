<p>Every morning waking up in Recoleta these days has been a joyful one, especially since she moved in to live with Aleph. Seeing him every morning, feeling his warmth as she hugged him close, watching him work under the fresh sunlight of a new day, and even when he carefully read every draft of hers, his gloved hand gently turning each page — all of it made Recoleta feel happier than ever before, especially when compared to her previously dull and monotonous life.</p>

<p>But this morning was different, and it felt off right from the very first moment. When Recoleta first cracked her eyes open at dawn, when the early sunlight had only just begun to pour golden light throughout the room, Aleph was already gone. His warmth still lingered on the sheets, his scent still clung to the folds of her clothes, but that tall figure with the fiery red hair was nowhere to be seen beside the female author as usual. The fiction-writer shifted slightly, burying her face into his still-warm pillow, letting it envelop her as she imagined Aleph was still there, holding her. He hadn’t said anything last night about leaving this morning, so this sudden disappearance made the young novelist feel a little uncomfortable inside. Still, she understood that he was a very busy man, and the fact that he spent so much time with her was already a huge sacrifice on his part — so she tried not to dwell on it too much. Drowsily, she sat up, slipped out from under the warm blanket, and pulled on Aleph’s oversized shirt that had been hanging at the head of the bed. After a moment’s thought, she decided to skip putting on pants from the clothes rack. If he wasn’t home, then probably no one else was in the house either, so the author felt lazier than usual.<br />
“Maybe he’s busy at the prison,” Recoleta mused as she stepped into the hallway, the morning chill slipping under the hem of the shirt and brushing against her bare lower half. “Guess I’ll prepare something nice for him tonight.”</p>

<p>But the moment she stepped into the kitchen, what Recoleta saw made it feel like someone had sucked all the air out of her lungs.</p>

<p>Standing right in front of her was a tall man, dressed entirely in stark white from head to toe, with that familiar blood-red long hair. He stood there, coffee cup in hand with faint steam still rising, looking at the female novelist as though he had been waiting for her all morning.</p>

<p>“Good morning, Recoleta,” he said calmly, taking a slow sip of coffee. “You’re up quite late.”</p>

The fiction-writer let out an incomprehensible scream before bolting into the nearby bathroom, hiding her lower half while only poking her head out.<br />
“What the hell are you doing here, Merlin?!”</p>

<p>“Why hide?” Merlin replied in that infuriatingly calm tone. “I’ve already seen everything anyway.”</p>

<p>“That’s not the point!” Recoleta shouted, feeling the blood rush hotter and hotter to her cheeks until they burned like two steamed mochi buns. “Why are you here this morning?! And where’s Aleph?!”</p>

<p>“Aleph had to go to the Bureau urgently this morning — something sudden came up with the market, so he had to meet them. He called me over to keep an eye on you today.”</p>

<p>“He didn’t say anything to me…”</p>

<p>“Well, now you understand what ‘sudden’ means,” Merlin replied, his voice now completely flat and emotionless — exactly like Aleph used to sound. He turned back to the pan, tending to the two pieces of meat that were halfway fried. “Now go get changed — if you want — and come down for breakfast before it gets too late. Don’t worry. I have zero interest in Aleph’s ‘little toy’.”</p>

<p>Merlin’s words painted Recoleta’s face bright scarlet, even her ears turning pink. He wasn’t entirely wrong — in truth, Merlin hadn’t done anything to her, and she had met him enough times to know he wasn’t that kind of person. Yet just minutes ago, she had reacted like an idiot, projecting the worst intentions onto him and probably insulting and hurting him in the process. Overwhelmed with shame that wrapped around her like a heavy coat, Recoleta fled back to her room without daring to look at Merlin even once.</p>

<p>She had no idea that Merlin had seen the entire scene.</p>

<p>***</p>

<p>The fiction-writer chewed on her pen absentmindedly, her mind drifting among the clouds. She had a manuscript deadline for the publisher next week, and as of today, she still hadn’t made any real progress. But right now, she couldn’t focus at all. The events of this morning still swirled in Recoleta’s consciousness, refusing to let her go. From that mortifying first encounter until now, neither of them had spoken another word to each other. When she finally came down to the kitchen later, she found only a neatly sliced piece of toast and perfectly cooked pieces of meat arranged on a porcelain plate. After breakfast, she saw Merlin enter the living room with her, but instead of sitting at the dining table in the center, he leaned against the window frame, letting the golden Buenos Aires sunlight spill across the pages of the book in his hands. Apparently he found those pages far more interesting than Recoleta’s face, because he never once looked up at her.</p>

<p>To be fair, Recoleta didn’t hate Merlin. But the idea of spending an entire day alone with him without Aleph around wasn’t exactly thrilling either. If there was one word that described Merlin perfectly, it was “mad” — and not in the same way Aleph was “mad.” No, Merlin’s madness was the madness of someone obsessed with a single truth, someone who would do anything to reach it and worshipped it like a personal religion. Aleph was like that too — they were both seekers of “Transcendence” — but Recoleta could always feel a certain gentleness in Aleph, a tenderness that made her like him from the very first letters. With Merlin, it felt like there wasn’t a trace of gentleness in anything he did. He didn’t hate her, didn’t despise her, didn’t particularly like her either. He simply acted according to the ideals he had set for himself, and if she wasn’t part of the path to his ultimate truth, he simply didn’t care about her. Maybe because he was a doctor, he always prioritized ruthless, rational choices?</p>

<p>“Hey, Merlin—” Gathering every ounce of courage she had, Recoleta finally spoke. He might not care about her, but she wasn’t the type to just brush off her own mistakes. “About this morning… I just wanted to say… um… I’m sorry for overreacting like that. I know you didn’t mean anything by it, but my bad temper made me act stupid. So if you felt hurt, I want you to know I didn’t mean it that way, and I’m really sorry.”</p>

<p>Merlin stared at her in silence for a long time — so long that Recoleta started to feel itchy and uncomfortable under his gaze. The fresh morning light of Buenos Aires poured through the clear glass, setting the blazing red of Merlin’s hair on fire. Only after a while, when Recoleta began to lose heart and was about to lower her head to return to work, ignoring the red-haired man at the window, did he finally move his lips.</p>

<p>“A book is not merely a structure of words; it is a dialogue established with the reader.” Merlin spoke slowly, standing up straight, eyes still fixed on the female novelist. “Do you know this quote, authoress?”</p>

<p>“Nota sobre Bernard Shaw, published in 1952 by Jorge Luis Borges,” Recoleta answered calmly, though a little surprised by the sudden question. “Borges wanted each of his works to become a living entity, something that could connect readers to literature — and to the author himself.”</p>

<p>“But that’s ridiculous, isn’t it? How can you consider a work a ‘living entity’? And how could anyone possibly see the author through the work?”</p>

<p>“Oh? And why not?” For a moment, Recoleta felt irritation rising inside her.</p>

<p>Merlin walked slowly toward the table where the fiction-writer sat, gently placed the book down, and sat facing her. Recoleta noticed the book he had been reading earlier was Buenos Aires Tango — one of her own works.</p>

<p>“More than one person, doubtless like me, writes in order to have no face,” she must have heard this line from Foucault, right? Literature is not something into which the author breathes a soul. No, Miss Recoleta, the author has no function in a story except to rearrange the discourses within it. What you think reflects the author’s soul is merely the re-establishment of a system where language reigns supreme, and the author is nothing more than someone tasked with restructuring phrases to form a work.</p>

<p>“You’re being absurd here, Merlin. System? Discourse? Those words might suit your works, but not mine. Romanticism isn’t about writing just to rearrange what already exists — it exists so that authors can create something new, pour their own shadows and beliefs into it. ‘Writing is nothing more than a guided dream,’ and dreams, doctor, aren’t they precisely the mirror of the subconscious?”</p>

<p>“The dialogue with the reader through works is not a dialogue between the author and those readers — it is the universality of a discourse system formed within society. It reflects nothing of the author, no matter how hard you try to make it so. ‘Writing today has freed itself from the theme of expression. It only refers to itself… unfolding as a game that always exceeds its rules and violates its own limits.’ Perhaps it’s precisely because you’ve always misunderstood the meaning of writing that you can never find your own ending through your art?”</p>

<p>“And I think you understand absolutely nothing about my literature.” For the first time, a feeling of “hatred” began to form in Recoleta’s subconscious as she spoke to the man before her. The knuckles of her clenched fists turned white. “If you want to chase your own truth, I won’t stop you. But that doesn’t mean you get to impose your foolish ideals on everything.”</p>

<p>“I’m not imposing anything, Recoleta. I’m merely pointing out the absurdity in your thinking, and the reason you keep hopelessly searching for what you call your ‘ending.’ Perhaps…”</p>

<p>Before the doctor could finish his sentence, the brown-haired authoress stood up abruptly, clutched her manuscript bundle, and marched straight toward her room. She moved so quickly she didn’t even glance back at Merlin once.</p>

<p>***</p>

<p>The plate made a sharp “clack” as Merlin — not particularly gently — approached and placed the dish of food on Recoleta’s desk. The fiction-writer pretended not to notice the person right beside her, still bent over her writing, fresh ink slowly drying on the paper.</p>

<p>“You haven’t had lunch,” Merlin said, as emotionless as ever. “It’s already past lunchtime.”</p>

<p>No response.</p>

<p>“It’s been almost two hours past lunchtime. Unless you want to keep harming yourself.”</p>

<p>Still nothing.</p>

<p>“Aleph asked me to come here today to babysit. So if you keep acting like a child, don’t blame me.”</p>

<p>This time, the brown-haired novelist finally spoke. Her voice sounded a little hoarse.</p>

<p>“I’m not hungry.”</p>

<p>“You’re not hungry — you’re starving,” Merlin nodded in confirmation. “Be a good girl this once.”</p>

<p>“You don’t understand anything.”</p>

<p>“I understand. Very well, actually. But I’m a doctor, remember? So I prioritize physical matters before I concern myself with mental ones.”</p>

<p>Recoleta’s shoulders trembled slightly — perhaps for a moment she reconsidered. But in the end, the sheepskin on the desk still attracted her more than Merlin’s face. Seeing this, the doctor only let out a soft sigh.</p>

<p>“Your books… they’re very good. I’ve read several of them. I think I understand what you write there — even your worries and contemplations. I only wanted to help you, fiction-writer, the way that red-haired man does. But it seems I misunderstood your intentions…”</p>

<p>“Art, for me, is not simply a collection of discourses, Merlin,” Recoleta replied after a long pause, her voice choked. “I know I’m not a complete person. That’s why, for me… literature is the only true portrait I can leave behind in this world. I have no reflection in an ordinary mirror. People around me only remember me for a short time. Only literature, only my words, can exist through the years, imprinting even just a faint image of me into the minds of readers. That is the only mirror still capable of reflecting me — and I will keep painting myself until this very life finds its own ending.”</p>

<p>Silence enveloped the space for a long while, until Recoleta felt something rough gently wiping away the tear sliding down her cheek. It was an imperfect finger, covered in scratches — but a real finger, not the fabric of a glove.</p>

<p>“‘What does it matter who is speaking?’ I still stand by my view on discourse and works. But—” For the first time that day, Merlin’s voice unexpectedly softened, like a wave gently lapping the shore — “Literature is a form of self-discovery. I will try to understand that saying of Borges. And try to understand you better too, Recoleta.”</p>

<p>“You’re really detestable, you know that?” This time the fiction-writer finally looked at Merlin. Her eyes were red and puffy, but a small smile curved her lips.</p>

<p>“I don’t deny it. The prisoners and patients who come to see me say that a lot. Now eat, before you turn into a starving ghost and Aleph tears me apart when he gets back.”</p>

<p>After lunch, Recoleta went to the living room to continue writing, and this time the man in white sat beside her. His fingers occasionally glided lightly over the pages, touching each word with an almost reverent attitude. Every now and then, the fiction-writer stole glances at the red-haired man, and whenever she saw that expression, she couldn’t help but smile to herself. The mad shell around him had completely shattered in her mind. Now, before the novelist’s eyes, there was only a likable man willing to listen and help her.<br />
“Maybe no matter who it is… Aleph will always just be Aleph, right?”</p>

<p>But something felt… off. The longer she wrote, the sleepier Recoleta became. A wave of exhaustion slowly took over, numbing her muscles and senses. She tried to fight it, but everything began slipping beyond her control.<br />
“What the hell… what’s happening to me?”</p>

<p>“Recoleta?” A sharp call. The novelist tried to lift her head to look, but failed. Her mind spun, her whole body burning as if she had a fever, and the image of the man before her blurred in her brown eyes.<br />
“Are you feeling unwell?”</p>

<p>“I… I’m fine…” For some reason she couldn’t even understand herself, the fiction-writer decided to lie to the doctor. She staggered, trying to stand up from the chair. “Sorry, but I need to go to my room for a bit…”</p>

<p>A firm pull — strong enough to keep her from standing, yet gentle enough not to hurt — brought her back down onto the wooden chair. In her daze, the authoress still felt a rough hand, moving slowly like a great python, sliding along her collarbone.</p>

<p>“Don’t worry. I’m a doctor, after all,” Merlin’s voice came soft as clouds, as though drifting from far away. “Let me examine you.”</p>

<p>“No… no…”</p>

<p>That hand finally found the thin white shirt and the loosely fastened button right above the soft, pale swell of her young chest. Slowly but skillfully, those large fingers began their work.</p>

<p>“You said your literature is a portrait of yourself? I doubted it — until I touched the words in your stories. Perhaps then I began to understand. But I must verify it… with my own hands. Forgive me, fiction-writer.”</p>

<p>“Al… Aleph…” The small woman whimpered, trying to call his name in her last moments of clarity.</p>

<p>“Wrong, my dear novelist. It’s Merlin.”</p>

<p>The button popped open — and with it, the last thread holding Recoleta’s consciousness snapped. She collapsed forward, falling into Merlin’s arms before sinking completely into darkness.</p>
