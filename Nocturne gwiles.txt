<p align="center"><em>Lento con gran espressione</em></p>

<p align="center">***</p>

<p>"You know you're kind of terrifying, right?"</p>

<p>Gwen looked up from the drumsticks she had been spinning through her fingers at the boy sitting cross-legged on the floor of his own bedroom, a sketchbook open on his knee. Gwen was quick; she could usually see a sentence coming three blocks away, which is what let her land the comeback before the other person finished the setup. But her radar never worked on Miles Morales, and it was not working now.</p>

<p>"You're going to have to narrow that down," she said, one eyebrow going up. "I'm terrifying in a lot of ways."</p>

<p>"Nah, I mean, okay, that came out wrong." Miles laughed, and did the thing he did, restarting the sentence from a different door. "Your drumming. That's what I mean. I've heard you play. It's like watching somebody get into a fistfight with time itself. And win. How do you even sit still long enough to listen to this?"</p>

<p>He tilted his head at the record player in the corner, a hand-me-down from his father, where something slow was turning, all brushes and warm bass and a horn that sounded like it had nowhere in particular to be. It had been playing the whole time he sketched. Gwen had been aware of it the way you are aware of weather.</p>

<p>"I wasn't listening. I was tolerating."</p>

<p>"See, that. That's the terrifying part."</p>

<p>"Music is supposed to hit you." Gwen sat up on the edge of his bed, sticks stilled. "That's the whole point. It hits, you hit back, everybody's honest. This stuff doesn't hit. It just kind of... stands there. Where's the payoff? Where's the anything?"</p>

<p>Miles didn't answer right away. He looked at her the way he looked at a wall before he touched it with paint, which was a look Gwen had catalogued months ago and filed under things she was not going to think about, and then he closed the sketchbook and got up.</p>

<p>"Okay. Get up."</p>

<p>"What?"</p>

<p>"You want to know where the payoff is. I could explain it, but you'd roast me the whole time and we'd get nowhere. So." He held out his hand, palm up, and there was silver paint dried along one knuckle, and he was not quite meeting her eyes, which meant he was being brave, because Miles being brave always looked exactly like Miles being embarrassed and doing it anyway. "Dance with me. One song."</p>

<p>For about a second and a half, a small war broke out inside Gwen Stacy.</p>

<p>On one side, she understood him. If he tried to lecture her about negative space and letting a melody breathe she would have folded her arms and picked him apart out of pure reflex, and they both knew it. Experience was the only argument she had ever actually lost to.</p>

<p>But on the other side, Gwen was afraid. Not of Miles, never of Miles, how could anyone be afraid of a boy with silver paint on his knuckles and a laugh like that. The fear was older than him and bigger than the room. She was "afraid" of standing that close, "afraid" of what her hands might admit if they were not holding sticks, "afraid" of the fact that every version of her story she had ever heard of ended the same way, with somebody falling, and she had promised herself a long time ago that the safest number of people to hold on to was zero. She was "afraid" of all of it, and for a girl who could name every subdivision of a bar at any tempo, she still could not name the thing that went off-beat in her chest every time this particular boy said her name.</p>

<p><em>She'd only come over to make fun of his playlist. How did it turn into this?</em></p>

<p>But she stood up anyway, and crossed the room to the boy waiting in the middle of it, because she did not know what she was doing, so she let her body decide. And her body, her chest, her stupid off-beat heart, decided on him.</p>

<p><em>Let's just say it's because he looked dumb standing there with his hand out.</em></p>

<p>"Fair warning, I don't do this," she said, though her mouth had gone dry enough that she had to bite her lip first. "I play the music. I don't sway to it. Swaying is for people at weddings."</p>

<p>"Don't worry about steps. Just, like, listen. You count everything anyway, right? So count this one. It'll tell you what to do way better than I could." He said it easily, as if he had not noticed the tension coming off her like static, and turned the record up one notch. "Let it lead. That's the whole trick."</p>

<p>He faced her and took her hand, and Gwen registered, uselessly, that he had gotten taller since the first time they stood this close, close enough now that she was at eye level with the collar of his hoodie, close enough to smell spray paint and laundry and something warm underneath that was just him. But that was definitely not why her pulse had lost the tempo.</p>

<p>She put her hand in his. Drummer's calluses against paint-stained fingers. For one brief moment Gwen was fairly sure her heart rate reached a number her doctor would want to discuss.</p>

<p><em>One, two, three, four, five, six.</em></p>

<p>The song moved in six. She found the count without trying, the way she found every count, the kick sitting soft on the one, the brushes whispering across the rest of the bar like snow being swept off a stoop. Slow. Warm. Her shoulders, which had been up around her ears, came down a centimetre at a time. Miles felt it too, and loosened his grip, giving her fingers room to breathe, giving her room to breathe, and the bass repeated its round, patient figure like someone saying it's fine, it's fine, it's fine in a voice too low to argue with.</p>

<p>Gwen let out a slow breath.</p>

<p><em>One, two, three... six.</em></p>

<p>Then the horn came in, one long note held across the whole bar, the player leaning into it and letting it bloom, long enough to stretch the room. Long enough for Gwen to exhale. Long enough for her pulse to give up its own count and borrow the record's. Long enough for her feet to start moving without asking her first.</p>

<p>Not steps, exactly. A sway, a drift, forward when the horn rose and back when it settled, and Miles moved with her, not steering, not leading, just there, the way the bass was there under the horn, steady behind every change she made, a floor for each beat to land on. And Gwen, who had spent her whole life inside the hits, started to hear the other thing. The space between them. The rests. The song was not standing still at all. It was choosing, every bar, over and over, not to rush, and the choosing was the payoff, the choosing was the whole point, tension held so gently you could live inside it, and she thought, distantly, that somebody could have told her that years ago, except somebody just had, without saying a word.</p>

<p><em>One... two...</em></p>

<p>The horn paused, a fraction of a bar, and Gwen closed her eyes and breathed in. She saw Brooklyn stretched out beyond the window, the water towers and the bridge lights and a sliver of moon over all of it, and she saw, without wanting to, the version of this that she was always bracing for, the fall, the ending every story like hers was supposed to have. And here, in a small room that smelled like paint, with one of his father's records turning, none of it arrived. The bass stayed steady, light but sure, like the hand at her back. The count went on without her counting it.</p>

<p>She opened her eyes and realized her fingers were laced through his, holding on with just enough pressure to be warm. When had that happened. She stopped caring. She let her thumb trace the dried silver along his knuckle, the callus where the spray can sat, the small scar on the back of his hand that she was not going to ask about tonight.</p>

<p>It was not a perfect hand. But it was a real one.</p>

<p>Real enough that Gwen only wanted to hold it tighter, to get the shape of it by heart.</p>

<p>"You planned this," she muttered into his collar.</p>

<p>"You literally came over here to bully my playlist."</p>

<p>She did not answer that, because there was no answer that would not lose. Instead she half-closed her eyes and kept moving, following the record, following the off-beat thing in her chest that had finally, for once, settled into time. Forward, back, a slow quarter-turn as the horn changed direction. She pulled him closer, close enough to feel the bass through him instead of the floor, close enough to feel it keeping its patient count inside his chest, warm and even and completely unhurried.</p>

<p><em>One.</em></p>

<p>The horn drew its last long note and met the bass on the same low warmth, the two of them dissolving into each other as the needle ran into the quiet grooves at the end of the side, and the breath of the two teenagers met in the small space that was left, and stayed there.</p>

<p>And in the room where everything had been rendered all evening in cool blues, the blues going soft at the edges the way watercolor goes when it is asked to hold too much, the color changed. It started where her hand was folded into his and spread the way morning spreads, unhurried, sure of itself, a warm rose-gold washing over the ink lines and the halftone shadows and refusing, gently, to stay inside any of them.</p>
